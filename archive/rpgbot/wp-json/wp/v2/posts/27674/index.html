                            Species: Some New Faces, Some Face Lifts<\/h2>\n\n\n

                            The Aasimar, Goliath, Orc have all made their way into the core rules. The Aasimar makes sense alongside the Tiefling. The Goliath has been a community favorite since at least 3.5. Bringing the Orc into the Player\u2019s Handbook makes sense after the removal of the Half-Elf and the Half-Orc. I\u2019m excited to see the core roster of species continue to expand beyond the Tolkien-esque line-up of previous editions.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            As we saw in the Unearthed Arcana playtest, all of the species have gotten updated mechanics. Many 2014 core species fell out of favor as newer, more powerful options were published, so I\u2019m excited to see what WotC does with these iconic options.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            The Aasimar\u2019s transformation has been updated to allow you to choose which transformation you use every time that you use it, which is a huge upgrade. Based on the Unearthed Arcana version, the Dragonborn has also gotten some major upgrades.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            We don\u2019t know how subraces will be handled yet. Post-Tasha\u2019s materials published new subraces like the Astral Elf as standalone race entries, so it\u2019s reasonable to assume that subraces like the High Elf and the Wood Elf will have independent traits rather than being added onto the base Elf traits. But until we know more, we can only speculate.<\/p>\n\n\n

                            Legacy Species<\/h3>\n\n\n

                            Species from older books are usable with the new rules, but species which are included in the new PHB (dwarf, goliath, etc.) are intended to replace the older versions. If your species hasn\u2019t been updated, the PHB contains a sidebar explaining how to handle those species.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            Based on the custom origin changes introduced in Tasha\u2019s Cauldron of Everything and the knowledge that ability score adjustments are now part of backgrounds, we can make some educated guesses. Ability score improvements will be ignored, and you\u2019ll likely be locked into any proficiencies provided by your race.<\/p>\n\n\n

                            Backgrounds: The New Heavy Hitter<\/h2>\n\n\n

                            Backgrounds introduced in the years following Tasha\u2019s Cauldron of Everything began to grant players feats at level 1. This sought to address the fact that almost all backgrounds provided a feature which amounted to the ability to couch surf.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            In the new rules, backgrounds all provide an \u201cOrigin Feat\u201d. From what was said in the videos, some feats are only available from one background, while others like Alert and Skilled are available from multiple backgrounds.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            In addition to the feat changes, backgrounds now also determine adjustments to your character\u2019s starting ability scores. Each background specifies three ability scores, and you can choose +2 to one and +1 to another or +1 to all three.<\/p>\n\n\n

                            Backgrounds? In my Character Optimization? Preposterous!<\/h3>\n\n\n

                            The changes here make backgrounds a massively important decision point when building a character. Just as races\/species have historically been locked into certain class choices, backgrounds will now be locked to specific classes.<\/p>\n\n\n

                            Legacy Backgrounds: What Do We Know?<\/h3>\n\n\n

                            We don\u2019t yet know how backgrounds from the 2014 rules will be handled. Adventures like Dragonlance and Strixhaven allowed players to choose backgrounds which didn\u2019t grant feats, then let players choose feats from a tailored list. Eventually this list was pared down to Skilled or Tough, so it wasn\u2019t a great list, but we also didn\u2019t have a pre-made list of Origin Feats.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            We don\u2019t know anything for certain, but we\u2019ll likely get guidance allowing players to pick an appropriate origin feat and to work with their DM to pick three appropriate ability scores. The idea of customizing backgrounds hasn\u2019t been discussed, so I\u2019m not certain if that will remain an option.<\/p>\n\n\n

                            Feats: Origin or Otherwise<\/h2>\n\n\n

                            We\u2019re getting a new group of feats called \u201corigin feats\u201d which are available at 1st level from your background. You can still choose Origin Feats whenever you would get another feat. Origin Feats are just a subset of the overall selection of feats which are available at level 1.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            Characters can now select an Epic Boon feat when they reach level 19 in a class. These feats include an ability score increase which can go past the maximum of 20, and according to Jeremy Crawford they go beyond what feats can normally do.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            Boon of Combat Prowess: Once per turn when you miss, you can decide to hit.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            One unnamed boon gives you Truesight, while another lets you teleport when you take the Attack action or Magic action.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            As in the 2014 rules, players who sit at level 20 long enough can accumulate enough experience to get more Epic Boons, potentially raising their ability scores to 30.<\/p>\n\n\n

                                                                Ranger: Oh Look, Controversy<\/h2>\n\n\n

                                                                Rangers have struggled throughout 5th edition. The 2014 Player\u2019s Handbook version of the class was riddled with issues and weak subclass options. Many of the class\u2019s features were heavily situational, so big parts of your class never mattered in most campaigns. It was consistently among the lowest-rated classes, occasionally trading places with the Monk.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                Over the past decade we\u2019ve seen a fully revised ranger in Unearthed Arcana, then a pile of replacement features in Tasha\u2019s Cauldron of Everything, then finally several rounds of revisions in the 2024 playtest process, all seeking to find a very narrow sweet spot for the Ranger. The post-Tasha\u2019s Ranger was a major improvement, especially with the powerful new subclass options, but the core of the class still had a lot of rough edges.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                The online response to the 2024 reveal video for the Ranger has been largely negative. In Crawford\u2019s words, \u201cThe Ranger, more than any other class in the new Player\u2019s Handbook, is a new class.\u201d That oversells the changes, but the changes are still significant. All of the Tasha\u2019s optional features are now permanent, and several of them were improved further. Spellcasting moving to level 1 makes level 1 much more satisfying, and Weapon Mastery adds some great tactical options, especially for melee builds where two-weapon fighting was previously both expected and also very difficult.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                The biggest worry I see is over-reliance on Hunter\u2019s Mark as a central mechanic of the class. The 2014 Ranger depended on it as a crucial damage boost to the point that you often couldn\u2019t use other spells in combat. Tasha\u2019s shifted that a bit by adding Elemental Weapon and Magic Weapon to your spell list, but it just meant that now you have a different spell monopolizing your Concentration to the exclusion of any other options.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                The 2024 Ranger apparently goes back to over-reliance on Hunter\u2019s Mark. Getting some free castings each day helps a bit, but it doesn\u2019t solve the Concentration issue. High-level features and at least one subclass feature all tie into Hunter\u2019s Mark, further locking you into the spell. Even the Ranger\u2019s capstone ability is now a modest buff to Hunter\u2019s Mark, which is probably better than the 2014 Ranger\u2019s capstone, but the bar is on the floor.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                I think it\u2019s likely that the updated Ranger is much more satisfying to play than the 2014 version in several ways. I\u2019m frustrated by the choice to lean even harder into Hunter\u2019s Mark since we\u2019ve known the issues with Hunter\u2019s Mark since 2014. But until we get the 2024 rules, we can\u2019t know for certain.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                  \n
                                                                • 1. Spellcasting at level 1. They want people who are new to the class to immediately acclimate to the Ranger\u2019s martial\/caster hybrid nature. Can change one of their prepared spells on a Long Rest; previously they could only retrain one on gaining a Ranger level.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 1. Favored Enemy. Reworked again. Hunter\u2019s Mark prepared for free, and a few free castings per day. Hunter\u2019s Mark is central to the Ranger\u2019s capabilities and concept, and they wanted the resource cost to be easier, especially at low levels.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 1. Weapon Mastery!<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 2. Deft Explorer. Updated from the version introduced in Tasha\u2019s. Expertise in one skill and 2 languages.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 2. Fighting Style. No more restricted, curated list. You can also take Druidic Warrior instead of a Fighting Style.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 6. Roving. +10 ft. speed in non-heavy armor, climb speed, and swim speed.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • Expertise in 2 more skills. Unclear when in the level progression.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 10. Tireless. Updated from the version introduced in Tasha\u2019s. Give yourself temporary hit points a few times per day, and whenever you finish a Short Rest you remove a level of Exhaustion.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 13. Hunter\u2019s Mark improves. Damage can\u2019t break your Concentration.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 14. Nature\u2019s Veil. Turn invisible. Appears to be identical to the version introduced in Tasha\u2019s.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 17. Hunter\u2019s Mark improves again. Constant Advantage on attacks against your target.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                                                                • 18. Blindsight 30 ft.<\/li>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Weapon Mastery: The Shiny New Toy<\/h2>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            For classes with the Weapon Mastery feature, Weapon Mastery options now add a rider effect when you hit with a weapon. This adds some much-needed tactical versatility to martial characters.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Very easy to miss among all of the other information: Todd Kenreck and Jeremy Crawford clarified that while the Nick mastery allows you to take the additional attack from two-weapon during your Attack action, it doesn\u2019t prevent you from making another attack using your Bonus Action. This means that a level 1 character wielding a weapon with Nick, such as a dagger, could attack three times in a turn<\/a>. This is surprising, and I\u2019m curious to see if they misspoke.<\/p>\n\n\n\n


                                                                                                                                            Rangers get mastery in two weapons at a time. Fighters get more, and their number increases with level. Rogues get Weapon Mastery based on a discussion of the Vex mastery.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Crawford specifically discussed using two-weapon fighting to perform combos using weapons with different masteries, such as using Vex to gain Advantage on the attack with your second weapon.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            We saw two rounds of Unearthed Arcana documents with versions of the weapons table, including Weapon Mastery. Based on the discussion in the video, it sounds like the masteries work as they did in those documents. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Jeremy Crawford ended the video on Weapon Mastery by discussing the fact that Weapon Mastery can apply on every attack. Because it can apply so often, they needed to keep the complexity low to avoid bogging down the game. For comparison, Tales of the Valiant\u2019s \u201cWeapon Options\u201d system requires you to use the Weapon Option instead of dealing damage, so Weapon Options are more of a precision instrument than a button that mash every turn.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            The complexity of Weapon Mastery slowing down combat was one of my biggest concerns when Weapon Mastery was introduced, but I\u2019ll withhold judgment until I get more time playing with Weapon Mastery. It\u2019s almost certainly worth the added complexity to make martial characters more exciting, but it\u2019s not going to address the long-lived criticism that D&D combat takes too long.<\/p>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Surprise Rules: No Longer a Win Condition<\/h2>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Changed to no longer prevent combatants from acting for a turn, unbalancing the game in some circumstances by trivializing encounters when players surprised their enemies. Instead, surprised creatures now have disadvantage on initiative rolls.<\/p>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Changes to Spell Lists<\/h2>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Spell lists are now in the class\u2019s description, rather than being in the Spells chapter. These tables also detail the spell\u2019s school, its components, and whether or not it requires Concentration, making these tables a useful reference table rather than simply a bulleted list.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Crawford also hinted at new spells on the spell lists. We saw a couple new options in the Unearthed Arcana playtest documents, but we don\u2019t know what has made it into the final version of the book.<\/p>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Changes to Spells<\/h2>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            According to Crawford, the 2024 Player\u2019s Handbook includes at least 30 spells not in the 2014 PHB, and every spell from the 2014 PHB has been revisited. Answers to common questions about spells have often been included in the spells\u2019 updated descriptions, so they\u2019re easier to understand without actually changing. Some spells have had their wording shortened to simplify them without changing their effects.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            The Spells\u2019 descriptions now also list what classes have that spell on their list so that you don\u2019t need to flip around as much. The Ritual tag has been moved within spell descriptions to make it easier to find. In general, it sounds like the formatting got a nice quality of life improvement.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Some high-level damage spells had their damage increased so that they\u2019re more satisfying to use. Flame Strike was listed as an example. High-level spells were also updated to make their effects clearer in many cases.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Healing spells have been upgraded to make them feel more satisfying to cast, but we didn\u2019t get details on how. Casting Cure Wounds only to have an enemy stab your target for the same amount of damage has felt awful for as long as DnD has had magical healing, which is why so few people are willing to cast anything except Healing Word during combat.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            The wording of many spells has been standardized to make their effects easier to understand. The new \u201cemanation\u201d AOE will cover a lot of spells like Word of Radiance and Spirit Guardians.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Shapeshifting spells like Polymorph have been updated so that they no longer grant a secondary pool of hp. Instead, they give you temporary hit points. We\u2019ve seen the same change with Wild Shape in the Unearthed Arcana documents. I\u2019m excited to see how this will affect spellcasters like the Wizard who don\u2019t have as much hp as the Druid to fall back on.<\/p>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Summoning Spells<\/h3>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            The excellent summoning spells in Tasha\u2019s were brought into the PHB, and some of them got new options.                                                                                                                                             Conjure X Spells<\/h3>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            The 2014 versions required hunting for stat blocks, added a bunch of creatures to the battlefield, bogged down play, and made DMs sad. They\u2019ve all been redesigned.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                            Instead, these spells now summon spirits that help you in some way. Crawford said that the response to those versions in Unearthed Arcana was positive, so I imagine that there were very few changes. I remember those spells all being worse versions of Spirit Guardians, so I hope that they at least feel more distinct now.<\/p>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              What it Means (Especially for Character Optimization)<\/h2>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              Building characters has a fun new lever in the updated Backgrounds. Since ability scores and feats are now attached to backgrounds, they\u2019ll fill a mechanical niche that historically has been filled by races through most of DnD\u2019s history. Creating new backgrounds will also require much less mechanical consideration than designing new races, so I\u2019m hoping that we\u2019ll see an abundance of options both in the Player\u2019s Handbook and in future supplements. I\u2019m sure homebrewers and 3rd-party creators will find fertile ground here.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              That said, losing the Tasha\u2019s-era flexibility in Ability Score Increases means that we\u2019re more constrained in our build choices under the 2024 rules. Constraints like this impose interesting cost-benefit comparisons which I really enjoy when building characters. Do I take the obvious background? Or do I pick one that has worse ability scores so that I can get a feat that\u2019s really good for my build?<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              The changes to races\/species which we\u2019ve seen seem to follow what we say in the playtest. We still don\u2019t have precise text, but I expect that weaker species will be brought in line with the more powerful species in the Player\u2019s Handbook. We know that the Goliath is getting some new options and the Dragonborn is getting an upgrade, but otherwise we don\u2019t have answers yet.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              Species will remain an important decision point in optimizing character, but unusual species\/class pairings may be more viable than they were in the 2014 rules.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              Changes to classes seem very close to the Unearthed Arcana documents so far. We saw a lot of changes throughout the 2 year UA process, so it will be interesting to see what they kept. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              Weapon Mastery is going to be huge for martials. I think folks will quickly find ways to make Weapon Mastery a problem since it applies to every attack. For example: the Slow mastery imposing a speed penalty when combined with other options like Ray of Frost can reduce enemies\u2019 speed to the point that they functionally can\u2019t get into melee. The ability to lock melee-only enemies out of range this easily will necessitate changes to encounter design, and even then it remains to be seen what will remain viable to challenge the players.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              All that said, I dislike some of the changes. Converting Divine Smite into a spell is absolutely going to limit Paladin players in a way that\u2019s less fun than the 2014 version. I want to be able to cast Shield of Faith, then charge into battle and smite things on turn 1. That\u2019s not allowed now. Limiting Divine Smite to \u201conce one each of your turns\u201d would have solved the burst problem just as well with minimal change to the Paladin\u2019s gameplay. They did that with plenty of other features, so it\u2019s not clear to me why it didn\u2019t happen here.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              Changing grapple and shove to unarmed strikes does address the overpowered grapple\/shove combo<\/a> somewhat, but now you can grapple on Opportunity Attacks, thereby preventing enemies from moving away. Even characters with dismal Strength can capitalize on this because even strong creatures will sometimes roll poorly. They\u2019ve essentially just moved the problem from Athletics checks to unarmed strikes rather than solving how easy it was to be unstoppable at grappling.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                              I\u2019m also worried about the adherence to a minimum DC of 15 when hiding which we saw in the last several UA documents. That stability makes it nearly certain that the rule will make it into the PHB. This would mean that a level 1 character purpose-built for stealth has a 50% chance to successfully hide, or a 60% chance if they have Expertise in Stealth. This would make low-level rogues extremely difficult to play, not to mention the frustration of literally anyone else trying to hide.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
