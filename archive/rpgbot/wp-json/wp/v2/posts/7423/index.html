Recent subclasses introduced in Tasha’s Cauldron of Everything introduced\n\t\ta new mechanic: free spells known as part of your subclass. This adds 10\n\t\tadditional spells to the Sorcerer’s meager 15 spells known at 15th level.\n\t\tThis addition dramatically expands the versatility of those subclasses\n\t\tcompared to other subclasses, leaving room for the Sorcerer to learn more\n\t\tsituational spells rather than sticking to reliable staples selected by\n\t\tnearly every other sorcerer.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Opinions on this addition have been mixed:\n\t\twhile many applaud the expansion to the Sorcerer’s capabilities and the\n\t\tnew-found tolerance for non-optimal spell choices, other find that this was\n\t\tan unneeded addition which robs the Sorcerer of their unique spellcasting\n\t\tcapabilities (using a small set of spells and modifying them with Metamagic).<\/p>\n\n\n\n

As a compromise, I propose meeting somewhere in the middle. For Tasha’s subclasses (Aberrant Mind and Clockwork Soul), allow the player to select just one of the two free spells at each spell level. For other subclasses, I suggest that the DM should generate a list of 10 spells (two at each spell level from 1 to 5) which fit the theme of the spell list, and allow the player to learn one of those spells for free at each spell level. This doesn’t touch on the retraining mechanic enjoyed by the Aberrant Mind and the Clockwork Soul, but I think that’s fine. They did it first, let them keep something unique.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

But generating those spells lists is hard, and when you’re doing it for your own players it’s easy for players to feel cheated or to feel like you’re giving them intentionally poor options. Having the authority of a published book to blame is convenient because it feels impartial and it feels like whoever wrote it is authoritative enough that it feels more “fair”.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Well, I’m not a book, but I’m here and I’ve got this weird blog, so here we go:<\/p>\n\n\n
