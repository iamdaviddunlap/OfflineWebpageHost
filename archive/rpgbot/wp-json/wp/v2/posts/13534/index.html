Review in Summary<\/h2>\n\n\n

Published by Free League and lead designer Tomas H\u00e4renstam in 2021, Forbidden Lands is a fantasy RPG in which the players play adventurers carving out an existence in a cursed world full of danger of all sorts. Adventurers are very human, monsters are very monstrous, you still need to eat to live, and success is nowhere near guaranteed.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Forbidden Lands isn\u2019t quite as rules light (or as brutal) as M\u00f6rk Borg, but it\u2019s also not as crunchy or superheroic as DnD or pathfinder. It hits a nice middle point in complexity while still managing to elegantly handle simulationist mechanics around travel, survival, crafting, and maintaining a home base which quickly turn into a series of spreadsheets in many RPGs. The variety of character options makes characters mechanically diverse and gives players clear hooks into the story of the forbidden lands, and there\u2019s plenty of room for characters who enjoy the mechanical aspects of the game to explore without requiring less mechanically-inclined players to follow along.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Players are encouraged to make their mark on the world both figuratively and literally. The core rules come in a box set with a map, and players are intended to permanently mark the map as they play, creating a permanent and unique artifact for your group. Considering that one of the Ennies was for Best Cartography, a suitably well-used map might be a cherished piece of art for your gaming group worthy of decorating whatever space you play in.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Forbidden Lands took home 4 Ennies the year it was published, and I can absolutely see why. The game is cool, the rules are good, the books are beautiful, and reading the game got me excited to play.<\/p>\n\n\n

Raiders and Rogues in a Cursed World<\/h2>\n\n\n

The Forbidden Lands are a dangerous place, and at times the adventurers will face overpowering opposition.<\/p><\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

This isn\u2019t DnD. This isn’t One Ring. Forbidden Lands makes no assertions that you\u2019re somehow the heroes, and it doesn\u2019t box you into an alignment to try and describe your characters\u2019 philosophical outlook. The game is intentionally hard to survive. Combat is deadly. Travel is deadly. Starvation is entirely possible. You might have a rough day, catch lice, become Broken, and die cold and alone.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

But this also isn\u2019t M\u00f6rk Borg where characters are made of paper. You might go the distance. Raid enough dungeons, and you might live long enough to get rich and famous. You might build yourself a stronghold. Maybe it\u2019s just a modest house, or maybe you refurbish an old castle or even build one of your own. Maybe you have ambitions about carving out a kingdom for yourself. But be prepared to defend it all.<\/p>\n\n\n

Throwing the Bones<\/h2>\n\n\n

Sooner or later, you will end up in situations where the outcome is uncertain, no matter how skilled you are.<\/p><\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

Forbidden Lands uses a slightly modified version of the Year Zero Engine\u2019s dice pool system. Players will need 15d6 of three different colors for \u201cbase\u201d dice, skill dice, and gear dice, plus one each of d8, d10, and d12 as \u201cartifact dice\u201d. Rolling dice generates \u201csuccesses\u201d and potentially skull symbols on a 1 which only matter if you \u201cpush\u201d your roll.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Pushing your roll allows you to reroll any dice which doesn\u2019t have a 1 (a skull) or a success symbol (6 or higher on a d6, d8, d10, or d12). This gives you another shot at succeeding at the cost of possibly taking damage and\/or damaging equipment. This has the hilarious side-effect that you could make a skill check, push the check, take damage, and become \u201cBroken\u201d. This becomes extra funny when you do this while trying to heal an ally.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

You suffer 1 point of damage per skull on your base dice and your items degrade by 1 for each skull on the gear dice, but you also get one point of Willpower for each point of damage that you take. Willpower is a generic resource spent to activate cool stuff about your character like using talents and casting spells. The rules specifically discourage rolling dice frequently because it makes it too easy to generate Willpower. If you\u2019re a player, try to abide by this. I understand the temptation to intentionally hurt yourself then use Healing to rack up a bunch of free Willpower.<\/p>\n\n\n

Character Creation and Advancement<\/h2>\n\n\n

Talk it through and let the whole group discuss what has happened.<\/p><\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

Characters are built by choosing your Kin (race, ancestry, species, etc.), Profession (class), Pride (something your character is personally proud of about themselves), Dark Secret, Relationships to the other party members, Attributes (ability scores), Age, and Talents (feats). Attributes range from 2 to 6 and do not increase, so high starting attributes are extremely impactful. Older characters get fewer attributes but more starting skills and general talents, which is allows some interesting trades when building your character.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Advancement is point-based. Characters earn XP by answering questions at the end of a session (Did you play? Did your pride\/secret come up?) similar to other Year Zero Engine games. Players spend XP to buy skills and talents, and must spend some time training and succeed on a Wits roll to learn new talents, so characters who aren\u2019t very smart might need some help to learn things. Learning magic talents notably requires a teacher unless you spend a massively larger amount of experience, so spellcaster players either need to get really good at checking the XP boxes or they need to befriend other spellcasters within the game.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

While characters can advance quite a bit via skills and talents, your attributes don\u2019t increase after character creation. This presents a challenge because they also act as pools of hit points. Injuries commonly damage Strength or Agility, while mental effects drain Wits or Empathy. Reducing any to 0 makes you \u201cbroken\u201d, which isn\u2019t dead but you\u2019re also not going anywhere. This means that even with a mountain of experience, your sorcerer might have 2 Strength and die by being stabbed with a knife by a disgruntled farmer.<\/p>\n\n\n

Swords and Occasionally Sorcery<\/h2>\n\n\n

It is up to you as a player to describe exactly what your adventurer does, how they look doing it, and what they feel when they raise their sword to strike\u2026<\/p><\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

If you\u2019ve read or played ALIEN or One Ring, Vaesen, or other Year Zero Engine games, the combat rules will feel familiar. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

Forbidden Lands using the combat system largely common to Year Zero Engine games. Initiative is determined by drawing cards. Positioning uses zones and range bands. Characters have a small number of hit points before suffering critical injuries. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

Gear is very similar to One Ring, but there are a few more armor and weapon options. Weapons deal a fixed number of damage modified by your successes. Weapons deal 1 to 3 damage plus any successes rolled, which is terrifying when characters might have just 2 Strength (Strength is also your hit points). You can get up to 6 Strength if you build around doing so, but most characters will fall in the 2 to 4 range, so getting hit even once might mean a critical injury.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Turns include a fast action and a slow action, which can be used for things like attacking, parrying, casting spells, etc. Talents can give you more ways to use the action economy, such as making it a fast action instead of a slow action to attack with a knife or allowing you to parry once for free each round. Stretching the action economy is crucial in combat, and being able to Dodge and\/or Parry effectively is the difference between life and death.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Magic is mechanically simple, but very few characters can use it. There aren\u2019t a huge number of spells (additional supplements have introduced more), but there are enough to get the job done, and what\u2019s there will cover all your classic fantasy tropes like turning into animals, magical healing, raising the dead, and summoning creatures to fight for you. Magic is broken into \u201cdisciplines\u201d which include a handful of related spells like the Healing discipline and the Blood Magic discipline. Spells are cast by spending Willpower, and spending more makes them stronger similar to upcasting a spell in DnD.<\/p>\n\n\n

When every piece of food counts, you count every piece of food<\/h2>\n\n\n

It demands a delicate touch \u2013 the adventurers should have to struggle for the resources, but not so hard that the effort isn\u2019t worth the reward.<\/p><\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

Forbidden Lands certainly isn\u2019t unique among RPGs which seek to make things like combat and traps deadly, but it sets itself apart with the simulationist aspects of the game. Characters need to track consumables like good using a somewhat abstract resource system that strikes a wonderful balance between counting every arrow and simply ignoring consumables as a concept, two extremes which people frequently embrace in games like DnD.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Fresh food collected in the wilderness spoils if it\u2019s not cooked and preserved by a trained chef (either one of the players or someone running an inn). You need to properly make camp or risk the impact of poor sleep and the effects of cold weather. These dangers don\u2019t go away as your characters grow more powerful, though greater skill in Survival can mitigate them somewhat by making failure mathematically unlikely, but that will take time and cost a lot of experience.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Consumables are tracked using \u201csupply dice\u201d ranging from d6 to d12 rather than tracking specific units of food, etc. Rolling poorly lowers the die one step (or removes the die if you\u2019re at a d6), so a lucky character might limp along on a d6 worth of drinkable water for a few days, or an unlucky one might go from a d12 to dehydration in a few days. This makes consumables intentionally abstract but still meaningful. Carrying capacity is very strictly limited (mounts help), so players can\u2019t haul barrels of water and beef jerky around to make survival a non-factor.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

ALIEN uses the same supply dice mechanic, so if you\u2019ve already read ALIEN this should feel familiar.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

These rules become especially important while traveling. The rules for Journeys feel similar to One Ring, though where One Ring emphasizes more fantastic dangers like orc ambushes or being lost in unexplored lands, Forbidden Lands focuses more on how much it sucks to be outside. Swarms of mosquitoes, ill effects of cold weather, lack of sleep, and eating spoiled food are all things that can humble even the most powerful adventurer.<\/p>\n\n\n

Strongholds and Hirelings<\/h2>\n\n\n

As your Reputation scores rise, the risk increases that your stronghold will attract unwelcome visitors<\/p><\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

Experienced and wealthy characters may eventually acquire a stronghold. This permanent base provides several benefits including a safe place to sleep, train, and repair equipment. However, it also presents a persistent liability because an undefended stronghold is vulnerable to mishaps. Even a well-defended stronghold might also be subject to a siege (yes, there are siege rules, and they\u2019re awesome).<\/p>\n\n\n\n

To maintain and defend your stronghold, you\u2019ll need to hire and pay hirelings. These hirelings have a range of roles, each providing special benefits like growing food, crafting arrows, defending your stronghold, and tending one of the \u201cspecial functions\u201d which you can build into your stronghold, such as gardens, libraries, and shrines.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

In a way, getting far enough in the game to acquire a stronghold changes it from a low-powered dungeon fantasy game into a combination management simulator. The system is simple enough to not be a huge headache, and players are absolutely free to ignore if they prefer to remain murder-hobos.<\/p>\n\n\n
