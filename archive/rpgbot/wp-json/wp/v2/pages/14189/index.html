Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

Poisoners are a classic character trope within the fantasy genre at large: Cunning assassins who only need to drop a small vial into their enemies food to do them in, as well as those who coat their blades with malicious substances to get an edge on their foes. As DnD attempts to represent all fantasy tropes with as much fidelity as possible, poisoners are a classic character type within the game as well. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

However, in the conversion from prior systems to 5th edition, the focus of poison use was diminished greatly. The unfortunate outcome was that few players actively choose to use poison. Prior to Tasha\u2019s Cauldron of Everything, everything we knew about poisons was confined to about two pages in the DMG. Tasha\u2019s attempted to rectify that with the Poisoner feat with some mixed success and a few unforeseen results.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

The Poisoner feat offers players easy access to poison as a tactical option, allowing players to craft their own poisons using clearly defined rules that don\u2019t require hauling a box of creatures around or similar shenanigans. We\u2019ll dig into how it works and what you can do with Poisoner below.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

If you want to go an extra step and dig into poisoners beyond \u201cBasic Poison\u201d and the Poisoner feat, we\u2019ve also dug into where you can find other poisons either by purchasing or harvesting them, what their effects are, and what poisons are and aren\u2019t worth the effort.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        Poisoner<\/h2>\n\n\n\n
        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        The Poisoner feat is not included in the SRD, therefore we cannot replicate the text in any significant way. Instead, crack open Tasha\u2019s Cauldron of Everything (page 80) and we\u2019ll proceed through each bullet point individually. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Bullet Point 1:<\/strong> While the ability to ignore poison damage seems good at first glance, it does very little to solve the primary problem that poison damage users face. In the Monster Manual, only five monsters have any form of resistance to poison, while a whopping 95 of them have immunity to it. This aspect of the feat does not negate immunity, and therefore leaves a poison damage user utterly incapable of harming a great deal of foes, and only increases your effectiveness against a handful of monsters. NPCs such as dwarves will be affected, but those will make up a small portion of the creatures you\u2019ll face in a typical campaign.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        Bullet Point 2.<\/strong> Being able to apply poison to an attack per turn is an incredible boost in your combat effectiveness. As consumable items, you should expect that using this every turn will cost you monetarily. At the very least, the ability to apply a poison that loses its effectiveness in a minute to a weapon as a bonus action will reduce the burden on you in fights that were not ambushes you prepared. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Although you gain knowledge of a craftable poison with this feat, we suggest reading the Sample Poisons and (Possibly) Harvestable Poisons section of this guide to best understand all the other poisons you can apply with this bonus action. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        Bullet Point 3, Line 1.<\/strong> Being able to use a poisoner\u2019s kit is a nice boon, which is especially nice if you don\u2019t have proficiency in nature checks in addition to the poisoner\u2019s kit. While there aren\u2019t a lot of rules governing the crafting of poisons beyond DM fiat, there are hard rules for harvesting poison, which is a DC 20 nature or poisoner\u2019s kit check (DMG 258.) This would be nicer if it gave you expertise in the poisoner\u2019s kit if you already had proficiency, but alas, no such luck. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        Bullet Point 3, Line 2-4.<\/strong> You gain the ability to craft a fairly effective poison. Athough it acts like an injury poison, it\u2019s not a true injury poison as by RAW it can be applied to any weapon, including bludgeoning weapons. The DC is high enough that you can expect that even high level creatures will fail sometimes, though it won\u2019t be consistent since Constitution saves tend to be disproportionately high. The poisoned condition imposes disadvantage on all attack rolls and ability checks, which your party front-liners are going to love.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

        The price compared to other poisons is better than anything listed in the DMG, aside from perhaps harvesting poison from a captured creature. There are a few injury poisons, such as the serpent\u2019s venom, wyvern poison, and purple worm poison that deal half damage on a save, which, unfortunately, the poisoner feat poison does not do. If they save against the DC, that\u2019s a dose of poison expended for nothing, but you can try again next round (or until you run out of poison).<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        Selling Your Poison <\/h2>\n\n\n\n
        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        Perhaps the most \u201cbreakable\u201d ability in the Poisoner feat is its crafted poison\u2019s ability to be traded. A basic poison vial\u2019s listed market price is 100gp, and the poison you are creating with this feat is simply better than a basic poison vial on every level, aside from the basic poison\u2019s ability to be applied to 3 pieces of ammunition.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Running very conservative math, let’s assume the Poisoner feat poison\u2019s market value is 100gp, like the basic poison. Even if you have to sell the poison at half price so the reseller can mark it up to market value, you\u2019re selling a single dose of the poisoner feat poison for 50gp. As you can craft a number of doses equal to your proficiency bonus for 50 gold, a first level character with this feat can double their money provided they find a place to unload their inventory of poison. As your proficiency bonus increases, so does your profit margin as you craft more poison per batch. If you\u2019re able to place the Poisoner feat poison\u2019s market value higher than the basic poison, then you\u2019re turning an even greater profit. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        Unloading your inventory may be tricky, and it\u2019s certainly morally dubious to flood a market with poison. Obviously, you\u2019re going to have to unload your stock in civilized areas, so, in games where you\u2019re often traveling in the wilderness, this strategy may not be as effective. Additionally, within the DMG, poisons are stated to be broadly illegal in most societies, so likely you\u2019re going to be dealing primarily with unscrupulous criminals and merchants. It may be worth starting with the criminal background for a contact that you could reliably deal with. As a player, I\u2019d attempt to set up a situation in which the party Face could work out a deal to sell directly to a military.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

        As a DM, I\u2019d make sure this has consequences, such as having enemy NPCs start their opening salvos with weapons coated with the poison that you have created with your own hands. I personally think having players running an underground poison ring is interesting enough to let it play out, but exploiting one of the largest weak spots in D&D\u2019s design (its economy) isn\u2019t going to fly with every DM. DMs are well within their rights to make it so nobody will purchase your poison, scale up the cost of the raw materials to make poison, or throw player characters in jail, so be warned. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        <\/div>\n\n\n\n

        The Problems With Poison<\/h2>\n\n\n\n
        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        Poisons generally suffer from \u201cthe consumable item problem.\u201d Using poison consumes the dose of poison, and a dose of poison often has hefty price tag attached. Compounded with this, the poison may still be negated with a single successful saving throw. This makes it challenging for players to know when is the best time to deploy them effectively. Because of these factors, it\u2019s possible that a dose of poison will sit in a player\u2019s inventory for an entire campaign. This can be negated having a lot of poison on your person, as it’s easier to expend a resource if it doesn’t feel scarce. Either by harvesting or crafting poison with the Poisoner feat, players will have enough poison to feel free to use them every combat. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        Applying poisons to a weapon is an action, performed with the Use an Object action. This generally means that, to make effective use of your time, you should output a worse debuff or higher damage than two of your turns combined. In most cases, the best way to do this is to apply poison before you enter combat, giving you one use of poison per fight. The action use to apply poison can be turned into a bonus action use, making poison vastly more efficient. You can do this if you have the Poisoner feat or if you are a rogue with the Thief archetype (Fast Hands). <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        However, the single largest strike against poison use is that a high proportion of creatures are immune to poison damage and the poisoned condition outright. While it\u2019s not always clear to a player which creatures will be immune, a good rule of thumb is that constructs, elementals, fiends and undead will not be affected by poison. There is nothing that can be done to circumvent this issue. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        Poison Types<\/h2>\n\n\n\n
        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        There are four categories of poison, each with different strengths and weaknesses. Understanding what type of poison and when to apply and how to apply them will make you much more efficient in their use. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Contact<\/span>: Carefully slather it on a weapon, throw a glass vial of it, or grab a brush and paint it on a door handle. Unlike injury poisons, contact poisons can be applied to bludgeoning weapons. As long as a creature touches contact poison with their skin, they have to make the save. Unfortunately, there are very few sample contact poisons, and the sample poisons have low DCs. You trade ease of use for reduced reliability.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        Ingested<\/span>: The most difficult method of delivering poison to a victim. This requires you to have access to your foe\u2019s food or drink, which happens rarely without good planning. Many ingested poisons have effects that are strong enough to partially offset that drawback, but enemies may still save out of the effects which makes it a big gamble. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Inhaled<\/span>: Inhaled poisons are great because they generally don\u2019t require an attack roll, though a DM may ask for one if you throw a bottle or other container to release the poison. Inhaled poisons not requiring an attack roll is also something of a double-edged sword, as inhaled poisons can\u2019t be applied to weapons and take an action to use. However, the Thief Rogue can use an object as a bonus action, which improves its effectiveness in combat greatly. Note that a target holding their breath doesn\u2019t stave off the poison, and the 5-foot cloud of poison disperses immediately after use. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Injury<\/span>: For an injury poison to take effect, you have to hit with and deal damage with a piercing or slashing weapon. It\u2019s worse than contact as a delivery method, but it\u2019s reliable. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                Harvesting Poison<\/h2>\n\n\n\n
                <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                The DMG has rules for harvesting a poison, allowing you to extract poison from a dead or incapacitated poisonous creature. There are four creatures which the DMG gives as examples of their poison, but the DMG states that many poisonous creatures can be harvested this way. By RAW, the creature must be dead or incapacitated, but it stands to reason that a willing creature could allow their poison to be harvested as well. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                Harvesting a poison takes 1d6 minutes. This requires a DC 20 nature check or a poisoner\u2019s tools check. If you fail the check by more than 5, you expose yourself to the poison. With either the nature or poisoner\u2019s tools check, you\u2019re likely going to be using intelligence as the governing modifier, though there\u2019s a potential argument for wisdom or dexterity with the poisoner tools. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                This means prioritizing your intelligence if you foresee doing this regularly, and adding twice your proficiency to either check makes this a great deal more reliable. The Artificer makes for an excellent poison harvester, as well as rogues with a decent intelligence score, or any character with the skill expert feat. Guidance, bardic inspiration, and the Help action make this a lot more reliable at lower levels. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                Making Sense of Poison Harvesting<\/h2>\n\n\n\n
                <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                Unfortunately, the DMG is infuriatingly vague on what \u201ca poisonous creature\u201d precisely is, and doesn\u2019t distinguish between \u201cpoisonous\u201d (the one where eating it is bad) and \u201cvenomous\u201d (the one where it eating you is bad). The exact wording from the text is \u201ca character can instead attempt to harvest poison from a poisonous creature, such as a snake, wyvern or carrion crawler.\u201d We know from the sample poisons in the DMG that this also includes purple worms. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                By looking at the sample poisons and the provided examples of harvistable creatures, we can infer a few things. At a minimum, it can be inferred that the intent is to define this as a creature that deals poison damage or causes the poisoned condition with a feature of their anatomy. Of our four clear examples given in the rules for harvesting poisons. All of the sample poisons that can be harvested from a creature are a contact or injury poison. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                5th edition doesn\u2019t have a difference in classification between natural and weapon attacks, so we have to define exactly what can be harvested ourselves. My chosen interpretation is that any creature that deals poison damage or causes the poisoned condition with a beard (yes, really), bite, claw, fist, slam, spit poison, sting, tail, tentacle, or touch attack, or the poisonous skin feature can be harvested. Your DM\u2019s mileage may vary. Whether or not an inhaled poison can be extracted from a creature is neither explicitly allowed or forbidden, as there are no examples either way. See \u201cIs This a Harvestable Poison?\u201d, below.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Additionally, a great deal of creatures who deal poison damage do not actually require a constitution saving throw for the poison to take effect. An example of this is the Flying Snake, and we know from the provided examples that snakes are harvestable for poison. This presents a dilemma to DMs if they have players extracting poison, as allowing a player to use a poison with no save DC may be deemed overpowered. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                As a house rule to address this gap, you might require all extracted poisons to have a Constitution saving throw. Doing a bit of reverse engineering of the mechanics, the DC should be equal to 8 + the harvested creature\u2019s proficiency bonus + their Constitution modifier. If the extracted poison deals damage, have the poison deal half damage on a successful save. This is consistent with the DCs provided by nearly all creatures that deal poison damage or cause the Poisoned condition. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                DM\u2019s Note:<\/strong> A soft nerf you can do to make it more challenging to harvest poison is to make the corpses of outsiders (celestials, fiends) turn to dust, fade away or bubble into liquid. This doesn\u2019t prevent your players from harvesting poison, but now they have to get clever about it. If that\u2019s still not enough, a hard nerf you can do is to have all poisons extracted replicate the effects of one of the sample poisons. You might also have poisons not listed in the DMG expire after a brief period (24 hours seems reasonable) so that players can\u2019t stockpile them. This allows the poisons to be used briefly while also explaining why only shelf-stable poisons are available for purchase.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                (Possibly) Harvestable Poisons<\/h2>\n\n\n

                Doing our best to interpret the vague rules on harvesting poison, the following poisons are from the creatures that are likely harvestable by both RAI and centrist\u2019s reading of RAW, but your DM\u2019s mileage may vary. The rules regarding which poisons can and cannot be harvested will ultimately always be their choice. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                All poisons that deal flat damage or half damage on a save are rated green or above. They can be applied to your weapon before combat or during combat (with the Poisoner feat) and increase your damage output with almost no drawback. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                  \n
                • Basilisk\u2019s Bite<\/span>: Injury poison. Deals 2d6 damage without a save. Always useful. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                • Bearded Devil\u2019s Poison<\/span>: Injury poison, DC 12. Poisons the target and makes them unable to heal while poisoned. The effect ends after 1 minute, or until the creature makes a successful saving throw, which they can do at the end of each of their turns. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                • Bone Devil’s Sting<\/span>: Injury poison. Deals 5d6 damage without a save, which is insane, and then forces the target to make a DC 14 save against the poisoned condition, which is okay. They can save out of the condition at the end of their turn, but we\u2019re here for the damage. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                • Bone Naga Venom<\/span>: Injury Poison. Deals 3d6 damage without a save. Better than basilisk poison, and that was already good. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                • Chuul Poison<\/span>: Contact poison. DC 13. Poisoning a creature for 1 minute or until they save out of it is bad, and the DC is low. Don\u2019t use this unless you have no other poisons. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                • Couatl\u2019s Bite<\/span>: Injury poison. DC 13. As a poison that knocks a creature unconscious this could be compared to drow poison, except it has a higher DC, a longer duration and damage doesn\u2019t awaken the creature (yet somehow shaking them awake does?) This is a free kill on any isolated creature, provided they fail the save. Couatls are lawful good, and can be summoned with the Conjure Celestial<\/a> spell, so you could potentially just ask to harvest some of its poison if you give it a reason why that would be in its interest. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                • Corpse Flower Poison<\/span>: Contact poison. Deals 3d6 damage without a save. Always useful. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                • Dire Troll’s Bite<\/span>: Injury poison. The target takes 1d10 damage without a save, which is good. It would be challenging in normal circumstances to capture a huge creature and keep it in a cage, but trolls have regeneration. Cut off its head, and carry it with you, and maybe it will keep generating more poison. It also might grow back its body, so it\u2019s a risky play. <\/li>\n\n\n\n

                    Is This a Harvestable Poison?<\/h2>\n\n\n

                    Things get a bit more fuzzy with some creatures, but we\u2019ve included as many options as we can just in case. If your DM doesn\u2019t think that a specific poison or \u201ctype\u201d of poison can be harvested, that\u2019s their prerogative. They\u2019re included here so that you and your group can make that decision together. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Harvesting Inhaled Poisons<\/strong>
                    There\u2019s no reason to assume inhaled poisons can\u2019t be harvested, but neither is there a reason to assume they can be. We are not given enough examples in the DMG for creatures that are able to be harvested for poison, so this is a question that should be considered by each DM individually. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Creatures that could be harvested for inhaled poisons would have a spores or breath attack, or the stench property (which is covered later in this article.) There aren\u2019t a lot of inhaled poisons that could be harvested, but a few of those that can are some of the strongest monster attacks in the game, which may reasonably put any DM off on letting players use them. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                      \n
                    • Dretch\u2019s Fetid Cloud<\/span>: Inhaled poison, DC 11. Causes the poisoned condition, and doesn\u2019t allow the target to use both an action and a bonus action on the same turn. Also the target can\u2019t take reactions. The latter part is probably the most useful, since not a lot of monsters have bonus actions. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Green Dragon Breath<\/span>: Inhaled poison. DC and damage based on the age category of the dragon. This ranges from a DC 11 and 6d6 damage with wyrmling, all the way to DC 17 and 22d6 damage with an ancient dragon. All of them allow a creature to save for half damage. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Iron Golem Breath<\/span>: Inhaled poison. DC 19. This deals 10d8 poison damage on a failed save and half as much on a success. It\u2019s great. Plus the Manual of Golems allows the creation of an iron golem who will follow your commands, though the price to build one is a whopping 100,000gp and 120 days of work. On the off chance you can do this, you have access to a near infinite supply of what is essentially nerve gas.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Myconid’s Euphoria Spores<\/span>: Inhaled poison, DC 11 (Adult) or 12 (Sovereign). This makes the target poisoned, which is fine, but after the poisoned condition, it gains a level of exhaustion. Exhaustion debuffs are really hard to come by, but they do stack and the effects get very bad for the target. Hit someone with this, and then drop Sickening Radiance on them. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Vegepygmy Chief’s Spores<\/span>: Inhaled Poison. DC 12. Imposes the poisoned condition on the target, and then an ongoing 2d8 poison damage. A target can attempt to save out at the end of its turn. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Vrock’s Spores<\/span>: Inhaled Poison. DC 14. Imposes the poisoned condition on the target, and then an ongoing 1d10 poison damage. A target can attempt to save out at the end of its turn. <\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n\n

                      Stench Poisons<\/h3>\n\n\n\n
                      <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                      Continuing with inhaled poisons, this is a section I imagine more DMs may take issue with, as defining \u201cstench\u201d as a poison is a challenging assertion. Stench does cause the Poisoned condition, but not every DM will see it as a poison. With no examples in the DMG stating one way or the other, it\u2019s up to each DM to decide for themselves. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                      Stench basically all operates the same way, aside from the creatures affected and the range of the stench (always 5 or 10 feet.) We can ignore the range of the stench if we\u2019re harvesting it, since we\u2019ll be delivering it as an inhaled poison, which always only occupies a 5-foot space. All of these are fairly lackluster as an object to use as an action, as they only last until the start of the creature\u2019s turn, and the creature is immune to the effects of the stench for 24 hours on a successful saving throw. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                      • Troglodyte Stench<\/span>: Inhaled poison, DC 12. Inflicts the poisoned condition on any creature other than troglodytes. <\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n\n

                        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                          Weird Poisons<\/h3>\n\n\n\n
                          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                          • Clockwork Iron Cobra’s Bite<\/span>: DC 13, Injury poison. This would be a normal poison, except you roll a d6 to see if the target is confused (on a result of 1-2), paralyzed (on a result of 3-4) or takes 3d8 poison damage.                           • Flumph’s Stench Spray<\/span>: No Save, Contact poison. It would otherwise it would be included in the inhaled section. It\u2019s easily the most interesting \u201cstench poison\u201d, as it poisons every creature within 5 feet of it without a save.                           • Kraken\u2019s Ink: Of every poison a monster creates in the core books, this one needs to be homebrewed the most for a character to use.                             Frequently Asked Questions about Poisons<\/h2>\n\n\n\n
                            <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                            Due to the general ambiguity surrounding a lot of the poison rules, I thought it prudent to have a section with questions. These are also questions I have, and will likely remain ambiguous until Jeremy Crawford deigns to answer them on twitter. Still, everything noted here is important for you to consider, and perhaps ask your DM which way they would rule. <\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                            <\/div>\n\n\n\n
                            \n
                            \n
                            \n

                            How often can a poison be harvested from an incapacitated creature?<\/h3>\n
                            \n\n

                            Unclear, and totally up to your DM, though you probably shouldn\u2019t expect more than a handful of times a day. In real life (according to the National Institute of Health) venom can be harvested from snakes every 60 days, but that\u2019s not entirely consistent with how much venom snakes seem capable of producing in game. As a DM, this is one of the tools you can use to make sure that players don\u2019t harvest so much poison that it becomes problematic.\u00a0<\/p>\n\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n

                            \n

                            Do effects that stem from the poisoned condition stack on top of each other? Do multiple ongoing poisons of the same type stack?<\/h3>\n
                            \n\n

                            Spells with ongoing effects of the same type cannot affect a creature twice. Poisons are not spells. That being said, you also can\u2019t get the poisoned condition twice, and the poisoned condition underlies most ongoing poison effects. This is a double-edged sword though: If DMs rule this way, they should prepare for players to have their characters get drunk before battles with venomous creatures, so that they won’t be affected by the creature\u2019s poisoned condition effects.<\/p>\n\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n

                            \n

                            For ingested poison, if a dose is added to a communal food container, how many people are affected by it?<\/h3>\n
                            \n\n

                            This is DM fiat, though the rules on inhaled poisons do say that the DM can choose for a partial dose to affect creatures with a reduced effect, either by granting them advantage on the save or lessening the damage dealt.\u00a0<\/p>\n\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n

                            \n

                            Can I harvest poison from transmuted or conjured creatures?<\/h3>\n
                            \n\n

                            This is totally outside the range of material covered in the rules, so the answer is it\u2019s totally up to your DM. If you are a player, you should try it. If you are a DM you should always make the poison disappear when the spell ends.<\/p>\n\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n
