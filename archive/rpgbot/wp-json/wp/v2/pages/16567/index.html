Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

When Eberron was released, it included the Artificer and three subclasses for the new class. When Tasha\u2019s Cauldron of Everything was later published, the Artificer and those subclasses were reprinted along with a new fourth subclass. It has remained as such since then: four official subclasses, the least of any class. But did you know that Keith Baker, the original author and creator of Eberron, published a third party supplement not long after Rising from the Last War? This book, Exploring Eberron<\/a>, contains two new subclasses for the Artificer (as well as one each for Bard, Cleric, Druid and Monk).<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

In this handbook, we\u2019ll be exploring the Forge Adept subclass for the Artificer, a frontline support combatant. This subclass is similar to the Battle Smith but gives up the go-go dog person sidekick for more personal power with a subclass exclusive Infusion, the Ghaal\u2019Shaarat. While any Artificer can use the Enhance Weapon Infusion to get a magical weapon, this feature does not take up a daily Infusion slot for the Forge Adept.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

One extra Infusion might not sound big, but avoiding the tax that a Battle Smith has to pay by Infusing a weapon to use Intelligence  to hit and damage is a big deal. We like to talk about Fundamental Math<\/a> quite a lot around here, but we don\u2019t usually explore just how much that math can be tilted sideways with even a +1 weapon. Assuming a four person party with three weapon users, a Forge Adept can get magic weapons on all of them from third level.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

The math is a little messy, but at lower levels the DPR increase from those weapons is ever so slightly higher than the average +2.5 attack bonus increase from Bless because of the +1 to both hit and damage. With enough other added damage the calculation does swing back into Bless\u2019 favor, but by the time that happens it might be reasonable to spend slots on Bless throughout an adventuring day. Not like anything is stopping you from stacking the pair either.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        Forge Adept Features<\/h2>\n\n\n\n
        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
          \n\t
        1. Tool Proficiency<\/span>: Smith\u2019s tools are alright, and you even get to pick any other tool if you already have Smith\u2019s proficiency. There are plenty of nice tools<\/a> to choose from so you\u2019ll find something useful here.<\/li>\n\t
        2. Forge Adept Spells<\/span>: A good mix of Paladin and Cleric spells, with a few Warlock exclusives mixed in as well. These spells lean onto the defensive and supportive side of things, with a few damage options thrown in. This pairs nicely with other Forge Adept features that provide damage elsewhere.<\/li>\n
            \n\t
          • 3rd Level<\/span>: A pair of defensive spells that work well for us, providing either Temporary Hit Points or an increase to AC. These spells would rank higher, but our half slot progression inhibits our ability to upcast.<\/li>\n\t
          • 5th Level<\/span>: These spells are ranked orange not because they are bad spells but because they are somewhat more situational options. We could get some mileage out of the damage spell, but it eats up our bonus action for the duration and by this level the damage output is already starting to lag.<\/li>\n\t
          • 9th Level<\/span>: More defensive and restorative spells. In particular, one of these buffs makes us a healer\u2019s best friend, not only for the effect it brings but also that they don\u2019t have to be the one Concentrating on it.<\/li>\n\t
          • 13th Level<\/span>: A couple of non-Concentration longer duration defensive buffs, the spells at this level are nice but we have so few high-level spell slots. Like before, these are not bad spells, they are just harder to justify using.<\/li>\n\t
          • 17th level<\/span>: We come to a pair of very strong control options that work well even at this level. It helps that one of these spells is a 5th-level Paladin spell and those were all balanced around being only available at higher levels.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\t
          • Battle Ready<\/span>: Exactly the same as the Battle Smith feature of the same name. As long as your weapon is magical, you can use Intelligence instead of Strength or Dexterity. This opens up plenty of possibilities by itself, but it pairs really well with the other 3rd level Forge Adept feature.<\/li>\n\t
          • Ghaal’Shaarat<\/span>: Goblin for \u201cmighty blade.\u201d Put very simply, Ghaal\u2019Shaarat is a special Infusion available to Forge Adepts that gives a weapon a scaling +X bonus as well as the Returning property if it happens to be a Thrown weapon. Where it differs from other Infusions is that it does not count against our Infusion limit and it only functions when held by the Forge Adept. This feature means we don\u2019t sacrifice one of our Infusion choices every day just so Battle Ready can function. Because this eventually reaches +3, which is the value we use when talking about Advantage, it\u2019s somewhat on par with a Reckless Raging Barbarian who can still cast spells.<\/li>\n\t
          • Extra Attack<\/span>: Because a Forge Adept focuses on fighting with their Ghaal\u2019Shaarat, extra attack is very helpful. Unlike the Battle Smith, the Forge Adept does gain on-hit damage bonuses, but we still have to wait till we\u2019re 9th level to get the first one.<\/li>\n\t
          • Runes of War<\/span>: AoE Elemental Weapons. While it does use our Concentration, this is a powerful effect in many parties and usable often enough to be available in most fights for the average adventuring day. This is more effective as your party makes additional weapon attacks, so in parties with classes like fighters, monks, and rangers this is great. In parties which rely on big single attacks (rogues, most casters) you will see less benefit.<\/li>\n\t
          • Perfect Weapon<\/span>: This has two features. The first benefit is like a reverse Reckless Attack, taking a penalty to our offense to boost our defense. Players of 3.x may recognize this as the Defending magic weapon property or Combat Expertise. The second benefit is one of two persistent upgrades to the Ghaal\u2019Shaarat that can be changed every morning after a long rest, one defensive and one offensive. Both are welcome additions that help keep us relevant at this level of combat.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n\n\n\n\n

            Forge Adept Ability Scores<\/h2>\n\n\n

            The Forge Adept doesn\u2019t stray from the typical spread laid out in the Artificer Handbook<\/a>. Thanks to Battle Ready we can focus on Intelligence for weapon attacks as well as Spell DCs.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

            <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

            Str<\/span>: We don\u2019t need this for anything.<\/p>\n

            Dex<\/span>: We\u2019re proficient with Medium armor and shields so we shouldn\u2019t need more than 14.<\/p>\n

            Con<\/span>: We\u2019re blessed to have proficiency with this saving throw, but we still want to have hit points.<\/p>\n

            Int<\/span>: This is our \u201cdo everything\u201d ability score. It does everything.<\/p>\n

            Wis<\/span>: Good for some skills and this kind of saving throw is dangerous to fail.<\/p>\n

            Cha<\/span>: We\u2019re not going to be the face of the team.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n
