        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        The Sorcerer’s psionics-themed subclass, the Aberrant Mind notably lacks the\n\tsame Psionic Energy Dice shared by the Psi Warrior and Soul Knife. Jeremy\n\tCrawford explained that feedback from the Unearthed Arcana playtest pointed\n\tout that the Sorcerer already has a pool of class-specific expendable\n\tresources in their Sorcery Points, and adding a second pool felt weird. So,\n\tWotC listened to community feedback and removed it for the final version of\n\tthe subclass. It’s fun to be part of the design process, isn’t it?<\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Without Psionic Energy Dice, there’s little that makes this subclass actually\n\t“psionic”; it’s mostly just themed around tentacles. In fact, it’s much more\n\t“Great Old One” than “Psionic”, though I’m starting to think that the two\n\tmight be more closely related than I realize. If you can’t decide between a\n\tGOOLock and a Sorcerer, this is a great compromise.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Mechanically, the Aberrant Mind is excellent. Psionic Spells dramatically\n\texpands your number of known spells, and doesn’t completely lock you into the\n\tgranted spells like most similar subclass features. The other subclass\n\tfeatures offer a number of interesting and powerful utility options, allowing\n\tyou to solve many problems without needing to find a suitable spell and\n\tcommitting one of your precious few spells known. If you’re more comfortable\n\tplaying a cleric or a wizard and having a lengthy list of spells available,\n\tthe Aberrant Mind can make the Sorcerer a much easier prospect since its\n\ttoolbox is so mucg larger than a typical sorcerer.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

            Clockwork Soul<\/a>\n\t(TCoE<\/a>)<\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n
            <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

            Where the Wild Magic Sorcerer is chaos, randomness, rerolls, and wacky fun\n\tchaos, the Clockwork Soul Sorcerer is order, no rerolls, numerical minimums,\n\tand straight order and efficiency.             The Clockwork Soul shares a lot of design philosophy with the Abberant Mind,\n\tgiving sorcerers 10 more known leveled spells and the ability to retrain them\n\tinto a pair of specific schools of magic. However, the Clockwork Soul’s other\n\tfeatures are notably more situational in nature than those given to the\n\tAbberant Mind. The Clockwork Soul is by no means bad or weak, though, and its\n\tspell list includes several excellent options from the Cleric spell list\n\t(though none of them are hit point restoration so you’ll need to get that\n\telsewhere), in addition to a lot of staple spellcasting options which a\n\tsorcerer might otherwise be forced to take with their very limited known\n\tspells to avoid leaving their party without crucial options like Dispel\n\tMagic.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

            For a veteran player with a solid grasp of the rules and of their party’s\n\tcapabilities, the Clockwork Soul Sorcerer is an excellent option. However,\n\tunless you know exactly where the Clockwork Soul fits into your party, you may\n\tfind that the subclass has a lot of redundancies with other spellcasters. If\n\tyour party wants to go without a cleric or druid, the Clockwork Soul is a\n\tgreat option, though you’ll still need to solve the issue of hit point\n\trestoration. For less experienced players, the Clockwork Soul may be a great\n\tintroduction to the Sorcerer due to their abnormally large list of known\n\tspells, but personally I would still point new players to less complex\n\tsubclasses like draconic bloodline or less “serious business” options like\n\twild magic.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
