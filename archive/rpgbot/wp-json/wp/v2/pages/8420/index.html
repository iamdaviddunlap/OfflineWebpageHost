Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

A divine spellcaster with little or no interaction with actual divine beings,\n\tthe Oracle draws their power from a mystery of some kind, and in exchange they\n\tget divine spellcasting but also a curse which is usually (but not always)\n\tsome degree of terrible.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

The Oracle is an exciting concept, originally based on DnD 3.5’s Favored\n\tSoul, then introduced in Pathfinder 1st edition, where it introduced the\n\tconcpet of the Oracle’s Curse. Throughout its history, the Oracle has been the\n\tdivine equivalent to the Sorcerer, sharing the cleric’s armor and weapon\n\toptions but using the Sorcerer’s spellcasting mechanics. In Pathfinder 2e,\n\tthat means using a Spell Repertoire like the Sorcerer while getting similar\n\tequipment to the Cleric.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Because the Oracle and the Sorcerer are so similar, it’s natural to draw some comparisons, especially since the Sorcerer can also use the Divine spell list<\/a>. The Oracle’s Mystery<\/a> and the Sorcerer’s Bloodline<\/a> serve to distinguish the two classes thematically, and while the handful of bloodlines that use the Divine spell list will often feel similar in many ways, oracles have more room for diversity while stil using the same spell list. The Oracle’s Mystery also comes with Drawbacks and a condition track, where the Sorcerer’s Bloodline is purely beneficial, so the Oracle brings some additional complexity that can be fun to play with at the cost of additional challenges.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Playing an oracle is challenging. The Oracle’s Curse is frequently frustrating and requires a lot of tracking and cost\/benefit calculations. The Focus Spells (called “Revelation Spells”) are no better than those of other classes, but come with additonal cost. The Oracle’s Class Feats are limited in number, and there are unusually many bad options.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The Oracle’s skills and saves are poor in many ways, and despite several options intended to support oracles using weapons they remain terrible at doing so. Your best bet is to play the Oracle like a weird sorcerer, and in many cases a sorcerer with the Divine Spell List will be both easier to play and more effective, if only because you’re not saddled with the Oracle’s Curse.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

            Oracle Class Features<\/h2>\n\n\n

            Key Ability<\/strong>: Charisma.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

            Initial Proficiencies<\/span>: The Oracle’s\n\tproficiencies are poor, which is typical for a full spellcaster.<\/p>\n

              Divine Spellcasting<\/strong>:<\/p>\n

                \n\t
              • Heightened Spells<\/span>: Heightening spells is\n\t\tan important mechanic in Pathfinder 2e. Many spells scale with spell level,\n\t\tremaining effective long past their base spell level. Since oracles use a\n\t\tSpell Repertoire, you need to learn spells at multiple levels to heighten\n\t\tthem in most cases, though Signature Spells are an exception.<\/li>\n\t
              • Cantrips<\/span>: Fantastic every time, on any\n\t\tcharacter, in any amount. Oracles learn 5 cantrips at level 1 and never get\n\t\tmore, but you can replace them by retraining or when you gain a level so\n\t\tyou’re not locked into whatever you pick at first level.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

                Spell Repertoire<\/span>: Spell Repertoires are\n\tless versatile than spellcasting like the Cleric’s or the Druid’s. You know a\n\tfixed number of spells which grows as you gain levels, and while you don’t\n\tneed to prepare your spells ahead of time you also lose the flexibility of\n\tchanging your spells every day. You also need to learn the same spells at\n\tmultiple levels if you plan to heighten them, which is a difficult cost to\n\tpay. Fortunately, the Oracle’s Signature Spells feature (see below) allows you\n\tto heighten some of your spells without learning them multiple times.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Mysteries<\/strong>: See my\n\tOracle Mysteries Breakdown<\/a>.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Revelation Spells<\/span>: You start with a Focus\n\tPool of 2 points for free, which is very generous. The wording of the feature\n\tis a little vague, but you do still follow the usual rules for Refocusing, so\n\tyou can only recover one point until you cast another Focus Spell, so you only\n\trecharge the full pool when you rest (until you get a feat that lets you\n\tRefocus to get more points back). You also get two Focus Spells, so you have\n\tmore options for your Focus Points than most characters do at first level.<\/p>\n

                Oracular Curse<\/span>: The drawback for your\n\tRevelation Spells, Oracular curse is a sort of “death spiral” mechanic where\n\tthings get worse for you the further you go down the track. At first level,\n\tyour curse can be in its “Basic” state, its “Minor” state, its “Moderate”\n\tstate, or you can be overwhelmed. Each time you cast a Revelation Spell, your\n\tcurse increases one step. You can reduce your curse to Minor by Refocusing,\n\tbut you can only get back to Basic if you rest for 8 hours. You add the\n\t“Major” state at level 11 and the “Extreme” state at level 17, each of which\n\tadds another step before you become Overwhelmed.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Becoming Overwhelmed sucks. Don’t do it unless you’re absolutely desperate.\n\tOnce you’re Overwhelmed, you can’t cast or sustain your Revelation Spells\n\tuntil you get a full rest. That means that if you cast a Revelation Spell that\n\tmade your Overwhelmed, you can’t Sustain that spell. You can still Refocus to\n\treduce your curse and use your Focus Points for other Focus Spells, but your\n\tRevelation Spells are gone for the day. Once you hit level 17 and get Extreme\n\tCurse, you’re also Doomed 2 until you rest. Again: becoming Overwhelmed sucks.\n\tDon’t do it.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Fortunately, becoming Overwhelmed by your curse is actually fairly difficult. At first level, you get 2 Focus Points. If you spend both, you’re at Major. If you Refocus, you go to Minor and get 1 Focus Point so you can only get back up to Major.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                You need another Focus Point from another source (likely from a class feat or from an archetype, but there are Ancestry Feats and items which can help, too). If you don’t get Focus Points from other sources, you’ll get a third point at level 11 when you get Major Curse, but you still can’t make yourself Overwhelmed unless you somehow get a Focus Point some way besides refocusing. I think this mechanic is in place specifically to discourage you from going beyond the maximum Focus Pool size of 3 by using items or feats which restore a Focus Point<\/p>\n\n\n\n
