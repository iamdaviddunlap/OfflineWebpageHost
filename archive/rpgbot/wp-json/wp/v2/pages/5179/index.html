Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

Available to the Champion, the Cleric, and a handful of other character options via feats such as Domain Initiate and Deity’s Domain, domains represent a specific divine concept, and are often associated with one or more deities.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

In practical terms, a Domain is a pair of Focus Spells with a similar theme. You generally need to learn the first spell (the “Domain Spell”) before you can learn the second (the “Advanced Domain Spell”), but you are not forced to learn the Advanced Domain Spell.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

      Darkness<\/a>CRB<\/a><\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

      If your party is sneaky, or if your party all has Darkvision and can function well in complete darkness, the Darkness domain offers some exciting options which your party can benefit from consistently.       Advanced Domain Spell: Darkened Eyes<\/span>: This\n\tis only useful if your whole party has Darkvision and you’re fighting in the\n\tdark.       Death<\/a>CRB<\/a><\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

      Death’s Call is an easy and reliable way to turn your Focus Points into damage mitigation. Eradicate Undeath is only useful if you expect undead enemies to appear in your campaign, but taking Domain Initiate to get Death’s Call doesn’t obligate you to go any further.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Domain Spell: Death’s Call<\/span>: Consistently\n\tuseful in combat, the temporary hit points can easily make up the gap in\n\tdurability between the Cleric and more durable classes like the Fighter.\n\tEncounters with multiple enemies are the norm in Pathfinder 2e, so you’ll\n\tnearly always have fodder to trigger Death’s Call while still having time for\n\tits effects to matter in the same encounter.<\/p>\n

      Advanced Domain Spell: Eradicate Undeath<\/span>:\n\tSituational by design, but against undead this is a solid, reliable burst\n\tdamage option.       Fate<\/a>CRB<\/a><\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

      If you and your GM are on board for the GM to predict the future and give you vague premonitions as a result, this domain can be a lot of fun. But that requires a degree of trust between you (the player) and the GM, and it requires that the GM have enough mastery of running tabletop RPGs for that arrangement to work in a way that’s both interesting and rewarding enough to justify taking Domain Initiate in order to get Read Fate. If you and your GM are prepared for that, there’s a lot of fun to be had here. But if your GM is new, adversarial, unconfident, or otherwise not in a good place to handle Read Fate, skip the Fate Domain.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Domain Spell: Read Fate<\/span>: I am intentionally\n\tnot rating this spell. While I absolutely love stuff like this, the effects\n\tare simply too subjective for me to provide any actionable advice which\n\tapplies universally.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      If you and your GM enjoy stuff like this, it can be a really fun addition to your game. Your GM could give you a word like “death” and you could spend the next hour trying to determine if the DM meant your death or someone elses (adventurers tend to do a lot of killing).<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Conversely, if your GM isn’t on board for stuff like this you might get a lot of “inconclusive” responses, or the GM might give you a word that makes sense at the time but which they immediately forget about so the word may have no bearing on what is actually going to happen within the spell’s duration.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Advanced Domain Spell: Tempt Fate<\/span>: This is\n\tintentionally a gamble. A Reaction to provide a +1 or +2 bonus on a saving\n\tthrow is great, but I’m hesitant to recommend this because you’ll usually make\n\tthis gamble without an accurate idea of the probabilities and often you won’t\n\tknow the results of a Critical Success or a Critical Failure. On some effects,\n\ta Critical Success is no better than a Success. On some effects, a Critical\n\tFailure means you die (or something similarly unpleasant happens).\n\tMathematically this is good, but I’m still nervous about it.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

      Freedom<\/a>CRB<\/a><\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

      Two good options to handle a variety of things which could prevent you and your allies from moving under your own power. Both options can get you out of grapples, but you can also handle more severe conditions like paralysis.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Domain Spell: Unimpeded Stride<\/span>: “Nothing\n\tcan hold you in place”… except the inability to perform Somatic components\n\tto cast this. If your hands are tied, Unimpeded Stride is useless, which\n\thonestly seems like a design oversight. Even so, this gets you out of grapples\n\tand many other things which might restrain you temporarily like spells and\n\tcreatures’ special abilities. Conveniently this also lets you Stride so you\n\tdon’t give up any action economy for it.<\/p>\n

      Advanced Domain Spell: Word of Freedom<\/span>:\n\tSituational by design, but between all of the listed effects this will come up\n\toften enough to be useful. The 1-Action casting time is especially helpful\n\tbecause you still get two Actions on your turn to Step away from a grapple or\n\tcast another spell. Keep in mind that the effect is only suppressed for one\n\tround (though any Grab is broken and doesn’t automatically reapply) so the\n\ttarget needs to act quickly once you cast this.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

      Healing<\/a>CRB<\/a><\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

      Don’t both unless you plan to pick up Rebuke Death. Healer’s Blessing is unimpressive and encourages you to spend spell slots in foolish ways, but Rebuke Death completely changes the tactics around dying allies.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Domain Spell: Healer’s Blessing<\/span>: This is\n\tfrustratingly the weak. This doesn’t synergize well with spells which provide\n\thealing over time, so the expectation is that you’ll cast this then repeatedly\n\thit the target with Heal or something similar. But doing so is typically a\n\tgrotesque misuse of resources.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      In-combat healing should typically only be done in an emergency, and outside of combat you need to use options like Medicine or Rebuke Death to preserve limited resources like spell slots. If you have time to make Healer’s Blessing work, you probably have time to use the Medicine skill’s Treat Wounds activity, and outside of combat that’s a much less expensive way to handle hit point restoration.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Perhaps the best use case for Healer’s Blessing is as a stepping stone until you can get Rebuke Death. At low levels, the additional healing from Healer’s Blessing can stretch your spell slots spent on healing magic enough to get you by without needing to heavily invest in Medicine or other hit point restoration. At high levels when the bonus has scaled considerably, spending a 1st-level spell slot to cast Heal can provide an impressive large amount of healing at low cost, though the AOE heal option can also trigger Healer’s Blessing and will restore considerably more hp for the same spell slot, so that’s still the preferred way to use Heal.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Advanced Domain Spell: Rebuke Death<\/span>: The\n\tability to bring a dying ally back to consciousness without applying the\n\tWounded condition is huge<\/em>. Wounded is what makes falling to 0 hit\n\tpoints scary in Pathfinder 2e, so the ability to remove that “death spiral”\n\tmechanic means that falling to 0 hit points is considerably less of a problem.\n\tBringing allies back up from 0 hit points gets them back into the fight (at\n\tleast temporarily), easily replacing the Actions which you spent to heal\n\tthem.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      The amount of healing is similar to the single-target and AOE versions of the Heal spell (though Rebuke Death uses a smaller die), but unlike Heal you can target up to three allies with no risk of healing your enemies. This allows you to heal multiple allies in the middle of combat much more easily than you could with Heal.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Outside of combat, this is a great way to heal your party members at no cost. Refocus takes 10 minutes compared to the 10-minute (or 1-hour) Activity for Treat Wounds, and with Rebuke Death you don’t need to invest skill increases and potentially feats to make Medicine into a renewable source of hit point restoration. Even better, Rebuke Death will restore more hit points than Treat Wounds, and will scale automatically as you gain levels, remaining consistently ahead of Treat Wounds even as you add more Skill Increases and potentially feats. Medicine may still be useful for treating poison and diseases, but Rebuke Death neatly solves the issue of hit point restoration outside of combat.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

      Indulgence<\/a>CRB<\/a><\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

      Two excellent options that have a variety of interesting of uses.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      Domain Spell: Overstuff<\/span>: Sickened is an\n\texcellent debuff which doesn’t go away on its own, and which the target may\n\tstruggle to remove even after spending multiple Actions to do so. The target\n\talso gets nourishment as though had eaten a full meal, entirely removing your\n\tparty’s need to worry about food. Admittedly this sounds like an uncomfortable\n\tand unpleasant replacement for eating, but it’s certainly efficient.<\/p>\n

      Advanced Domain Spell: Take its Course<\/span>:\n\tSituational by design, but it covers conditions which are common enough that\n\tyou and your party will definitely benefit from this even if you only use it\n\tdefensively. Poison and disease afflictions can cause serious problems for\n\tyour allies (though disease afflicitions typically have a generous delay\n\tbefore they become a problem) and granting an additional save to try to end\n\tthem early can save you a lot of trouble. Similarly, for persistent poison\n\tdamage you can allow an ally to make an additional save to end the effect and\n\tset the flat check DC to 5, giving them a 80% chance to end the effect and\n\tthereby prevent a huge amount of additional damage.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      If you have someone in the party who likes to use poisons (alchemists are a great example), forcing enemies to make another save against the poison with a -2 penalty can increase the severity of the affliction and cause all manner of problems for them. Re-triggering persistent poison damage sounds tempting, but the damage will at best match the damage from hitting the target with an attack cantrip, so re-triggering the persistent damage is generally a poor use of a Focus Point unless you’re desperate and struggling to harm the target by other means.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

      Magic<\/a>CRB<\/a><\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n
