Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

Since the days of 3.0, the Monk has been somewhat of an enigma. An almost strictly combat character with medium Base Attack Bonus progression, d8 hit points, and no armor. Monks in Pathfinder still have the same features, but many of their most glaring issues have been heavily improved upon.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Due to their unique abilities, it can be difficult to identify the monk’s role in the party. With their skill list, they can function as a scout, but their combat skills make them a passable defender or striker (See my article on Party Composition<\/a>).<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

This guide is for the vanilla Monk. For the Unchained Monk, see my\n\t\tUnchained Monk Handbook<\/a>.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

            Monk Class Features<\/h2>\n\n\n

            Hit Points<\/span>: d8 hit points is hard for a\n\t\tstrictly melee class.<\/p>\n

            Base Attack Bonus<\/span>: The Monk’s primary\n\t\toffensive option, Flurry of Blows, pretends that the Monk has full BAB,\n\t\twhich is usually enough in combat but can be frustrating when qualifying for\n\t\tfeats.<\/p>\n

            Saves<\/span>: All good saves.<\/p>\n

            Proficiencies<\/span>: The Monk is proficient\n\t\twith Monk weapons (which make up a collection of strange, exotic, and often\n\t\tforeign weapons) and a couple of other odds and ends like hand axes,\n\t\tcrossbows, and spears. Strangely, the Monk weapons tend to be the best\n\t\toptions since they can benefit from Flurry of blows. Monks gain no armor or\n\t\tshield proficiencies, and generally rely on their Monk AC bonus instead.<\/p>\n

            Skills<\/span>: The Monk skill list has several\n\t\texcellent options, several of which are difficult for the monk to capitalize\n\t\ton. By splashing a few skills and focusing on the best ones, the Monk can\n\t\tmake excellent use of their 4+ skill points.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

            <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

            AC Bonus<\/span> (Ex): The Monk AC bonus helps\n\t\tto offset the monk’s lack of armor. The scaling is fairly negligible, but it\n\t\tstill feels good to have. However, your AC will still be awful without\n\t\tadditional items and AC improvements.<\/p>\n

            Flurry of Blows<\/span> (Ex): This is the Monk’s\n\t\ticonic attack. It follows the same progression as a fighter maximizing the\n\t\ttwo-weapon fighting feats, but you have no “off-hand” attacks. You don’t\n\t\teven need free hands. You can use your feet if your hands are full. You can\n\t\talso alternate freely between weapons and unarmed strikes, and you can\n\t\tsubstitute disarm, sunder, and trip combat maneuvers for unarmed attacks.<\/p>\n

            Unarmed Strike<\/span>: This is why you generally\n\t\tplay a monk: because you want to hit things with your bare hands\/feet\/etc.\n\t\tThe damage dice starts off fairly small, but when you hit d8 at level 4, it\n\t\tbegins to outpace whatever weapons you might be two-weapon fighting with. At\n\t\tlevel 12, your fists are greatswords. You can also deal nonlethal damage\n\t\twithout penalty, which is situationally useful. It’s important to note that\n\t\tyour unarmed strike damage is heavily size-based, so you want to do\n\t\teverything you can to increase your effective size. Get enlarged and throw\n\t\tImpact on an Amulet of Mighty Fists. It’s also interesting to note that the\n\t\tdamage progression stops at 20th-level, so items like a Monks Belt stop\n\t\tbeing useful at high levels unless you multiclass.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
