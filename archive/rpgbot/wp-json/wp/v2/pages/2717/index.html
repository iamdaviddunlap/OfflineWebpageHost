Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

A staple of fantasy fiction and folklore, Elves are a simple yet effective\n\tbase race with subraces which are numerous and diverse enough that elves are\n\tusable in a variety of builds and character concepts. Unfortunately, because\n\tthe subraces differentiate the Elf’s traits so much, it’s rare for more than\n\tone subrace to be viable in the same class unless you’re using the Customizing\n\tYour Origins optional rules presented in Tasha’s Cauldron of Everything.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Since the initial release of 5th edition, the evolution of the game has\n\trepeatedly shaken up the Elf’s place in the character optimization meta. At\n\tinitial release, the Wood Elf was the only race with increases to Dexterity\n\tand Wisdom (excluding humans and half-elves since they have flexible\n\tincreases), and the Elf was one of very few races which got additional skills,\n\tespecially since elves get Perception. This made them appealing in many\n\tbuilds, including Druids and Monks. As we got additional source books, the\n\tintroduction of Booming Blade made the High Elf a powerful option for rogues\n\tand for “gish” builds.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

The introduction of the custom origin rules was a big hit to the Elf, making\n\tthe Wood Elf largely pointless, but making other elf subraces much more\n\tappealing. The High Elf especially is now an even more appealing option for\n\tgish builds since you can reassign the Intelligence increase to something\n\telse, making the High Elf an appealing option for martial bard builds,\n\tclerics, druid, rogues, and some multiclass builds. The Drow’s innate\n\tspellcasting is appealing for Charisma-based spellcasters. The sea elf exists.\n\tThe Eladrin and the Shadar-kai offer teleportation for martial builds,\n\tespecially non-casters. There’s an elf for every build.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Elves are one of very few published races with an aquatic option. While the\n\tSea Elf is conceptually interesting, it gets very little beyond the ability to\n\tfunction well underwater, so I habitually ignore it in my character\n\toptimization content. I will continue to do that here, but if you’re playing\n\tin an aquatic campaign, remember that the Sea Elf is a thing.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          Elf Classes (Customizable Origins)<\/h2>\n\n\n

          This section assumes that you’re using the option “Customizing Your Origin<\/a>” rules presented in Tasha’s Cauldron of Everything. If you’re not using those rules, scroll down to the next section.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Artificer<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The High Elf is the obvious go-to option for the Artificer. An extra cantrip\n\tis a great addition to the Artificer’s somewhat limited spellcasting. However,\n\tthe Artificer takes most of their cantrips from the Wizard’s spell lists, and\n\tall the obvious options that you might want (Booming Blade, Fire Bolt, etc.)\n\tare already on the Artificer’s spell list. You don’t get anything new, just\n\tmore of the same. That’s not a bad thing, of course.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          If you’re fine with the Artificer’s limited number of cantrips, consider the\n\tEladrin (either version) or the Shadar-Kai. Both will give you access to\n\tteleportation long before you get it from the Artificer’s spellcasting, and it\n\twill help conserve precious spell slots.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Barbarian<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Eladrin (the version in Mordenkainen’s Tome of Foes) and the Shadar-Kai\n\toffer access to teleportation, which is a powerful asset for a class so\n\theavily locked into melee. Avoid the Drow, the Pallid Elf, and the High Elf\n\tsince you can’t cast or concentrate on spells while raging.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Bard<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          Access to Booming Blade might be single most simplest way to make front-line\n\tbards viable, despite getting two subclasses (swords and valor) which attempt\n\tto make that happen, so the High Elf is a great choice. For casting-focused\n\tbards, the Drow’s innate spellcasting is a powerful addition, and the Eladrin\n\t(both kinds) offers access to teleportation. The Shadar-Kai is also an option,\n\tbut their teleportation is only once per day, so the Eladrin is a better\n\tchoice for a spellcaster who doesn’t need to teleport into the thick of melee\n\tcombat.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Cleric<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          Clerics planning to use weapons struggle to keep up with clerics relying\n\tsolely on cantrips. Even with the benefit of Divine Strike,\n\tcantrips are still more effective<\/a>. Access to Booming Blade makes melee weapon attacks a viable option, so the\n\tHigh Elf is again a great choice.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          For casting-focused clerics, the Pallid Elf’s innate spellcasting is\n\ttempting, but you can get similar benefits from the Trickery Domain. The\n\tEladrin (either version) may also be appealing since clerics don’t have\n\tshort-range teleportation on their spell list.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Druid<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          Booming Blade is great option for Circle of Spores. The Eladrin’s\n\tteleportation is great for a class with no teleportation on its spell list.\n\tThe Shadar-Kai also works, but since their teleportation is only once per Long\n\tRest, the Eladrin’s is more appealing for a class that generally doesn’t want\n\tto teleport into melee. The Wood Elf exists, but Mask of the Wild isn’t\n\tespecially powerful.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Fighter<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Eladrin and the Shadar-Kai are appealing for melee builds who want access\n\tto teleportation. The High Elf is appealing for the Eldritch Knight, but other\n\tfighters will do better relying on regular attacks.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Monk<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Eladrin (either version) and the Shadar-Kai are your best options, but short-range teleportation is less useful thanks to Step of the Wind and the Monk’s exceptionally high speed. The Elf works here, but the Elf’s traits add very little to the Monk.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Paladin<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Eladrin and the Shadar-Kai are appealing for paladins who want access to\n\tteleportation. A high elf with Booming Blade might work, but most paladins\n\twant to use Extra Attack in hopes of smiting on a critical hit.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Ranger<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          Like other martial classes, the Eladrin and the Shadar-Kai are appealing,\n\tadding teleportation to class that generally doesn’t have it. Combining this\n\tcapability with the Horizon Walker can be a very satisfying build.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          While rangers typically rely on on-hit damage bonuses from things like\n\tFavored Foe or Hunter’s Mark, a high elf with Booming Blade is still an\n\toption, especially if you have a subclass feature that takes a Bonus Action\n\tand adds an on-hit damage bonus like the Fey Wanderer’s Dreadful Strikes since\n\tthose features often make Hunter’s Mark less appealing.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Rogue<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Rogue has a lot of great options. The Drow’s spellcasting offers easy\n\tways to get Advantage or otherwise solve problems. The High Elf’s access to\n\tBooming Blade offers easy hit-and-run tactics in conjunction with Cunning\n\tAction. The Eladrin and the Shadar-Kai offer access to teleportation, though\n\tCunning Action might make that less important. The Pallid elf’s ability to\n\tcast Invisibility is very appealing for any rogue.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Sorcerer<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Drow and the Pallid Elf both offer some innate spellcasting, and having\n\taccess to additional spells, even just once per day, is huge for the Sorcerer.\n\tSimilarly, the Eladrin offers teleportation which you would normally get from\n\tMisty Step, allowing you to safely skip that crucial tactical option and spend\n\tyour extremely limited number of spells known on other things.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Warlock<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Drow’s access to Faerie Fire is a powerful tool for a warlock built\n\taround Eldritch Blast, and any amount of innate spellcasting is helpful for a\n\tclass with so few spell slots. Similarly, the Eladrin and the Shadar-Kai offer\n\taccess to teleportation without the high price of a spell slot. High Elf’s\n\taccess to a cantrip is nice, but the Warlock gets enough of them that it\n\tdoesn’t add anything meaningful.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Wizard<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          An elf wizard is one of those classic DnD tropes that dates back to the\n\tearliest editions. High elves in particular were the “default” race for\n\twizards in the core rules. But with Intelligence increases available on any\n\trace, the High Elf is no longer just “the one you pick for wizards”, and\n\tstrangely it’s probably not the best elf option, either.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          While the High Elf’s additional cantrip is nice, the wizard arguably already\n\tgets enough to get by. Unless you really want that cantrip, the Eladrin is a\n\tbetter choice. A 2nd-level spell slot spent on Misty Step (not to mention\n\tpreparing it) can be a steep cost, so having built-in teleportation is a\n\tpowerful option. The Pallid elf similarly reduces the need to prepare\n\tInvisibility.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Elf Classes (Default Rules)<\/h2>\n\n\n

          This section assumes that you’re not<\/em> using the option “Customizing Your Origin” rules presented in Tasha’s Cauldron of Everything or the updated version of the race published in Mordenkainen’s Monsters of the Multiverse. If you are using those rules, scroll up to the previous section.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Artificer<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The High Elf makes a perfect Artificer, adding both Dexterity and\n\tIntelligence, as well as a Wizard cantrip to complement the Artificer’s\n\tspellcasting.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Barbarian<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          One of few classes where the Elf is a truly bad option. The Sea Elf and the\n\tShadar-kai are probably your best bet here solely because they’re the only elf\n\tsubraces which get increases to either Strength or Constitution. But the\n\tBarbarian is so strictly locked into Strength that it’s hard to function\n\twithout an increase.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Bard<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          Drow, Eladrin, and Mark of Shadow elves all recieve Charisma increases. The\n\tcore elf Perception proficiency and Dexterity increase both complement the\n\tBard nicely in any build.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Cleric<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Cleric is a challenging option for the Elf, but it’s absolutely doable.\n\tWood elves and Pallid Elves both get a Wisdom increase, and between Dexterity\n\tand Wisdom they make fine lightly-armored clerics. Avoid domains which provide\n\theavy armor proficiency, and look for domains which complement your subrace\n\ttraits.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Druid<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          Generally a better option for the Elf than the Cleric, the Druid’s adherence\n\tto lighter armor makes it easier to rely on Dexterity without disregarding\n\tother class options like proficiency in medium and\/or heavy armor. The Wood\n\tElf’s mask of the Wild works while using Wild Shape, and the Pallid Elf’s\n\tinnate spellcasting offers some interesting new options for the Druid to\n\texplore.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Fighter<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          One of few classes where a large number of Elf subraces can truly shine.\n\tSince the Fighter only needs an increase to either Strength or Dexterity, an\n\tElf can fill any Dexterity-based build, and depending on your subrace you can\n\temphasize different capabilities like spellcasting, stealth, or even Face\n\tskills.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Monk<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Wood Elf is among the best race options for the Monk. Dexterity and\n\tWisdom are exactly what the Monk needs, and no class is more dependent on\n\tperfect ability scores. The Pallid Elf has the same ability score increases,\n\tbut the innate spellcasting may be less appealing than the Wood Elf’s traits.\n\tBoth options work great, so use whichever appeals to you.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Paladin<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          Like the Bard, elf subraces which provide Charisma increases make great\n\tpaladins, but don’t overlook subraces which provide Constitution increases.\n\tWhile high Charisma is great, start with 14 or 15 is perfectly fine if you\n\tcan’t find a Charisma increase on the subrace that you like. Obvious options\n\tinclude the Drow and the Eladrin because they do get Charisma increases, but\n\tSea Elves and Shadar-Kai make good options too, and you might even be able to\n\tmake the High Elf work if you can make good use of attack cantrips like\n\tBooming Blade from time to time. Mark of Shadow may seem like a strange choice\n\tfor the Paladin, but mechanically it works very well.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

           <\/div>\n\n\n\n

          Ranger<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Wood Elf is almost tailor-made to be a ranger, but the Ranger’s only\n\tmajor ability dependency is Dexterity so any elf subrace makes a fine\n\tranger.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Rogue<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          The Rogue needs Dexterity and almost nothing else, but free Perception\n\tproficiency does at lot to complement the Rogue’s already spectacular skills.\n\tNearly any elf subrace works for the rogue, offering unique options depending\n\ton your racial traits. High elf rogues should consider booming blade, which\n\tmakes for great hit-and-run tactics when combined with Cunning Action. Mark of\n\tShadow offers several excellent options for the Rogue, even if you don’t plan\n\tto pursue Arcane Trickster.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          The Rogue is, in my opinion, the single best use case for Elven Accuracy. If\n\tyou’re consistently relying on Advantage to deliver Sneak attack the extra die\n\timproved your chance of rolling a natural 20 (and therefore a critical hit)\n\tfrom 9.75% to 14.26%. And if you’ve ever gotten to roll a critical hit with\n\tSneak Attack you understand how exciting it is to roll all of those dice. If\n\tyou use the Steady Aim Optional Class Feature in conjunction with Elven\n\tAccuracy, you get to roll that nonsese almost every turn. It honestly feels\n\tlike cheating.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Sorcerer<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          Drow, Eladrin, and Mark of Shadow elves all get Charisma increases, which is\n\treally all that you need to succeed as a sorcerer, but they don’t add\n\tcapabilities which the sorcerer couldn’t already provide.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Warlock<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          Drow, Eladrin, and Mark of Shadow elves make excellent warlocks. Extra\n\tCharisma is all that you truly need, but Dexterity helps to pad the Warlock’s\n\tAC in light armor, which is crucial for Hexblades. Drow innate spellcasting\n\thelps to complement the Warlock’s limited spell slots, and be sure to pick up\n\tDevil’s Sight to capitalize on the ability to cast Darkness. Eladrin can\n\tteleport, reducing the need for Misty Step. Mark of Shadow grants some extra\n\tspellcasting, and adds several new spell options to the Warlock’s spell\n\tlist.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Wizard<\/a><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          A high elf wizard is among the most obvious and iconic wizard builds. All you\n\ttruly need to succeed as a wizard is an Intelligence increase, and the core\n\tElf’s Dexterity increase and Perception proficiency will help to keep you\n\talive. One extra cantrip may not seem like much since you already start with\n\tthree, but cantrips are very powerful and an extra can do a lot to diversify\n\tyour capabilities.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Elf Feats<\/h2>\n\n\n\n
          <\/div>\n\n\n\n

          Elven Accuracy<\/a>XGtE<\/a><\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n

          If you can get Advantage reliably, this is a fantastic feat. The +1 ability\n\tincrease is nice, but the reroll mechanic is the real draw here. You need\n\tAdvantage to trigger the reroll, but you can reroll one die each time you roll\n\twith Advantage (though you’re limited to attack\/checks\/saves with the four\n\tlister ability scores), so Advantage is essentially rolling three dice and\n\tchoosing the highest.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          It’s a little unusual to lean into Elven Accuracy this far, but my blog post\n\t“Oops All Elves<\/a>” dives into\n\texactly how crazy you can get by building a party around one feat.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Revenant Blade<\/a>ERLW<\/a><\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n

          If you’re proficient with martial weapons and want to engage in two-weapon\n\tfighting, the Double Scimitar is appealing (provided that it’s available in\n\tyour game). Since the primary attack has a larger damage die than weapons\n\twhich qualify for two-weapon fighting, you’ll get more average damage once you\n\tgain Extra Attack. However, most builds which use two-weapon fighting are\n\tDexterity-based, so without the Revenant Blade feat the double scimitar can be\n\ta hard choice.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          If you’re building for two-weapon fighting, Revenant Blade is an improvement\n\tis excellent but not necessarily your best option. It’s an option to replace\n\tDual Wielder, and Revenent Blade isn’t always better. Both feats provide a +1\n\tAC bonus, so let’s ignore that and compare the differences:<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Revenant Blade adds the Finesse property to the Double Scimitar, making it\n\tequivalent to using a rapier and a dagger with Fighting Style (Two-Weapon\n\tFighting). Dual Wielder allows you to use non-light weapons while two-weapon\n\tfighting, allowing you to use two rapiers, two longswords, or something else\n\talong those lines. Two rapiers is better damage than a double scimitar (if we\n\tignore magic weapons), but Revenant Blade adds a +1 increase to either\n\tStrength or Dexterity (probably Dexterity) and doesn’t require a Fighting\n\tStyle.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Once you hit 20 Dexterity, Dual Wielder with Fighting Style (Two-Weapon Fighting) will have better damage output because two rapiers are again more powerful. But the possibility of magic items tips things toward Revenant Blade since it’s easier to find one magic weapon than two, and the resource cost to spend a Fighting Style is steep, so taking a Fighting Style (Defense) with Revenant Blade is typically a more effective combination than Dual Weilder.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          We go into this in more detail in our Practical Guide to Weaponized Bonus Actions<\/a>.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

          Elf Dragonmarks<\/h2>\n\n\n

          Dragonmarks are detailed in Eberron: Rising from the Last War. Elves treat\n\tdragonmarks like a subrace, retaining their core racial traits and adding the\n\ttraits provided by the dragonmark.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Mark of Shadow<\/h3>\n\n\n

The Arcane Trickster seems like\n\tthe most obvious beneficiary, but the only dragonmark spell which is new to\n\tthe Arcane Trickster is Pass Without Trace. Pass Without Trace is amazing, but\n\tit may not be enough make you reconsider your subclass to enjoy it.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          If you’re using the custom origin rules, Mark of Shadow becomes an easy way\n\tto make any character into a Scout, especially if you can cast spells.\n\tArtificers, clerics, druids, and eldritch knight fighters can all benefit\n\tgreatly.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
