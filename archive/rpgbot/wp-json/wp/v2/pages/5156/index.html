Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

The Druid is an interesting class. Thanks to the Primal Spell List, they have some of the healing capabilities of the Cleric, as well as some of the blasting capabilities of the Wizard. But the Druid goes far beyond their spell list: Druids can train a powerful Animal Companion to fight alongside them; they can transform into beasts like wolves, dinosaurs, dragons, and kaiju; or, they can double-down on their core class features, allowing them to do things like conjuring storms or talking to plants at will.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

In a party, the Druid most frequently serves as a Blaster, Healer, and Support Caster. Depending on your build, you can easily also serve as a Defender, Face, Scout, and Striker. However, this broad skillset means that the class can be complicated to build and play, and you’ll need to plan out your build well in advance.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Subclasses – Druidic Orders<\/h2>\n\n\n

                    Your subclass grants you a Trained skill, an Order Spell (which is a Focus Spell), and a Druid Class Feat. There are also a handful of Druid Class Feats which are exclusive to a specific Druidic Order. You start with one order, but you can take the Order Explorer class feat to get the benefits of multiple orders.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Animal<\/span><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

                    An Animal Companion is a great expansion to your existing capabilities, and since you can direct it with a single Action it’s a great way to get more out of your turn without giving up the ability to cast a spell. While you can do without Heal Animal (which means Order Explorer is an option), it’s a great way to keep your Animal Companion combat-ready without consuming other limited resources so if you plan to include Animal Order it should probably be your first choice.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            Ability Scores<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

                            Conventional<\/h3>\n\n\n

                            Most druid only need Wisdom to fuel their spells and class features, then enough Dexterity and Constitution to keep them alive.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            Str<\/span>: Dump. Druids don’t get good enough armor\n\tor weapon proficiencies to be effective in melee, so fighting in melee is very\n\tdangerous.<\/p>\n

                            Dex<\/span>: You need at least 14 if you want to\n\twear hide armor or 16 if you want to wear studded leather. At low levels, you\n\tmight choose to use a crossbow or other ranged weapon in combat, though I\n\tdon’t recommend doing so.<\/p>\n

                            Con<\/span>: Hit points are always important.<\/p>\n

                            Int<\/span>: Only useful for skills, but your\n\tability score needs are otherwise limited, so more skills are a good way to\n\tbenefit from Ability Boosts which you won’t otherwise use.<\/p>\n

                            Wis<\/span>: Your Key Ability Score.<\/p>\n

                            Cha<\/span>: Most druids can dump Charisma, but if\n\tyou plan to play your party’s Face or to pursue the Leaf Order you’ll benefit\n\tfrom a little bit of Charisma.<\/p>\n\n\n

                            Wild Order<\/h3>\n\n\n

                            This set of ability scores assumes that you’ll be investing heavily in Wild Shape and its associated feats. If you don’t plan to take Form Control, you can dump Strength.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            Str<\/span>: You need 14 for Form Control at level\n\t4, and you need 18 for Perfect Form Control at 15th level, and you’ll get\n\tthree sets of ability boosts in between those two points. If you invest\n\theavily in Strength, you may be able to beat the Attack Bonus provided by Wild\n\tShape spells, but that’s only likely to happen if you invest heavily in\n\tHandwraps of Mighty Blows and if you’re using Form Control to extend Wild\n\tShape’s duration at the expense of casting Wild Shape 2 spell levels lower\n\tthan nromal.<\/p>\n

                            Dex<\/span>: If you’re in Wild Shape, your\n\tDexterity doesn’t matter except for Acrobatics checks. You should still get at\n\tleast 14 to fill out hide armor in case you get attacked before you can Wild\n\tShape.<\/p>\n

                            Con<\/span>: Wild Shape will give you some temporary\n\thit points, but they won’t last. You need as many hit points as you can get.\n\tExpect to start with 16 and boost it at every possible opportunity, then add\n\tToughness.<\/p>\n

                            Int<\/span>: More skills never hurt, but otherwise\n\tIntelligence doesn’t offer the Druid much.<\/p>\n

                            Wis<\/span>: When Wild Shape runs out or when\n\tturning into an animal can’t solve a problem, you still need to be a druid.<\/p>\n

                            Cha<\/span>: You get Intimidation for free, but\n\tyou’re not better at being a face than any other character. There are some\n\toptions which seem to imply that Wild Shape builds should work well with\n\tIntimidation, but the inability to speak or activate magic items while\n\tpolymorphed makes it exceptionally hard to do so.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                            Ancestries<\/h2>\n\n\n

                            Wisdom increases are obviously crucial, but otherwise you have plenty of room to consider other Ancestry traits. Wild Order druids who plan to rely on Wild Shape should also look for a Consitution increase and high racial hit points.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            Catfolk<\/span>: The Wisdom Flaw is fixable thanks\n\tto the Optional Flaw rules, but there’s not much here that’s worth the effort.\n\tCat’s Luck is good but not essential, and most of the Catfolk’s other feat\n\toptions won’t help the Druid.<\/p>\n

                            Dwarf<\/span>: Constitution and Wisdom boosts, and a\n\tCharisma flaw. Charisma is the druid’s dump stat, so this lines up perfectly.\n\tFree Ability Boosts are always nice, but honestly you don’t need it so use it\n\thowever you like. The Dwarf gets the most racial hit points, and Dwarven\n\tStoutness at level 9 adds more hit points on top of everything else (including\n\tToughness). The Forge Dwarf and Strong-Blooded Dwarf add damage resistance\n\twhich can add even more to the Dwarf’s excellent durability. This is a great\n\toption for any druid, but it’s absolutely fantastic for Wild Shape builds.<\/p>\n

                            Elf<\/span>: The ability scores can work for most\n\tdruids, but there’s very little in the Elf’s Ancestry Feats which helps the\n\tDruid.<\/p>\n

                            Gnome<\/span>: While the Gnome’s ability scores\n\taren’t great on their own, the Gnome otherwise has a lot to offer, especially\n\tto druids focused on spellcasting. Use the Voluntary Flaw rules and you’ll\n\thave two Free Ability Boosts to put into Wisdom and one other Ability Score.\n\tSeveral of the Gnome Heritages have interesting benefits, and perhaps the most\n\tinteresting is the ability to get an additional cantrip from any of the four\n\tpublished spell lists. Keep in mind that these are “Innate Spells”, so they’re\n\tCharisma-based. I recommend sticking to options that don’t depend on your\n\tspellcasting ability score because your Charisma will lag behind your Wisdom.\n\tThis means no attack options, but options like Guidance and Prestidigitation\n\tare great. You can then get another cantrip from Fey World Magic and a\n\tfamiliar from Animal Accomplice. You can also take Gnome Weapon Familiarity to\n\tmake the Gnome Hooked Hammer a Simple weapon for you, but I don’t recommend it\n\tbecause druids are just awful at using weapons.<\/p>\n

                            Goblin<\/span>: Using the Voluntary Flaw rules, you\n\tcan put both additional Ability Flaws into Charisma (or split them between\n\tStrength and Intelligence) and both free Ability Boosts into Wisdom, giving\n\tyou a final result of +2 Dex, +2 Wis, -2 Cha. Once you’ve dealt with that\n\tmess, the Goblin’s Ancestry Feats are very good. Burn It! is an absolute must\n\tsince the Primal Spell List includes numerous options for fire damage, and\n\tGoblin Scuttle can help you get out of melee combat if you’re not built for\n\tmelee.<\/p>\n

                            Halfling<\/span>: The Halfling’s Ability Boosts work\n\tgreat, and if you use the Voluntary Flaw rules you can even manage a Strength\n\tincrease to help support a Wild Shape build without too much trouble. Staple\n\tHalfling Ancestry Feat options like Halfling Luck are always welcome, and you\n\tcan use Cultural Adaptability to borrow great options like Fey World Magic\n\tfrom the Gnome or Burn It! from the Goblin.<\/p>\n

                            Human<\/span>: Two Flexible Ability Boosts, and\n\tAmbitious Nature is the only way to get one of the Druid’s 1st-level metamagic\n\tfeats at level 1, and at high levels options like Multitalented add a lot of\n\tuseful things. You have several great Heritage options, too. For example:\n\tHalf-Orcs can make use of orc Ferocity to give them an emergency survival\n\toption for Wild Shape builds.<\/p>\n

                            Kobold<\/span>: Put your Free Boost into Wisdom and\n\tyou’re ready to build a back-line sorcerer. Kobold Breath can be a helpful\n\treplacement for offensive cantrips, especially since it deals area damage, and\n\tthe Kobold’s other feats can get you some Innate Spellcasting to complement\n\tyour regular spellcasting. Unfortunately, the Innate Spellcasting is Arcane\n\tand Charisma-based, so try to stick to utility options and buffs.<\/p>\n

                            Orc<\/span>: The Orc is an odd choice for the\n\tDruid, but it can work for the Wild Order Druid. The Druid doesn’t have a\n\tbuilt-in way to make unarmed strikes viable outside of Wild Shape, but the Orc\n\tdoes. On top of that, Bloody Blows appears to work while in Wild Shape. And,\n\tof course, Orc Ferocity is great for anyone expecting to be on the front lines\n\tin melee.<\/p>\n

                            Ratfolk<\/span>: The boosts and flaws work fine,\n\tbut the Ratfolk offers nothing else that appeals to the Druid.<\/p>\n

                            Tengu<\/span>: Two boosts and no flaws, but the\n\tTengu’s Ancestry Feats offer nothing that appeals to the Druid.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                              Skills and Skill Feats<\/h2>\n\n\n

                              You get Skill Increases at 3rd and 5th level to raise skills to Expert, increases at 7th, 9th, 11th, and 13th level to raise skills to Master, and increases at 15th, 17th, and 19th level to raise skills to Legendary. That means that you can maximize at most three skills, and the rest of your skills might not advance beyond Trained.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                              You get Skill Feats at even-numbered levels, giving you a total of 10 Skill Feats (and maybe another from your Background) by 20th level. Generally, you want to invest these feats in the same skills which you are choosing to maximize, though in some cases you may want to grab feats from skills which don’t require that you be more than Trained.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
