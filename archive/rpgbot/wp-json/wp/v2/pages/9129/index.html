Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

Grappling has been a staple tactic of clever players since 5th edition’s release, and has existed for decades in prior editions of Dungeons and Dragons.                   Grappling<\/h3>\n\n\n
                  \n

                  When you want to grab a creature or wrestle with it, you can use the Attack\n\t\taction to make a special melee attack, a grapple.<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                  This tells us some important things:<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n
                  • Grappling uses the “Attack” action, and is not its own action<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                  • It is a “special melee attack” which means that it’s done in melee (so you\n\t\tcan’t grapple from 30 feet away), and since it’s an attack it interacts with\n\t\tother rules that care about attacks, such as the Barbarian’s Rage and the\n\t\tInvisibility spell.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                  • Because grappling is done with the Attack action, you can’t grapple as an\n\t\tOpportunity Attack.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n\n
                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                    \n

                    If you\u2019re able to make multiple attacks with the Attack action, this attack\n\t\treplaces one of them.<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    Very important for martial characters likely to rely on grappling, this means\n\tthat if you have Extra Attack you can grapple and still make other attacks\n\tusing the same Attack action.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n

                    The target of your grapple must be no more than one size larger than\n\t\tyou…<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    This part is easy to overlook, but extremely important. A small creature can\n\tonly grapple creatures of up to medium size, so your gnome barbarian is going\n\tto struggle against big chunks of the Monster Manual. As a result, grapple\n\tbuilds should expect to include a medium race.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                    \n

                    …and must be within your reach. <\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    Note that this is your character’s reach, not the reach of their weapon. You\n\tcan’t grapple using a whip or a glaive or whatever.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n

                    Using at least one free hand…<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    Extremely important. If you typically fight with a “sword and board”\n\tarrangement (meaning a weapon and a shield), grappling is difficult. However,\n\tif you fight unarmed to even using a two-handed weapon, you have a free hand\n\tmost of the time with which you can attempt a grapple.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Strangely, after you start the grapple, there’s no requirement to keep that\n\thand free. You’re free to use that hand to go back to attacking with a\n\ttwo-handed weapon or to grapple a different creature.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    This also means that creatures without hands can’t initiate a grapple unless\n\tthey have some other way to do so. For example: a giant toad has no hands, but\n\tif they hit a creature with their bite, the target is grappled. This notably\n\tdoesn’t require a check and doesn’t care about size; it simply happens.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                    \n

                    …you try to seize the target by making a grapple check instead of an\n\t\tattack roll: a Strength (Athletics) check…<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    Instead of an attack roll, you make an ability check to initiate a grapple.\n\tThis tells us that, at the very least, if you want to grapple you need decent\n\tStrength and proficiency in Athletics. This means that you’re unlikely to see\n\tmany wizards grappling, but barbarians, fighters, and paladins should do\n\tfine.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n

                    …contested by the target\u2019s Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics)\n\t\tcheck (the target chooses the ability to use).<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    This is an important point for creatures attempting to resist grapples. Not\n\tevery creature is immensely strong, but many are nimble, wiggly, or otherwise\n\tdifficult to restrain, and either set of capabilities can help you get out of\n\ta grapple.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Offensively, this means that grapplers will want to pick targets who are bad\n\tat both Acrobatics and Athletics. Grappling ogres or quicklings may be hard,\n\tbut grappling wizards is typically easy. Defensively, this means that melee\n\tbuilds may want a way to escape grapples, typically by being proficient in\n\teither Acrobatics or Athletics depending on which of their Strength or\n\tDexterity is higher.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                    \n

                    If you succeed, you subject the target to the grappled condition. The\n\t\tcondition specifies the things that end it…<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    The Grappled condition is why you want to start a grapple. It’s a little bit\n\tcomplicated, so we’ll examine the Grappled Condition below.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n

                    and you can release the target whenever you like (no action required).<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    “Whenever you like” seems to indicate that it doesn’t need to be on your\n\tturn, especially since it doesn’t require an action. If you’re unable to take\n\tactions for some reason, but that reason doesn’t also break the grapple, it\n\tlooks like you can still choose to release the target.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                    Escaping a Grapple<\/h4>\n\n\n

                    Once a creature is grappled, they’ll frequently want to stop being grappled.\n\tYou can still fight in melee while grappled without too much trouble, but not\n\tevery creature likes to stand still and kick things in the shins until their\n\thit points drop to 0, so sometimes you need to escape a grapple.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n

                    A grappled creature can use its action to escape.<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    Escaping a grapple takes an Action. Considering that it takes an attack\n\t(which might be one of several taken with the Attack action, or might just\n\tgrapple the target as a secondary effect) to start a grapple, the action\n\teconomy is heavily in the attacker’s favor.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    That imbalance in the action economy is one of the reasons why players like\n\tto grapple offensively, but also why players try really hard to get out of\n\tgrapples by any other means possible. See Countering Grapples, below.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n

                    To do so, it must succeed on a Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity\n\t\t(Acrobatics) check contested by your Strength (Athletics) check.<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    These are the same ability checks used to initiate and to resist a grapple.\n\tThat consistency in the rules is nice because it’s to remember what you need\n\tto do.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                    The Grappled Condition<\/h3>\n\n\n

                    You did it! You grappled something! Or something grappled you! I don’t know,\n\tI’m not your DM. Either way, you need to know what that means for whoever is\n\tgrappled.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n

                    A grappled creature’s speed becomes 0, and it can’t benefit from any bonus\n\t\tto its speed.<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    Reducing a creature’s speed to 0 is the entire point of grappling them. The\n\tprimary benefit is that they can’t run away, but importantly if your speed is\n\t0 you can’t do anything<\/em> which requires expending some portion of your\n\tmove speed. For example: you can’t stand up (see Shove, below) and you can’t\n\tmount or unmount a horse.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    The target also can’t benefit from bonuses to speed, so things like\n\tLongstrider or Haste don’t magically let you walk out of a grapple.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Having a creature’s speed reduced to 0 is already a significant benefit if\n\tyou go no further. Having allies place ongoing damage effects like Create\n\tBonfire or Cloud of Daggers is easy damage output at minimal cost, making your\n\twhole party more efficient and effective.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                    \n

                    The condition ends if the grappler is incapacitated (see the\n\t\tcondition).<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    Incapacitated is an important condition, and in addition to being a condition\n\ton its own it’s also applied by other conditions like paralyzed and\n\tunconscious. So you can end a grapple by paralyzing the grappler, putting them\n\tto sleep, and a whole bunch of other things.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n

                    The condition also ends if an effect removes the grappled creature from the\n\t\treach of the grappler or grappling effect, such as when a creature is hurled\n\t\taway by the thunderwave<\/a> spell.<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    Getting the grappler and the grappled creature apart is typically the easiest\n\tand most efficient way to break a grapple. Thunderwave is an excellent and\n\teasily-understood example, but you can also use teleportation, or you can just\n\tShove the grappler away.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    The Bugbear race has a weird edge case here: The long-limbed trait extends\n\tyour reach for melee attacks on your turn by 5 feet. This means that you could\n\tinitiate a grapple (grapple is a melee attack), but if the grappled creature\n\tis then outside of your reach after the attack, the grapple immediately\n\tends.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                    Moving a Grappled Creature<\/h4>\n\n\n

                    While a grappled creature has its speed reduced to 0, the creature who\n\tinitiated the grapple has no such problem. Sometimes, as a grappler, you may\n\twant to move the grappled creature somewhere else, such as by dragging them\n\tinto a wall of fire or tossing them into a pit. This is frequently a more\n\tefficient way to reposition a creature than by using Shove, so understanding\n\thow it works is very helpful for front-line martial characters even if you\n\tdon’t grapple frequently.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                    \n

                    When you move, you can drag or carry the grappled creature with you, but\n\t\tyour speed is halved…<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    Useful, but vague.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    There’s no clear explanation about what “drag” means here, so we’re left to\n\tfill in the meaning using plain English. “Drag” means to pull, so you can’t\n\tpush the target away by grappling them (though maybe you could by carrying\n\tthem?). There’s also no rules about where the creature is positioned, so\n\tyou’ll need to work it out with your DM on a case-by-case basis.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Carrying a grappled creature is even less clear. If you’re a human grappling\n\tan ogre, how are you supposed to carry them? Can you pick them up, carry them\n\tto a window, then drop them out of it? I honestly have no idea.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    As a DM, I try to interpret these rules as simply as possible. When you move\n\twhile grappling a creature, any creatures remain in the same position relative\n\tto your space, so they basically “float” in space as you move.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    These rules also don’t seem to care how many creatures you have grappled, so\n\tif you grapple numerous foes (or any creature really, since you can\n\ttechnically grapple allies) you’re free to drag them all around at the same\n\ttime.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    The rules also don’t care about what sort of movement you use, and Jeremy\n\tCrawford has tweeted that\n\tit works with movement of any sort<\/a>. This means that you could grapple creatures then fly into the air, drag\n\tthem up walls with a climb speed or spider climb, or if you’re a monk\n\tpotentially even run up a wall while grappling a creature. This both\n\tdiscourages creatures from breaking the grapple and gives you an easy source\n\tof additional damage output when you inevitably decide to drop them.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Since I get this question frequently: These rules\n\tdon’t care about a creature’s actual weight<\/a>, so the carrying capacity rules do not matter here. The Monster Manual\n\tdoesn’t specify creatures’ weights, and your options in a grapple shouldn’t\n\thinge on whether or not the target has had a heavy lunch.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                    \n

                    unless the creature is two or more sizes smaller than you.<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

                    A medium creature could grapple a tiny creature such as a pixie or a house\n\tcat and drag them around without any impact on your speed. If you become\n\tlarge, you can do the same for small creatures like halflings.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Shove<\/h3>\n\n\n

                    While it’s not part of the Grapple rules, the rules for Shoving a Creature\n\tare frequently used in combination with grappling. Shoving a Creature allows\n\tyou to shove a creature prone. When combined with the Grappled condition, this\n\tprevents creatures from standing (you can’t expend movement when your speed is\n\t0, and standing requires expending half of your movement).<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    This is frequently referred to as the grapple+shove combo, and it’s a\n\tpowerful tactic for martial characters. Forcing enemies to remain prone is an\n\teasy way to get Advantage on attacks, and a character optimized for grappling\n\talready has everything that they need to Shove, too. This is typically the\n\tmost useful way to consistently benefit from grappling in combat, so I’ll\n\trefer to the grapple+shove combo repeatedly throughout this article.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Note that your allies will also be affected by you shoving creatures prone.\n\tOther melee attackers will benefit, while allies relying on ranged attack\n\trolls (archers, warlocks, etc.) will suffer Disadvantage to hit the target. If\n\tyour ranged allies complain about you knocking foes prone, encourage them to\n\tlearn spells like Create Bonfire or to switch targets.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                    Two-Weapon Fighting<\/h3>\n\n\n

                    While this is an edge case, you can<\/em> grapple or shove in the same\n\tturn that you use Two-Weapon Fighting, provided that you have Extra Attack. To\n\tperform two-weapon fighting, you need to take the Attack action and attack\n\twith a qualifying weapon. However, you only need to make that attack once, so\n\tif you have Extra Attacks you could spend any additional attacks to grapple\n\tand\/or shove (remember that they’re “special melee attacks”). However, since\n\tyou need a free hand to grapple you may need to either throw or drop one of\n\tyour weapons.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                    Climbing a Creature<\/h3>\n\n\n

                    An optional rule presented in the Dungeon Master’s Guide, the Climbing a\n\tCreature rule allows creatures to use rules similar to Grapple and Shove to\n\tclimb the creature, granting your Advantage on attacks against the creature\n\tthat you’re climbing. While this optional rule is far from perfect, it’s a\n\tgreat way for grapple builds to remain relevant when facing creatures that are\n\ttoo large to grapple, potentially even allowing small races to produce\n\tsuccessful grapple builds.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Optimizing For Grapples<\/h2>\n\n\n

                    Optimizing for grappling, including the grapple+shove combo, isn’t especially\n\tdifficult, but there’s a lot to consider, and the further you go the more\n\teffective you’ll be.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    The Fundamentals<\/h3>\n\n\n

                    I recommend addressing the following points in this order. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                      \n
                    1. Strength.<\/strong> A crucial part of the formula, especially at low\n\t\tlevels before your Proficiency Bonus scales. An optimized grappler will\n\t\ttypically start with a +3 Strength modifier.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    2. Proficiency.<\/strong> Proficiency and Expertise in Athletics are\n\t\tthe most mathematically impactful thing that you can do to improve your\n\t\tgrappling. If you can get Expertise, such as from levels in rogue or from\n\t\tthe Skill Expert feat, that will have a huge impact, especially as your\n\t\tProficiency Bonus scales with level.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    3. Extra Attack.<\/strong> The ability to make multiple attacks in the\n\t\tsame turn means that you can grapple and shove in the same turn, which gets\n\t\tthe grapple+shove combo working in one turn rather than two, doubling your\n\t\teffectiveness.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    4. Size.<\/strong> You can only grapple creatures up to one size larger\n\t\tthan you, so if you want to grapple everything you meet, you’ll want to be\n\t\tas large as possible. The Climbing a Creature optional rule can make this\n\t\tpoint less crucial, but only if you’re okay with whatever you’re grappling\n\t\twalking around while you climb all over it.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    5. Advantage.<\/strong> Advantage works out to slightly more than +3,\n\t\tbut your Strength modifier can almost match that right at level 1, and your\n\t\tProficiency Bonus will scale with level, especially if you have Expertise\n\t\tand are doubling your Proficiency Bonus, which is why those points are more\n\t\tcrucial.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    6. Imposing Disadvantage.<\/strong> Typically harder than finding a way\n\t\tto get Advantage, imposing Disadvantage on your target is just as impactful.\n\t\tSpells like Hex can provide consistent, easy ways to accomplish this.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    7. Other Stuff.<\/strong> Other sources of bonuses for yourself and\n\t\tpenalties for your targets are less common and often less permanent, but\n\t\tthey can still have a useful benefit to improve your grappling. Examples\n\t\tinclude things like Guidance, the Grappler feat, and Fighting Style\n\t\t(Unarmed).<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n\n\n\n

                      I consider 1 through 4 the essentials, but more is always better. Other\n\tpeople may disagree with me regarding what should be considered “essential”,\n\tand that’s fine. I consider 1-4 essential because they are typically permanent\n\tparts of your character, while Advantage\/Disadvantage are frequently\n\tcircumstantial, temporary, or constrained by limited resources like daily uses\n\tof Rage or spell slots.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                        Classes<\/h4>\n\n\n

                        Your class is the only thing that can give you Extra Attack, which makes your\n\tclass extremely important.
