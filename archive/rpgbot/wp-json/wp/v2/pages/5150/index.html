Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

The Bard is a versatile spellcasting class that can thrive in a variety of\n\troles. Their signature role is as a Support character, but they can also serve\n\tas a Healer, Librarian, Scout, and Utility caster depending on your build.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Successful bards needs to be very comfortable with managing their action\n\teconomy, tracking status conditions, and supporting the party indirectly. Many\n\tof the Bard’s most effective features are single Actions which produce\n\tshort-duration status effects that you’ll need to track constantly, and nearly\n\tnone of the Bard’s feats involve harming enemies (spellcasting being the\n\tlargest exception). This is a class that will reward a player who is\n\tcomfortable with Pathfinder 2e’s mechanics and with constantly re-calculating\n\tnumbers.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

              Bard Class Features<\/h2>\n\n\n

              Key Ability<\/strong>: Charisma. This makes the bard a natural Face,\n\tand makes it easy to capitalize on things like innate spellcasting from your\n\tAncestry.<\/p>\n\n\n\nHit Points<\/span>: 8+ hit points is typical for\n\tclasses not built to be a front-line martial character. You’ll be fine if\n\tyou’re built to fight at range, but if you want to get into melee be sure to\n\tinvest in Constitution to pad your hit points.<\/p\n>\nInitial Proficiencies<\/span>: The Bard’s\n\tproficiencies are limited. You get few weapons and you’re not very good at\n\tusing them, and defensively you’re very vulnerable. Expect to invest in\n\tDexterity and Constitution just to stay alive.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

                \n\tPerception<\/span>: Starts at Expert, and remains\n\t\tabove average as you gain levels.<\/li\n\t>\n\tSaving Throws<\/span>: The worst Fortitude saves\n\t\tin the game, and only middling Reflex saves. The Bard’s proficency in Will\n\t\tsaves is among the best available, which is great because you may not be\n\t\table to afford to invest in Wisdom. You’ll need to invest in Dexterity and\n\t\tConstitution to compensate for your other saves.<\/li\n\t>\n\tSkills<\/span>: A total of 6+ skills is above\n\t\taverage, but you’re locked into Occultism and Performance, and most bards\n\t\twill take Deception, Diplomacy, and Intimidation to capitalize on their high\n\t\tCharisma, so you may find yourself with less flexibility than you’d like\n\t\tunless you invest in Intelligence for extra skills.<\/li\n\t>\n\tAttacks<\/span>: Simple weapons and a handful of\n\t\tmartial weapons. Your proficiency advances at the same rate as the Wizard,\n\t\twhich should tell you how infrequently bards are expected to use weapons.\n\t\tDon’t expect to use weapons extensively, but you might make a Strike every\n\t\tfew turns when you have a spare Action, so a shortbow can be a useful\n\t\toption, especially at low levels where your comparably poor proficiency is\n\t\tless significant and you have fewer spells to throw around.<\/li\n\t>\n\tDefenses<\/span>: Light armor, and your\n\t\tproficiency advances at the slowest rate. Your AC is going to be bad, which\n\t\tis a problem on top of your 8+ hit points.<\/li\n\t>\n\tClass DC<\/strong>: Bard don’t get a class DC. That’s not much of a\n\t\tproblem unless you’re trying to capitalize on Critical Specialization\n\t\tEffects, but even then many of them don’t care about your Class DC, and you\n\t\tdon’t get access to them from the Bard until level 11.<\/li\n\t>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

                  \n\tHeightened Spells<\/span>: Heightening spells is\n\t\tan important mechanic in Pathfinder 2e. Many spells scale with spell level,\n\t\tremaining effective long past their base spell level. Since bards use a\n\t\tSpell Repertoire, you need to learn spells at multiple levels to heighten\n\t\tthem in most cases, though Signature Spells are an exception.<\/li\n\t>\n\tCantrips<\/span>: Fantastic every time, on any\n\t\tcharacter, in any amount.                   Subclasses – Muses<\/h2>\n\n\n

                  Each Muse emphasizes a specific part of the Bard’s capabilities, offering new\n\tfeats to support that part of the class. While technically you’re not\n\tobligated to take any of those feats (beyond the one which you recieve at 1st\n\tlevel), many of the Bard’s best class feats are Muse-specific.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                  You can gain access to feats from other Muses with the Multifarious Muse\n\tfeat, broadening your class feat options. However, adding more Muse types will\n\thave diminishing returns because you have a limited number of class feat\n\tslots. You simply can’t do everything in one build, so you’ll need to pick and\n\tchoose.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                  \n\tEnigma<\/a\n\t>\n\tCRB<\/a><\/sup><\/h3\n>\n\n\n\n

                  The Enigma is the master of knowing stuff about stuff. You don’t need much\n\tIntelligence of Wisdom to back up this knowledge, but you’ll still lean\n\theavily on Recall Knowledge. Amazingly, this knowledge comes at a surprisingly\n\tlow Skill Increase\/Feat cost, but it also doesn’t give you much other than\n\tknowledge. You’ll need to lean on general Bard Class Feats or combine this\n\twith another Muse to find a niche for yourself in the party.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n\tFeat: Bardic Lore<\/span\n\t\t> (CRB<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t\t>: See below, under class feats.<\/li\n\t>\n\tSpell: True Strike<\/span\n\t\t> (CRB<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t\t>: The best thing that I can say about True Strike is that it costs one\n\t\tAction so you can easily fit a Composition Cantrip, True Strike, and a\n\t\tsingle Strike into the same turn. Unfortunately, your Strikes are going to\n\t\tbe fairly pointless beyond low levels, and at low levels you have very spell\n\t\tslots so it may be hard to justify spending one on True Strike.<\/li\n\t>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

                    The Maestro is essentially the generic bard. It doesn’t emphasize any\n\tspecific part of the Bard’s capabilities, but it has a good mix of options\n\tthat run across Compositions, regular spellcasting, and skills.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    Unless you want to focus solely on a single Muse, starting with Maestro and\n\tthen taking Multifarious Muse to add a second Muse type is a great option. The\n\tMaestro’s feats can complement those of your second Muse, allowing you to\n\tbuild something better than a single Muse could produce on its own.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                      \n\tFeat: Lingering Composition<\/span\n\t\t> (CRB<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t\t>: See below, under class feats.<\/li\n\t>\n\tSpell: Soothe<\/span\n\t\t> (CRB<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t\t>: The Occult spell list doesn’t get Heal, so Soothe is the next-best\n\t\tthing.<\/li\n\t>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

                      The Polymath emphasizes two thing: Skills and spellcasting. But it does so in\n\ta weird way. The Polymath’s skill-related feats help the Bard succeed with\n\tskills in which the Bard hasn’t spent resources, so if you want to play a\n\thigh-skill character with poor Intelligence, the Polymath is great. The\n\tPolymath’s spellcasting adds a spellbook and unusually easy options to adjust\n\tyour Spell Repertoire, allowing you much more flexibility with your spells\n\tthan most Spell Repertoire users can achieve.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                        \n\tFeat: Versatile Performance<\/span\n\t\t> (CRB<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t\t>: See below, under class feats.<\/li\n\t>\n\tSpell: Unseen Servant<\/span\n\t\t> (CRB<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t\t>: Useful outside of combat, but spells which require you to Sustain them\n\t\tare hard for the Bard because you’re already stretching your Actions to use\n\t\tboth Composition Cantrips and your regular spellcasting.<\/li\n\t>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

                          Ability Scores<\/h2>\n\n\n

                          Bards only really need Charisma, but with poor defenses you want to invest\n\tsome boosts in Dexterity and Constitution to keep yourself alive. You may want\n\tmore Intelligence for skills and Wisdom for Perception and Will saves. That\n\tleaves Strength as your only dump stat option, but beyond dumping Strength and\n\tmaximizing Charisma, you have a lot of flexibility depending on your\n\tpriorities for your character. Remember that wherever you put your boosts will\n\tbe a trade. You can’t be great at everything at the same time.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\nStr<\/span>: Dump. All that you get is carrying\n\tcapacity and weapon damage. You don’t need either enough to invest boosts.<\/p\n>\nDex<\/span>: AC and Reflex saves, and if you plan\n\tto use weapons they’ll almost certainly be either Finesse weapons or ranged\n\tweapons. You don’t necessarily need a ton, but you shouldn’t totally ignore\n\tDexterity.<\/p\n>\nCon<\/span>: 8+ hit points and poor Fortitude saves\n\tis a recipe for a dead character.<\/p\n>\nInt<\/span>: More skills. You may be able to get\n\tby on Untrained Improvisation and Bardic Lore, which would allow you to dump\n\tIntelligence while still being reasonably effective at things that normally\n\trequire it.<\/p\n>\n

                          Ancestries<\/h2>\n\n\n

                          Look for boosts that match the ability scores you want. Since there is so\n\tmuch room for the bard to tweak their ability scores to suit the build you\n\twant, your Ancestry’s default boosts can be far more important than they are\n\tfor other classes. An ancestry with Dex\/Con\/Free boosts is ideal, but almost\n\tanything can work depending on what you’re trying to build.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                          Beyond your Ability Boosts, look for things which complement the Bard’s\n\tfeatures or fill in gaps. Additional defenses like damage resistance are\n\tgreat, as are high ancestry hit points. The\n\tboosts work great, and if you’re fine staying behind your allies the low hp\n\tand Constitution Flaw are manageable. Ancestral Linguistics and the\n\tOtherworldly Magic feat chain are great choices for Ancestry Feats, though the\n\tElf’s Innate Spellcasting feats are all Arcane, so you’ll want to stick to\n\tutility options unless you can advance your proficiency with arcane spells\n\tsomehow. Goblin Song is an obvious choice,\n\tbut there aren’t many other feat options that appeal to the Bard.<\/p\n>\nHalfling<\/a\n\t> (CRB<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t>: Good boosts\/flaws, but no Ancestry Feats which directly complement the\n\tBard. Options like Cringe\n\tand Grovel offer other interesting options with low Action costs that can let\n\tyou expand your impact far beyond what you can do with spells.<\/p\n>\nOrc<\/a\n\t> (APG<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t>: Bad ability boosts, and the Orc’s Ancestry Feats are almost all about being\n\tin melee combat.<\/p\n>\nRatfolk<\/a\n\t> (APG<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t>: Good boosts\/flaws, but the Ratfolk’s Ancestry Feats do absolutely nothing\n\tuseful for the Bard.<\/p\n>\nTengu<\/a\n\t> (APG<\/a>)<\/sup\n\t>: Workable boosts and now flaws, but Squawk is the only feat which looks\n\tappealing.                             Skills and Skill Feats<\/h2>\n\n\n

                            You get Skill Increases at 3rd and 5th level to raise skills to Expert, increases at 7th, 9th, 11th, and 13th level to raise skills to Master, and increases at 15th, 17th, and 19th level to raise skills to Legendary. That means that you can maximize at most three skills, and the rest of your skills might not advance beyond Trained.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            You get Skill Feats at even-numbered levels, giving you a total of 10 Skill Feats (and maybe another from your Background) by 20th level. Generally, you want to invest these feats in the same skills which you are choosing to maximize, though in some cases you may want to grab feats from skills which don’t require that you be more than Trained.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
