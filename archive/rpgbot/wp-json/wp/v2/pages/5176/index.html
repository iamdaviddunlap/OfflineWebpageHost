Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

The Wizard has been my favorite class since 3rd edition Dungeons and Dragons.\n\tPowerful, versatile, and thematically interesting, the Wizard goes out into\n\tthe world to test their wits and their knowledge against whatever challenge\n\tthe multiverse has to offer.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

The Wizard is the iconic arcane spellcaster. With a broad and diverse spell list, the Wizard can solve nearly any problem magically. In a party, your role is defined by the spells you employ. You can serve as a Blaster with area damage spells like fireball, a Defender by summoning creatures to stand between your party and your enemies, a Face by enchanting other creatures, a Scholar with your vast Intelligence and ample skills, a Scout with divination spells and stealth options like Invisibility, a Striker with single-target spells like Disintegrate and Power Word Kill, a Support caster with a wide range of buffs, debuffs, and area control spells, and a Utility caster with access to all manner of spells for solving the world’s mundane problems with magic.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

In truth, there is very little that the Wizard can’t do, and while it’s not\n\tthe uber-class that it was in previous editions, the Wizard is still a\n\tprofoundly capable and versatile class. However, with great versatility comes\n\tgreat complexity. Playing a wizard involves a lot of planning, tracking, and\n\tmanagement. You can keep the class simple by playing an Evoker and solving\n\tyour problems by blowing them up, but many wizards will have a large suite of\n\tspecialized tools and will need to choose from a long list of options to\n\temploy in any given situation. If you’re afraid of “analysis paralysis”,\n\tconsider other class options like the Sorcerer.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

              Wizard Class Features<\/h2>\n\n\n

              Key Ability<\/strong>: Intelligence. Like any spellcaster it’s used for your Spell Attacks and for your Spell DCs. Those are two of your most important stats, so keep your Intelligence as high as you can get it. Intelligence also grants you additional Trained skills at 1st level, allowing you to easily invest in numerous skills, including Lore options which other classes often need to ignore in favor of more important skills.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Hit Points<\/span>: 6+Con hit points. Fortunately, you\n\thave plenty of room to boost your ability scores so your Constitution bonus\n\tcan compensate.<\/p>\n

              Initial Proficiencies<\/span>: The Wizard’s\n\tproficiencies are terrible. Your high Intelligence will give you an abundance\n\tof Trained skills, but that’s the height of the Wizard’s proficiencies in\n\tanything except spellcasting.<\/p>\n

                \n\t
              • Perception<\/span>: Trained at 1st level, and it\n\t\tmaxes out at Expert at 11th level. Expect to rely on allies for Perception\n\t\tchecks, and strongly consider Incredible Initiative if you don’t want to go\n\t\tlate in the turn order in every fight.<\/li>\n\t
              • Saving Throws<\/span>: Only one good saving throw.\n\t\tWill is a great option, but only one good saving throw is still a serious\n\t\thandicap, and it never improves past Master. Your other saving throws will\n\t\talso increase to expert, but no further. You can take the Canny Acumen feat,\n\t\tbut that won’t get you past Expert until 17th level, so you’ll spend most of\n\t\tyour career with absolutely dismal saving throws compared to your\n\t\tallies.<\/li>\n\t
              • Skills<\/span>: Arcana, plus 2+int Trained skills\n\t\tat 1st level, for a total of 3+. That’s standard, and with 18 Intelligence\n\t\tat 1st level you’ll have plenty of trained skills. You should get more than\n\t\tanyone except the Alchemist and the Rogue.<\/li>\n\t
              • Attacks<\/span>: 5 weapons plus unarmed strikes. A\n\t\tdagger is your best weapon option. Don’t expect to use any weapon except in\n\t\tthe most unusual circumstances.<\/li>\n\t
              • Defenses<\/span>: No armor proficiencies, and your\n\t\tproficiency in Unarmored Defense never increases past Expert. Expect to rely\n\t\ton spells like Mystic Armor, and be sure that you have nice sturdy friends to\n\t\thide behind. The Wizard’s saves are also terrible.<\/li>\n\t
              • Spells<\/strong>: Standard progression for full spellcasters.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n\n
                <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                Arcane Spellcasting<\/strong>: The Wizard uses the Arcane spell list,\n\tand they are prepared spellcasters so you need to prepare a spell in each of\n\tyour spell slots during your Daily Preparations.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                  \n\t
                • Heightened Spells<\/strong>: Heightening spells is an important\n\t\tmechanic in Pathfinder 2e. Important spells like Mage Armor scale with spell\n\t\tlevel, allowing them to stay relevant long after you learned them. Since\n\t\twizards don’t use a Spell Repertoire, you only need to learn a spell once,\n\t\tthen you can prepare it at any level that you can cast.<\/li>\n\t
                • Cantrips<\/span>: Fantastic every time, on any\n\t\tcharacter, in any amount. Wizards can prepare 5 cantrips, and they start\n\t\twith 10 in their spellbook so you have lots of room to customize your\n\t\tarsenal every day.<\/li>\n\t
                • Spellbook<\/span>: Your spellbook is your most\n\t\tvaluable possession. Guard it jealously; if you lose it, you’ll be unable to\n\t\tprepare spells. Also, expect to buy several over the course of your career.\n\t\tThey have a finite number of pages, and as you gain levels your spells will\n\t\ttake up increasing numbers of pages. If you add spells to your spellbook\n\t\tbeyond the free 2 spells that you gain at each level, you’ll exacerbate this\n\t\tproblem. Fortunately, after very low levels the cost of extra spell books\n\t\twill be negligible.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

                  Arcane School<\/strong>: See “Subclasses – Arcane School”, below.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                  Arcane Bond<\/span>: Recast any one spell you\n\tprepared today. This is hugely useful, and it makes it easier to prepare a\n\tdiverse array of spells rather than preparing Fireball 8 times.<\/p>\n

                  Arcane Thesis<\/span>: Arcane thesis adds an extra\n\tmechanic to your Wizard. While this option isn’t as defining as your Arcane\n\tSchool, it’s a helpful extra ability which will remain useful at every\n\tlevel.<\/p>\n

                    \n\t
                  • Improved Familiar Attunement<\/span>CRB<\/a><\/sup>: Familiars are really good, but their capabilities are limited by their\n\t\tnumber of abilities. Normally you only get two, so this triples the number\n\t\tof abilities which you can give your familiar. You can also take the Enhance\n\t\tFamiliar feat to add another 2.<\/li>\n\t
                  • Metamagical Experimentation<\/span>CRB<\/a><\/sup>: More class feats is always great, but the level limitations on the second\n\t\tfeat means that you’ll probably end up learning a metamagic feat then\n\t\tretraining it once you can take it with Metamagical Experimentation. It also\n\t\tseems unlikely that you’ll change out your chosen metamagic feat frequently.\n\t\tChanging out metamagic feats to prepare for expected challenges doesn’t make\n\t\tas much sense as changing your spells for the day.<\/li>\n\t
                  • Spell Blending<\/span>CRB<\/a><\/sup>: This is a gamble. Higher-level spells grow dramatically in power, but\n\t\tit’s hard to say if a small number of higher-level spell slots are better\n\t\tthan the regular spell slot arrangement. If you take this option, expect to\n\t\tburn a big spell or two right at the beginning of a fight in a way that will\n\t\tdefine the outcome of the fight, then expect to fall back on cantrips.<\/li>\n\t
                  • Spell Substitution<\/span>CRB<\/a><\/sup>: If you can’t decide what to choose for your Arcane Thesis, Spell\n\t\tSubstitution is a great option. Wizards are generally stuck with the options\n\t\tthat they picked at the beginning of a day, and you can rarely predict your\n\t\tneeds with perfect accuracy. This is great for when you encounter unexpected\n\t\tobstacles and don’t have time to spend 8 hours resting in order to\n\t\tre-prepare spells.<\/li>\n\t
                  • Staff Nexus<\/span>APG<\/a><\/sup>: Before you go any further, go read the rules for Casting Spells from a\n\t\tStaff and Preparing a Staff. If you don’t know those rules, none of the\n\t\tadvice I’m about to give you will make any sense.\n\t\t

                    The Makeshift Staff starts off very weak, but grows in efficacy as you\n\t\t\tgain levels, and takes a massive jump when you upgrade it to a real magic\n\t\t\tstaff. At levels 1 and 2, basically all that it does is give you an extra\n\t\t\tcantrip per day since you still get to prepare your normal number of\n\t\t\tcantrips. You get one 1st-level spell in the staff, but you need to spend\n\t\t\ta 1st-level slot to get 1 charge so you’re effectively turning a 1st-level\n\t\t\tspell slot into a 1st-level slot, leaving you right where you started.<\/p>\n\t\t

                    Normally when you Prepare a Staff you get some free charges, but the\n\t\t\tMakeshift Staff doesn’t get that benefit (until you turn it into a real\n\t\t\tstaff), so you need to expend a spell slot to get any use out of the\n\t\t\tleveled spell in your staff. As you gain access to higher-level spell\n\t\t\tslots, you can turn them into multiple slots for repeated castings of your\n\t\t\t1st-level spell, so it’s really important that the spell which you choose\n\t\t\tis a going to remain useful throughout your career without casting the\n\t\t\tspell at a higher spell level. Choose carefully.<\/p>\n\t\t

                    When you eventually turn your Makeshift Staff into a real staff, it’s a\n\t\t\thuge upgrade. Magic staffs are available as early as level 3, but most are\n\t\t\teither level 4 or 6 at their lowest-level version. Once you upgrade, you\n\t\t\tadd your cantrip and 1st-level spell to your upgrade staff, giving you a\n\t\t\ttotally unique item. You could have a Staff of Ice that can also be used\n\t\t\tto cast Produce Flame and Magic Missile (not great examples of a powerful\n\t\t\tcombo, but a good example of the mechanic). Keep in mind that you get no\n\t\t\tspecial benefits to perform this upgrade, so you’ll need to put some Skill\n\t\t\tIncreases into Crafting.<\/p>\n\t\t

                    If you’re going to take Staff Nexus, you need to be prepared to do two\n\t\t\tthings: first, you need to like how staffs work and want to use them;\n\t\t\tsecond, you need to craft magic items (mostly your staff, but your party\n\t\t\tis inevitably going to ask you to make other stuff). If you’re okay with\n\t\t\tthat, and if you don’t mind suffering through low levels with the\n\t\t\tMakeshift Staff, you’ll do just fine.<\/p>\n\t<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Subclasses – Arcane School<\/h2>\n\n\n\n
                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Generalist<\/span><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

                    \n\tThe ability to re-cast one spell of every spell level that you can cast means\n\tthat you get as many leveled spells in total as a Specialist, but you’re more\n\tlimited in the number of individual spells that you can prepare. You get an\n\textra 1st-level Wizard feat, and more class feats are always a good thing, and\n\tyou get access to several feats like Bond Conservation and Universal\n\tVersatility.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Specialist<\/span><\/h3>\n\n\n\n

                    You choose to be a specialist for two reasons: Focus spells, and an extra\n\tspell slot at each spell level. The extra spell slot is limited to your\n\tschool, but many schools have options at every level which are good enough to\n\tprepare every day. However, because your extra spell slot is limited and you\n\tcan’t re-cast expended spells (unless you prepared duplicates), you can’t\n\tmatch the versatility of the Generalist. Requiring you to spend a Class Feat\n\tto get your second school spell seems like an unnecessary tax, so if your\n\tschool’s second school spell is bad don’t waste a feat on it.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Ability Scores<\/h2>\n\n\n

                    The Wizard’s ability scores are dead simple: You need Intelligence for your\n\tspells, and you need Dexterity, Constitution, and Wisdom for your defenses.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Str<\/span>: Dump. Wizards have no need for Strength.\n\tYou’re not carrying heavy equipment, and you’re not wearing armor.<\/p>\n

                    Dex<\/span>: AC and Reflex saves. You’ll eventually\n\twant at least 16, but don’t bother getting more than 20.<\/p>\n

                    Con<\/span>: Hit points and Fortitude saving\n\tthrows.<\/p>\n

                    Int<\/span>: Your Key Ability Score.<\/p>\n

                    Wis<\/span>: Perception and Will saves.<\/p>\n

                    Cha<\/span>: Probably a dump stat unless you want\n\tto use your numerous skills to play the party’s Face, or if you want to use\n\tDemoralize with your third action every turn.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Ancestries<\/h2>\n\n\n

                    An Intelligence boost is crucial, but since Free Ability Boosts are part of\n\tevery Ancestry that’s easy to accomplish. Bonuses to other important ability\n\tscores are helpful, but not crucial, so Ancestry Feats are often as important\n\tas an Intelligence Increase.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Catfolk<\/a>APG<\/a><\/sup>: The Boosts\/Flaws work fine, but nearly none of the Catfolk’s Ancestry Feats\n\tsupport the Wizard. Black Cat Curse is really good, but one feat at 13th level\n\tisn’t enough on its own. Consider Adopted Ancestry or a Versatile Heritage if\n\tyou’re determined to play a catfolk.<\/p>\n

                    Dwarf<\/span>: The Dwarf’s Ability Boosts work\n\tgreat if you put the Free Ability Boost into Intelligence, but Dwarf Ancestry\n\tFeats offer very little for the Wizard. Strongly consider Adopted Ancestry.<\/p>\n

                    Elf<\/span>: The Elf’s Ability Boosts are great,\n\tbut a Constitution Flaw is really problematic, so you may want to use the\n\tVoluntary Flaws rules to offset the Constitution penalty. Seer Elf gets you\n\tDetect Magic for free, and Otherwordly Magic gets you an extra Arcane cantrip\n\t(though it’s an innate spell so it’s still Charisma-based), so you can expand\n\tyour cantrip options, but the Gnome can get cantrips from other spell lists,\n\twhich dramatically expands your options.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Gnome<\/span>: Despite the lack of a fixed\n\tIntelligence increase, the Gnome works surprisingly well if you put your Free\n\tAbility Boost into Intelligence. Gnome Ancestry Feats can get you\n\tautomatically-scaling Lore skills, extra cantrips, a familiar, and a few other\n\texcellent options. Two of the Gnome’s Heritages give you additional cantrips,\n\tbut since they’re innate spells they’ll be Charisma-based, so look for utility\n\toptions rather than offensive ones, especially from other spellcasting\n\ttraditions which offer spells that the Wizard can’t normally select.<\/p>\n

                    Goblin<\/span>: With your Free Ability Boost in\n\tIntelligence, the Goblin’s ability increases look similar to the Gnome’s. Burn\n\tIt! is a great option for blasters, and you could make good use of Very Sneaky\n\tand Very Very Sneaky, but those don’t specifically supplement your\n\tcapabilities as a wizard so I’m not sure if they’re better than what you could\n\tget from Adopted Ancestry.<\/p>\n

                    Halfling<\/span>: Workable ability boosts, and\n\tHalfling Luck is good on literally every character. Cultural Adaptability can\n\tget you Burn It! from the Goblin Ancestry Feats, too. Unfortunately, none of\n\tthe heritages look especially helpful.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                    <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                    Human<\/span>: Take the Voluntary Flaws to dump\n\tStrength and Charisma, and you can do +2 to Dex, Con, and Int (or switch Dex\n\tor Con to Wis if you’d like). There isn’t anything you need from Versatile\n\tHeritage, so consider other options. If you take Half-Elf you can easily take\n\tmulticlass archetype feats for other spellcasting classes. Adapted Cantrip\n\tlooks tempting, but it’s only useful for offensive options and wizards already\n\tget the best offensive options.<\/p>\n

                    Kobold<\/a>APG<\/a><\/sup>: Despite lacking a built-in Intelligence Boost, the Kobold has a lot to\n\toffer. The Spellscale Kobold gets you a free Arcane cantrip (though remember\n\tthat it’s innate so it’s Charisma-based, so stick to utility options), Kobold\n\tBreath offers an easy offensive option similar to cantrips, and at higher\n\tlevels the Dracomancer feat chain offers some additional spellcasting.\n\tDefensively, options like Cringe and Ally’s Shelter offer ways to compensate\n\tfor your poor durability.<\/p>\n

                    Orc<\/a>APG<\/a><\/sup>: Nothing useful for the Wizard beyond the Free Ability Boost.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                      Skills and Skill Feats<\/h2>\n\n\n

                      You get Skill Increases at 3rd and 5th level to raise skills to Expert, increases at 7th, 9th, 11th, and 13th level to raise skills to Master, and increases at 15th, 17th, and 19th level to raise skills to Legendary. That means that you can maximize at most three skills, and the rest of your skills might not advance beyond Trained.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                      You get Skill Feats at even-numbered levels, giving you a total of 10 Skill Feats (and maybe another from your Background) by 20th level. Generally, you want to invest these feats in the same skills which you are choosing to maximize, though in some cases you may want to grab feats from skills which don’t require that you be more than Trained.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                        \n\t
                      • Acrobatics<\/span> (Dex): Surprisingly important\n\t\tbecause it’s used for maneuvering while flying.\n\t\t
                          \n\t\t\t
                        • Cat Fall<\/span>: Being knocked prone while\n\t\t\t\tflying is an easy way to counter flying creatures, and enemies can do it\n\t\t\t\tjust as easily to you as you can do it to them. Cat Fall will reduce the\n\t\t\t\teffective distance you’ve fallen, allowing you to take less damage from\n\t\t\t\ta fall. However, the effects of Cat Fall scale based on your Proficiency\n\t\t\t\tlevel, so it may not be worth the skill feat unless you plan to increase\n\t\t\t\tyour proficiency in Acrobatics.<\/li>\n\t\t<\/ul>\n\t<\/li>\n\t
                        • Arcana<\/span> (Int): Essential, and you get it\n\t\tfor free.\n\t\t
                            \n\t\t\t
                          • Arcane Sense<\/span>: If you can make Detect\n\t\t\t\tMagic free, you should cast it frequently any time that there’s even a\n\t\t\t\tremote chance that you’ll encounter magic. You’re going to maximize\n\t\t\t\tArcana anyway, so this will get better as you gain levels. Of course,\n\t\t\t\tDetect Magic is a Cantrip which you could get by several means, and\n\t\t\t\tnormal Cantrip scaling will be faster than waiting to become Legendary\n\t\t\t\tin Arcana.<\/li>\n\t\t\t
