            How Does Multiclassing Work?<\/h2>\n\n\n

            Multiclassing is actually pretty simple. It looks confusing and the text in\n\tthe Player’s Handbook has some gaps (which I’ll fix below), but if you\n\tunderstand the core rules of advancing a single-class character you don’t need\n\tto know much more to successfully advance a multiclassed character.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

            <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

            This page uses two terms which were used in previous editions of DnD, but\n\tdon’t exist officially in 5th edition. However, they’re useful descriptors of\n\tconcepts core to multiclassing, so I’m going to use them throughout the\n\tarticle.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

            <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
              \n
            • Character Level<\/strong>: Your characters total number of levels\n\t\tacross all of their classes. For a single-class character, your Character\n\t\tlevel is the same number as your Class Level.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
            • Class Level<\/strong>: Your number of levels in a single class. For\n\t\texample, if Joe is a Fighter 3 \/ Rogue 1, their class level in Fighter is 3\n\t\tand their class level in Rogue is 1.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n\n
              <\/div>\n\n\n\n

              Selecting a Class<\/h3>\n\n\n

              When multiclassing, typically your first step is to consider which class you want to multiclass into. Every class has things to offer, so I’ll go into more detail on suggestions later in this article. Provided that you meet the prerequisites<\/a>, you can take levels in that class the next time you would gain a level.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Keep in mind that you can’t take the same class more than once. You can’t\n\tmulticlass into a class in which you already have levels. No Rogue 1 \/ Rogue\n\t1. I see new players ask about that a lot.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

              <\/div>\n\n\n\n

              Prerequisites<\/h3>\n\n\n

              There are some prerequisites to multiclass into every class. Typically, all\n\tthat’s required is an ability score of 13 or better in one or two ability\n\tscores which the class is dependent upon. See the Customization Options\n\tchapter of the Player’s Handbook for the table of multiclass prerequisites.\n\tFor the Artificer, see the Artificer class description in Eberron: Rising from\n\tthe Last War or in Tasha’s Cauldron of Everything.<\/p>\n\n\n

              Experience Points<\/h3>\n\n\n

              Experience points are based on your character level, and that doesn’t change\n\tregardless of how many classes you have.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

              <\/div>\n\n\n\n

              Hit Points and Hit Dice<\/h3>\n\n\n

              Hit Points are additive: As you gain levels, you’ll roll for hit points (or\n\ttake the rounded average, which you should always do) and add them to your hit\n\tpoint maximum just like if you gained a level in your original class. Remember\n\tthat you use the “Hit Points at Higher Levels” entry for your class rather\n\tthan the “Hit Points at 1st Level” entry. “Hit Points at 1st Level” refers to\n\tyour character level, not your class level.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Hit Dice are similarly additive. Every time you gain a level in a class, you\n\tadd a hit die of the appropriate size to your pool of hit dice. This will\n\toften result in having multiple hit die sizes. When you spend a hit die, you\n\tcan choose which die to use. But the hit dice aren’t interchangeable: if you\n\tonly have one d12 hit die (such as from one level in Barbarian) and the rest\n\tare d6’s (such as from several levels in Wizard), you only get to use the d12\n\thit die once per day.<\/p>\n\n\n

              Proficiency Bonus<\/h3>\n\n\n

              Proficiency Bonus is based on your character level. While it’s included in\n\tevery class’s class features table, it advances based on the sum of your\n\tlevels rather than your level in a single class. If you multiclass, your\n\tProficiency Bonus is still just as good as everyone else’s.<\/p>\n\n\n

              Cantrips<\/h3>\n\n\n

              Knowing a cantrip can come from a variety of sources: your race, your class,\n\tfeats, or maybe your DM just gave it to you or something. Cantrips, like\n\tProficiency Bonus, advance based on your character level. No matter how many\n\tclasses you take, your damage-dealing cantrips will add more damage at\n\tcharacter levels 5, 11, and 17. If you multiclass late in your career, your\n\tdamage is still based on your character level, so if you spend your 20th level\n\tto take one level of Wizard you’re suddenly very good at cantrips.<\/p>\n\n\n

              Ability Score Increases<\/h3>\n\n\n

              Ability Score Increases are a class feature. While most classes gain Ability\n\tScore Increases at 4th, 8th, 12th, 16th, and 19th level (Fighters and Rogues\n\tboth get more), it’s still a class feature. It is entirely possible to\n\tmulticlass so much that you never gain an Ability Score Increase.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

              <\/div>\n\n\n\n

              Proficiencies<\/h3>\n\n\n

              When you multiclass into a new class, you don’t get everything which you\n\twould normally get from being a 1st-level character of that class. You do get\n\teverything listed in the class features table for the class, but you’ll\n\ttypically get a reduced set of the class’s initial proficiencies (armor,\n\tskills, tools, weapons, etc.), and you never gain new starting equipment or\n\tsaving throw proficiencies when you multiclass into a new class unless the\n\tclass grants a feature that gives you additional saving throw proficiencies\n\tlike the Rogue’s Slippery Mind.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Keep in mind that duplicate proficiencies do not get replaced or upgraded or\n\tanything like that. If you get proficiency in medium armor twice, you’re still\n\tjust proficient in medium armor. If you get proficiency in Stealth twice,\n\tyou’re still just proficient in Stealth. The rule which allows you to replace\n\tredundant proficiencies is specific to backgrounds, and applies only to\n\tproficiencies granted by your background.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              See the Customization Options chapter of the Player’s handbook for the table\n\tof which proficiencies you gain from multiclassing.<\/p>\n\n\n

              Class Features from Multiple Classes<\/h3>\n\n\n

              Several classes grant features with the same name. When this happens, there\n\tare some specific rules for some of these features. The Player’s Handbook\n\tdetails Channel Divinity, Extra Attack, and Unarmored Defense but I’ve added\n\tsome additional class features which new players often find confusing.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                \n
              • Ability Score Increases<\/strong>: Ability Score increases are\n\t\tindependent of one another. You can gain as many as you can squeeze out of\n\t\t20 levels.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
              • Channel Energy<\/strong>: You don’t get more uses between rests, but\n\t\tyou can add additional ways to use it.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
              • Cantrips<\/strong>: Cantrips known are additive, but remember that\n\t\tthe source that gives you the cantrip determines the ability score which you\n\t\tuse with that spell. For example: a High Elf Cleric \/ Wizard would get one\n\t\tcantrip from their race and a few from each class. You could select Toll the\n\t\tDead from all three sources technically (don’t do that) and you would have\n\t\tthe ability to cast it using both Wisdom (Cleric) and Intelligence (High Elf\n\t\tor Wizard).<\/li>\n\n\n\n
              • Extra Attack<\/strong>: The wording of Extra Attack very\n\t\tspecifically doesn’t add<\/em> an attack; it replaces the number of\n\t\tattacks you can make. If two sources say “you can make two attacks”, you can\n\t\tstill only make two attacks.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
              • Unarmored Defense<\/strong>: Unarmored Defense provides new\n\t\tcalculation methods for determining your AC, just like donning armor or\n\t\thaving natural armor from your race. You can choose which AC calculation to\n\t\tuse (generally you’ll want to use the highest), but you still only get to\n\t\tuse one at a time.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n

                Spellcasting \/ Pact Magic<\/h3>\n\n\n

                The rules for spellcasting for multiclassed characters are detailed, and it’s\n\timportant that you read the original source text. I can’t reproduce it here\n\tbecause it’s not part of the SRD, so please see the Player’s Handbook.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                One interesting note: All of the partial spellcasters in the Player’s\n\tHandbook (paladins, arcane tricksters, etc.) round their levels down when\n\tcalculating your effective level for the purposes of spellcasting. The\n\tArtificer rounds up, and currently they’re the only class to do so.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                Should I Multiclass?<\/h2>\n\n\n

                Now that you understand how multiclassing works, it’s time to ask yourself an\n\timportant question: Should I multiclass?<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Generally, no. A single-class character is usually perfectly fine. While\n\tmulticlassing is a great way to get something different<\/em> for your\n\tcharacter, it’s not guaranteed to be something better<\/em> than what you\n\twould get from a single class.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                That said, if you do multiclass you can find a great number of powerful new\n\toptions to improve your character.<\/p>\n\n\n

                When Should I Multiclass?<\/h3>\n\n\n

                This question has two meanings: “When should I choose to make a multiclass character?” and “when in my character’s level progression should I multiclass?”, but that doesn’t fit well into a section heading.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                You should multiclass when you’re looking to address a gap in your\n\tcharacter’s capabilities, such as poor action economy, low AC, or new\n\tspellcasting options. As explained above, many characters work fine as a\n\tsingle-class character, but sometimes new class features can really add a lot\n\tto your character.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                When to begin taking levels in a new class is more complicated. It’s tempting to start looking at other classes right at level 2, and sometimes that’s okay to do. But be careful about what happens at level 5. Level 5 brings powerful options like Extra Attack and 3rd-level spells like Fireball, and the game’s math is designed to respond to that sharp increase in player characters’ capabilities with an equivalent jump in difficulty.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                If you postpone crucial features like Ability Score Increases, Extra Attack, and high-level spells, you may have trouble with high-CR foes. If you do choose to multiclass, be sure that whatever you get in exchange will offset whatever you give up or postpone.<\/p>\n\n\n

                How Many Levels Should I Take in Each Class?<\/h3>\n\n\n

                This is probably the second-hardest question, right behind “which class do I\n\ttake?”, and it has a similarly frustrating answer: it depends on your\n\tbuild.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Players will commonly take what I refer to as a “class dip”, where they take\n\ta small number of levels in a class before moving on to another class or back\n\tto their original class. This is often an effective way to build a\n\tmulticlassed character because classes often grant several powerful features\n\tin their first few levels.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Sometimes you’ll instead want to alternate sets of levels between two\n\tclasses. This is less common because it often results in being bad at two\n\tclasses rather than really good at one with some goodies from another, but I\n\thave seen a handful of builds where it makes sense.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Classes have some logical “break points” where it often makes sense to break\n\taway from that class. This is typically after you get some specific class\n\tfeatures like the class’s initial subclass features or an Ability Score\n\tIncrease. I’ll discuss break points for each class in the Classes and\n\tSubclasses section, below.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                Classes and Subclasses<\/h2>\n\n\n

                Below I’ll discuss each class and subclass and what they can offer other\n\tclasses. There are few truly bad options since nearly every class and subclass\n\thas something to offer, but not every class\/subclass will add something useful\n\tto every build. I’ll list numbered bullets with break points and explain why\n\tyou might advance to that level before switching away from the class.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                If you’re building a new character and planning to multiclass at some point,\n\tI encourage you to explore the section below now. Starting with a level in a\n\tclass changes your character a great deal, and even if you never take another\n\tlevel in that class a 1st-level class dip can be a great build choice.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                Artificer<\/h3>\n\n\n

                Artificers are a great class dip for many spellcasting classes both because\n\tof their unique rules for multiclassing, access to medium armor and shields,\n\tand because they get proficiency in Constitution saving throws, not to mention\n\ta number of fantastic class features which essentially any character can\n\tuse.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                  \n\t
                1. Magical Tinkering, Spellcasting<\/span>: Magical\n\t\tTinkering doesn’t do much, but the Artificer’s spellcasting includes a lot\n\t\tof options from the Wizard’s spell list, and notably you get to round\n\t\tArtificer levels up when you calculate your effective level for determining\n\t\tyour spell slots. The Artificer gets ritual casting, and they get access to\n\t\thealing spells like Cure Wounds and Healing Word, so for spellcasters like\n\t\tthe wizard a single level offers a lot to like.<\/li>\n\t
                2. Infuse Item, 2 Infused Items<\/span>: Infuse Item\n\t\tis really good, but unless you make it to level 6 the options are very\n\t\tlimited. However, staple options like Enhanced Arcane Focus, Enhanced\n\t\tDefense, and Replicate Magic Item offer enough options that you can easily\n\t\tfind 2 options to use to good effect. For help, see my\n\t\tArtificer Handbook<\/a>.\n\t<\/li>\n\t
                3. Artificer Specialist, The Right Tool for the Job<\/span>: The benefits of your Artificer Specialist vary. The Right Tool for the\n\t\tJob is situational, but can be useful for a class that’s so good with\n\t\ttools.<\/li>\n\t
                4. Ability Score Increase<\/span>: Every class gets\n\t\tan Ability Score Increase at 4th level, and if you already took three levels\n\t\tin the class it’s often very tempting to take one more just to get the\n\t\tASI.<\/li>\n\t
                5. Artificer Specialist feature<\/span>: Varies.<\/li>\n\t
                6. Tool Expertise, 3 Infused Items<\/span>: Tool\n\t\tExpertise is neat, but you’re probably here for the additional Infused Item\n\t\tand the additional Infusion options.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n\n\n

                  Armorer<\/h4>\n\n\n

                  Tempting for the Arcane Trickster, but otherwise the Battle Smith is a better\n\tclass dip for “gish” builds. It’s easy to think “I just want good armor” and\n\tlook at the Armorer, but unless you’re going to use the specific benefits of\n\tthe armor it’s not worth the class dip compared to anything else that gets you\n\theavy armor proficiency.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                    \n\t
                  1. Tools of the Trade, Armorer Spells, Arcane Armor, Armor Model<\/span>: The armor is why you’re here. The Defender armor is good for front-line\n\t\tbuilds, while Infiltrator Armor is very tempting for the Rogue because you\n\t\tget persistent Advantage on Dexterity (Stealth) checks and you can use the\n\t\tLightning Launcher to deliver Sneak Attack. However, it doesn’t address the\n\t\tMAD issues for the Arcane Trickster like the Battle Smith does, which makes\n\t\tit a hard choice.<\/li>\n\t
                  2. Extra Attack<\/span>: Arcane tricksters might\n\t\tdecide that 2 more levels to get Extra Attack is worth the cost, but I would\n\t\tthink long and hard about how desperate you are to make a second attack\n\t\tcompared to what you give up, especially since Booming Blade is an\n\t\toption.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n\n\n

                    Alchemist<\/h4>\n\n\n

                    Nothing good enough to justify the opportunity cost of multiclassing.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                      \n\t
                    1. Tool Proficiency, Alchemist Spells, Experimental Elixir<\/span>: The spells are nothing new to the Artificer’s spell list. Experimental\n\t\tElixir is a neat way to turn your 1st-level spell slots into some new\n\t\teffects, but it’s not good enough to justify multiclassing for three\n\t\tlevels.<\/li>\n\t
                    2. Alchemical Savant<\/span>: A nice bonus to\n\t\tcommon damage spells and to healing spells, but compared to taking more\n\t\tlevels in a class with full spellcasting (sorcerer, wizard, etc.) it’s not\n\t\tworth the levels.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n\n\n

                      Artillerist<\/h4>\n\n\n

                      Three levels to get an Arcane Cannon can be a powerful asset, even if you\n\tdon’t have a lot of Intelligence to throw around. Arcane Cannon is that\n\tgood.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                        \n\t
                      1. Tool Proficiency, Artillerist Spells, Eldritch Cannon<\/span>: The spells are nothing new to the Artificer’s spell list. You’re here for\n\t\tEldritch Cannon. Even if your Intelligence is mediocre, the Protector option\n\t\tis still spectacular. If you’re built around Intelligence, the Flamethrower\n\t\tand Force Ballista options both work fine even with only three levels in\n\t\tartificer. Even the Wizard might find Eldritch Cannon a better use of a\n\t\t1st-level spell slot than most spells. If you don’t want to pour spell slots\n\t\tinto replacing cannons, leave yours out of combat and it’s still effectively\n\t\tcasting False Life on your whole party before every encounter.<\/li>\n\t
                      2. Arcane Firearm<\/span>: Tempting, but for a full\n\t\tspellcaster this isn’t good enough to justify giving up two levels of your\n\t\tmain class’s spellcasting and the additional spell level which it\n\t\tgrants.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n\n\n

                        Battle Smith<\/h4>\n\n\n

                        Battle Ready is the primary reason to multiclass into Battle Smith. It’s an\n\tinteresting option for Intelligence-based arcane\/martial subclasses (arcane\n\ttrickster, etc.), but it won’t always fix your MAD problems, and you need to\n\tconsider if giving up 3 to 4 levels in your primary class and postponing your\n\tclass’s high-level class features is worth having a better Intelligence bonus\n\tto back up your spells without sacrificing your attack bonus.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                          \n\t
                        1. Tool Proficiency, Battle Smith Spells, Battle Ready, Steel Defender<\/span>: There are two notable things here: Battle Ready and Steel Defender.\n\t\tBattle Ready is tempting for arcane tricksters, eldritch knights, and\n\t\tbladesingers who all typically need to balance their ability scores between\n\t\teither Strength or Dexterity and Intelligence. However, your Steel Defender\n\t\tis much less useful since its hit points are calculated based on your\n\t\tartificer class level.<\/li>\n\t
                        2. Extra Attack<\/span>: Arcane tricksters might\n\t\tdecide that 2 more levels to get Extra Attack is worth the cost, but I would\n\t\tthink long and hard about how desperate you are to make a second attack\n\t\tcompared to what you give up, especially since Booming Blade is an\n\t\toption.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n\n\n\n\n

                          Barbarian<\/h3>\n\n\n

                          The Barbarian is a tempting class dip for martial characters or for other\n\tclasses which want to improve their martial capabilities beyond what their\n\tclass offers on its own. However, it can also be a difficult option because\n\tthere are so few boring levels in the class so it’s hard to pick a break\n\tpoint.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            \n\t

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    Example Quick Multiclass Builds<\/h2>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    All the stuff above was… well, it was a lot. I get it. Character optimization can be complicated, and those complications increase exponentially when you bring multiclassing into the equation. This section details several examples of multiclass options which appeal to specific classes. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    If you already know the class that you want to focus on (your “primary class”) but want to explore multiclassing, this section offers several go-to multiclass options for each class which you can pick up without a ton of trouble and without adding a lot of complication to your build.<\/p>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    Artificer Example Builds<\/h3>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    Fighter (Any) 1 \/ Artificer (Any) X<\/strong>: Starting with a level\n\tin fighter gets you heavy armor without giving up proficiency in Constitution\n\tsaves. For artificer subclasses which don’t already get heavy armor\n\tproficiency, this can be a significant boost to your AC and can reduce the\n\tneed for Dexterity so that you can focus on other ability scores. However, if\n\tyou don’t meet the Strength requirement you’ll face a persistent and annoying\n\tspeed penalty.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    Rogue (Thief) 3 \/ Artificer (Any) X<\/strong>: Do you like items? Of\n\tcourse you do! On top of Expertise, three levels in rogue (thief) can get you\n\tFast Hands, allowing you to take the Use an Object action as a Bonus\n\tAction.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    Wizard (Bladesinging) 2 \/ Artificer (Battlesmith) X<\/strong>: Bladesong is a fantastic buff for an Intelligence-based character, and the Battlesmith is an Intelligence-based front-line character so the combination is pretty obvious. Between Battle Ready and Bladesong you’re adding Intelligence to many of your most important stats so you can focus on Intelligence almost exclusively. However, Bladesong limits you to light or no armor, so you’ll need to invest heavily in Dexterity.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      Sorcerer (Any) 1, 2, or 3 \/ Bard (Any) X<\/strong>: Sorcerers get\n\tproficiency in Constitution saving throws (crucial for Concentration) and get\n\tmelee cantrips like Booming Blade which aren’t normally available to the Bard\n\twithout spending powerful resources like Magical Secrets.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       Warlock (Hexblade) 1, 2, or 3 \/ Bard (Any) X<\/strong>: Hexblade significantly simplifies a lot of the Bard’s deficiencies in melee combat.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       Get 14 Dexterity, a suit of half plate, and a shield and you’ll have 19 AC and you can focus on your Charisma and improve both your martial capabilities and your spellcasting at the same time. A second Warlock Level gets you invocations, and a third can get you both Pact of the Blade and Improved Pact Weapon. However, that second or third level means trading some spellcasting for more martial capabilities, so you need to decide what balance you want to strike with your character.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      Wizard (School of Divination or Chronurgy Magic) 2 \/ Bard (College of Eloquence) X<\/strong>: Seize control of the outcome of rolls!                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       The remainder of your levels go into bard primarily for Bardic Inspiration.\n\tYour number of uses of Bardic Inspiration are Charisma-based, so you want this\n\tto be a high-Charisma build and limit your wizard spells to spells which don’t\n\tdepend on your spellcasting modifier (absorb elements, shield, and utility\n\toptions are all good choices).<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      To go even further, add Halfling for the Lucky racial trait, and consider the\n\tBountiful Luck feat. You can add the Lucky feat regardless of your race, too.\n\tHowever, that’s two feats which puts off Ability Score Increases for a long\n\ttime, and between that and being a full spell level behind on your bard\n\tspellcasting you’ll need to avoid offensive spells and spells which depend on\n\tyour spellcasting ability modifier as much as possible.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        Cleric Example Builds<\/h3>\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        Fighter 1 or 2 \/ Cleric (Any) X<\/strong>: Starting with Fighter gets you proficiency in Constitution saves, which is a huge help with Concentration checks. It also gets you heavy armor if your domain doesn’t provide it and a Fighting Style.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        Two levels gets you Action Surge, allowing you to do things like casting two spells in the same turn without running into the limitation on Bonus Action spells. Action Surge only works once per Short or Long Rest, but it’s still noteworthy. You might go to three levels for a subclass, but that’s uncommon.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        Monk (Any) 1 \/ Cleric (Any) X<\/strong>: Unarmored Defense and Martial Arts are interesting options for clerics that don’t get heavy armor proficiency. Grab a monk weapon and focus on Dexterity and Wisdom, and many domains which offer a version of Divine Strike can be considerably more effective in melee. The second attack from Martial Arts will be redundant for War Domain, but other domains like Grave Domain or Light Domain gain a reliable way to make more than one weapon attack per turn.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        Unarmored Defense also allows you to hit 20 AC (though this requires quite a few Ability Score Increases) without using armor or a shield, which means that you can keep a hand free to perform spellcasting components without rely on War Caster or juggling your weapon.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        Paladin (Any) 2, 5, or 6 \/ Cleric (Any) X<\/strong>: Two levels of paladin offers Divine Smite, which combined with the cleric’s better spell progression (and therefore larger pool of spell slots) offers more smites and generally better martial options for the Cleric. You won’t get proficiency with Constitution saves like you would with a Fighter class dip, but If you can manage 6 levels Aura of Protection can help compensate or you can take Resilient.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        Unfortunately, this build can be very MAD since paladins are already MAD and you’re adding a Wisdom requirement. Most likely, you’ll have better results attempting the same build with a Divine Soul Sorcerer.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        Rogue (Any) 1, 2, or 3 \/ Cleric (Trickery) X<\/strong>: The Trickery cleric makes sense in a stealthy party, but lacks the core proficiencies brought by a rogue, and doesn’t have access to finesse weapons better than a dagger. If you want to replace a rogue with a trickery cleric, starting with a level of rogue gets you all of the rogue’s proficiencies and a splash of Sneak Attack damage.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        Two levels gets you Cunning Action so you’ve always got a good use for your Bonus Action without pouring spell slots into Spiritual Weapon, and three levels gets you a bit more Sneak Attack and a subclass. Personally I would go for Arcane Trickster for the spellcasting or Mastermind for Master of Tactics to complement the cleric’s support capabilities.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          Monk (Any) 1 \/ Druid (Circle of Spores) X<\/strong>: I know, two monk\/druid builds seems redundant, but this is a really great combo that I can’t ignore.
