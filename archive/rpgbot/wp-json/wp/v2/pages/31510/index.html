(*insert righteous outrage here*)<\/p>\n\n\n\nWell, the Merriam-Webster’s online dictionary gives many possible\n\tdefinitions.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe one that applies most is, “One controlling a particular aspect or part of\n\treality.”<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

How does that sum up Wizards for ya?<\/p>\n\n\n\nMore importantly, when I talk about a “god” Wizard, I’m talking about style\n\tnot power. (like writing that will get me out of trouble.)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nWhat god comes down and finishes off all his mortal enemies personally? No,\n\tinstead he provides his followers the tools required to do it themselves.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Greek heroes are all pretty much examples of this principle. That is the\n\trole of the wizard in the party. He doesn’t win the combat, he instead\n\tprovides the tools for the rest of the party to win, by “controlling reality”.\n\tAKA: a god.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

The Party Roles:<\/h2>\n\n\nAnyone reading this who has not been playing D&D over the past thirty\n\tyears may not know that there are some fairly “official” party roles that date\n\tback to the original basic set. They are Fighter, Thief, Magic User and\n\tCleric. The idea is that the Cleric heals, the Fighter takes hits and does\n\tdamage, the Thief opens locks, disarms traps and backstabs, and the Magic User\n\tthrows magic missiles and fireballs. These roles are every bit as outdated as\n\tBasic D&D itself, yet you still see players flocking to “fill” these\n\troles, thinking that this remains the most effective party, despite a game\n\tthat resembles Basic D&D cosmetically only.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nHow many times have you gotten\/sent an email when wondering what kind of\n\tcharacter you could make, and a reply comes up with something like, “We have a\n\tRogue, a Druid and a Sorcerer.” The implication of course is, “Make a\n\tfighter-type”, but in fact, the email is useless. Is the Rogue a melee rogue\n\tor an archery rogue? Is the Druid going to be engaging in melee? What kind of\n\tspells will the Sorcerer have? Maybe the best thing for this party is a\n\tWizard, or another rogue…who knows?<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nInstead the email should say, “In combat we’ve got a Tank, a Striker and a\n\tBattlefield Controller, another Tank would be great. Out of combat we need a\n\tparty face. How about some kind of Melee character with some social skills?”\n\tCharacters are too flexible in D&D (and Pathfinder) to define roles by\n\tclass. Instead the role should be defined by what they do.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe mechanics of the game changed enough in 3.0 that the optimal party\n\tchanged with them, yet many players never realized that the iconic party is no\n\tlonger optimal, so we still see Wizards throwing fireballs (sorry, just threw\n\tup a bit in my mouth), and Clerics running around healing, wondering why they\n\tcan’t heal as fast as the party seems to take damage.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe reality is that D&D isn’t all combat, but combat plays a major role\n\tin pretty much every campaign. Therefore you can break party roles into two\n\tmajor categories. The “out of combat” roles, and the “combat” roles. Let\u2019s\n\tlook at how the God Wizard fits into each.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

5 OUT OF COMBAT ROLES:<\/h2>\n\n\n

Social (“The Fop”)<\/span><\/p>\n\n\n\nCan the wizard fill this role? Well if you specialize in enchantment kinds of\n\tspells, then you very well might be able to, but, you shouldn’t. First, you\n\taren’t the best choice to fill this role, and secondly, this guy tends to\n\tthink he’s the leader, do you know what happens to the leader? He gets\n\ttargeted first. Let the Paladin, Cleric or Bard take this role. Pretend you’re\n\tjealous.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

Sneak (“The Corpse”)<\/span><\/p>\n\n\n\nCan the wizard fill this role? Well if you specialize in spells that imitate\n\tthe iconic rogue skills like invisibility and knock, and a few divinations you\n\tvery well might be able to, but, you shouldn’t. The purpose of the Sneak is to\n\tscout out ahead in the enemy’s lair, look for traps and disarm them, scout out\n\tthe enemy and report back, and do this all alone. Wonder why I call him “the\n\tCorpse”? Read what he does again.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

Healbot<\/span><\/p>\n\n\n\nCan the wizard fill this role? Actually, in Pathfinder he can’t, but don’t\n\tworry, you don’t want it. The healbot gets to use up all his resources\n\t“servicing” the party between combats. Sound pleasant?<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

The Lump (“The lump”)<\/span><\/p>\n\n\n\nI can’t think of another name for him. He’s the character of the player that\n\tmade his character specifically for combat, and is really uninterested\n\twhenever he’s not rolling his attack rolls. He makes the best use of his time\n\twhen not in combat by snoozing, or reading a novel, or making it very clear to\n\tthe DM that he’s bored. You DEFINITELY don’t want to be this guy.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

Utility Caster (“Everything else”)<\/span><\/p>\n\n\n\nThe party transporter, the party Diviner. One way or another – this is the\n\tcasters’ role – in other words – this is you.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThat’s pretty much it. A party should look to cover all those bases (except\n\tthe lump, but all too often it gets filled regardless, and often a single\n\tcharacter can fill more than one (The party Cleric may be both the Fop and the\n\tHealbot…lucky guy), but let’s be honest here, D&D is largely about\n\tcombat, so even if you have a character that is the Fop, the Corpse, the\n\tHealbot and the Utility Caster, if you aren’t contributing to combat, then you\n\tare a liability to your party, because when characters die, it’s usually in\n\tcombat.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

The Four Combat Roles:<\/h2>\n\n\nThe Tank: (“The Big Stupid Fighter”):<\/span> This\n\trole involves two things: Doing Hit Points damage to BBEG (big bad evil guy),\n\tforcing BBEG to attack you with his viscous weaponry. The Big Stupid Fighter\n\tis not always a fighter (though stereotypically he is). He may be a Barbarian,\n\ta Summoned Critter, or a Druid. In order to qualify as a Big Stupid Fighter he\n\tshould be any character that actively tries to be the target of enemy attacks.\n\tFor those who wonder why I would label this character as “stupid” regardless\n\tof their INT score – reread the previous sentence.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Striker: (“The Glass Cannon”):<\/span> This role\n\tinvolves one thing: Doing HP damage to BBEG. The Glass Cannon is like the Big\n\tStupid Fighter except he does not want to take damage. Usually this is not due\n\tto superior intelligence – but instead due to inferior HP or AC (or in most\n\tcases – both). The Glass Cannon is often a Rogue (Or Rouge for our 13 year old\n\treaders), a Gish, an Archer, or a Blaster (the inferior wizard).<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe controller of reality: (“GOD”):<\/span> When\n\treality would entail the above two meeting a rather messy end – someone will\n\tneed to make some adjustments to said reality in order for the above two to\n\tinstead meet glorious victory. What other label could such a force be labelled\n\tas than “God”? Well – how about “Primary Caster” One label or another – this\n\tguy needs to control reality to make sure the right team wins.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Waste of Space:<\/span> What else do you call\n\thim? The Waste of Space is the guy who thought that multi-classing Wizard and\n\tSorcerer made an excellent “character concept”, or maybe he figures that a\n\tdedicated healer is an appropriate contributor to a combat environment, or\n\tmaybe he’s the Cleric who insists on swinging a mace for 1d8 damage per round\n\tbecause he won’t memorize any useful combat spells, I could go on, but you\n\tknow who I mean, there is probably one in your current group. If there\n\tisn’t…well…maybe there is…*cough* *cough*…ahem.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n* Why isn’t the Healer useful in combat? There are two ways you can live your\n\t“pretend” life – “reactively” or “proactively”. The God Wizard will alter\n\treality to prevent damage, a healer will try to do “damage control” (pun\n\tintended) after the damage has been taken. Simple truth: The mechanics of the\n\tgame make preventing damage more efficient then healing damage after the fact.\n\tThat’s not to say a well placed “Heal” or even “CLW” never has use in combat –\n\tbut if you’re doing your job – it should never be required as a primary\n\trole.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

What roles to fill?<\/h2>\n\n\nObviously, I’m suggesting here what you should be doing. You should be a\n\tutility caster outside of combat, and in combat, you should be the “God\n\tWizard”. However, not every party is the same, and sometimes the roles are\n\tweak or need some filling. Fortunately, you can help out when needed. Focusing\n\ton any of these areas shouldn’t be your prime concern, but consider yourself a\n\t“patch” for a leak.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Fop: Your charisma likely ain’t great, and you likely have all the social\n\tskills of the Big Stupid Fighter. Filling this role is going to be a\n\tchallenge. However, you can provide some aid to the party Fop when required.\n\tThe key here is enhancement and enchantment. Use enhancement spells like\n\tEagle’s Splendor if your party Fop isn’t all he should be, and even a simple\n\tprestidigitation can clean him and his clothes up when he stinks like he’s\n\tbeen crawling a dungeon for the past week.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Corpse: You aren’t any kind of sneak, but you can magically cover the\n\tskills. If the party is short on the scouts, a planar bound Imp is a pretty\n\tgood one. Even a low level summoned monster (like a Dire Rat) can scout out\n\tunder a door or around a corner. Your familiar can also make a great scout.\n\tWho’s to know yet another bat in the cave is in fact an intelligent creature?\n\tKnock spells trump any open lock skills, and don’t worry about disarming\n\ttraps…set them off (not personally, but that summoned Dire Rat can, or\n\tperhaps an unseen servant to open doors and chests that may be otherwise\n\tdangerous. Invisibility + Fly is as good as any stealth skill most of the\n\ttime.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Healbot: Did you know that a 9th level wizard can cast Cure Serious\n\tWounds? OK, OK, not directly, but he CAN summon a Bralani Azata, and that\n\tBralani Azata can cast Cure Serious Wounds…twice. Not something you want to\n\tdo often, but if you need some quick healing in a combat, it can be done. That\n\tsaid, the wizard is an absolute terrible healer, but he can help out in a\n\thealing weak party. Out of combat, don’t get suckered into doing this. Get\n\tsome cure light wounds wands (one of the best deals in the game) and somebody\n\tin the party surely can use it (whether Cleric, Druid, Bard or anyone with Use\n\tMagic Device)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Big Stupid Fighter: Obviously you will never do this personally, but\n\tthat’s what summoning spells are for right? A summoned critter may not be as\n\ttough as a PC fighter, but he takes up space, draws attacks, and has some form\n\tof attack as well…and they never need healing after the fight.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Striker: That’s what Blast spells make a wizard. I’m not a fan of these\n\tkinds of spells, but they have their place, and that’s the place of a weak\n\tstriker in the party.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Waste of Space: Everything I’ve just pointed out you want to avoid doing.\n\tWizards aren’t the best at any of these roles, and when you start filling\n\tthese roles you will notice your spells running out really fast. A Wizard may\n\tbe able to do nearly anything, but don’t get fooled into thinking he can do\n\teverything. Spells\/day is the limitation that ensures that a Wizard doesn’t\n\tget too big for his britches, or he finds himself filling the one role you\n\tnever wanted to, the “Waste or space”<\/p\n>\n\n\n

Get a Job NA-NA-NA-NA, NA-NA-NA-NA-NA:<\/h2>\n\n\nIn combat, the God wizard doesn’t have one job, or two, he’s got\n\tthree…yippee! Remember these three jobs, and it will help you evaluate\n\tspells, pick memorization’s, and fulfill your combat role effectively. None of\n\tthese jobs is to do damage to the enemy. In fact, a Wizard can be very\n\teffective and powerful without ever “killing” anything.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe God Wizards three jobs are: Control the Battlefield, Debuff the enemy,\n\tBuff your allies. Lets look at each separately.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

Battlefield Control:<\/span><\/p>\n\n\n\nIn order to be an effective battlefield controller – you should consider your\n\tprimary goal to line up your enemies flanked by your Glass Cannon and Big\n\tStupid Fighter one at a time and backwards, all while standing on their heads.\n\tThis will make the BSF and the GC win the combat with little damage to\n\tthemselves – and they will feel like “they” won. That’s the point – you’re God\n\tafter all, let the mortals have their victory.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe point of Battlefield Control is that you are ensuring your allies gain\n\ttactical advantage over your enemies. If you secure tactical advantage, and\n\thold it, you will win. A simple wall spell can separate enemies so that they\n\tcan be taken on one at a time. Putting the BSF and the Sneak Attacker in\n\tflanking positions makes each of them more effective than they otherwise would\n\tbe. Some characters have difficulty with flying enemies or difficult terrain.\n\tSometimes hampering vision can force an enemy to close the distance with your\n\teffective melee characters. The best thing about battlefield controls, is\n\tquite often they offer no saving throw, no spell resistance, so luck ceases to\n\tbe a factor, it’s all about smart tactics and evaluating the situation\n\tcorrectly.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

Debuffing:<\/span><\/p>\n\n\n\nIn order to be an effective Debuffer – you should consider your primary goal\n\tto have your BBEG standing in front of your GC and BSF dazed, stunned,\n\tnauseated, STR = 1, Dex = 1, Level = 1, and Blind for good measure. This will\n\tmake the BSF and the GC win the combat with little damage to themselves – and\n\tthey will feel like “they” won. That’s the point – you’re God after all, let\n\tthe mortals have their victory.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n(Note to readers: Debuffing can more literally be termed as reducing\n\tattributes or levels of opponents – I extend the term to be anything that\n\timpairs or damages the abilities of the enemy – which is why I include things\n\tlike “blinding” to be a debuff)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nUsually when we talk about debuffing we talk about flat penalties or status\n\teffects. Look for debuffs that either affect large groups of enemies or that\n\toffer no saving throw. A spell like Slow spell thrown over 6 enemies may\n\tstagger only 3, but that creates a huge advantage for your allies. Numerical\n\tdebuffs also adjust the math in the favor of your allies as well. Why worry\n\tabout a save or die, when a save-or-lose will do the job?<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

Buffing:<\/span><\/p>\n\n\n\nBuffing means making your allies more powerful. In order to be effective at\n\tbuffing – you turn your Big Stupid Fighter into a Colossal, Stupid Fighter on\n\tcrack, and your Glass Cannon into an Adamantium Chain Gun. This will make the\n\tBSF and the GC win the combat with little damage to themselves – and they will\n\tfeel like “they” won. That’s the point – you’re God after all, let the mortals\n\thave their victory.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe great thing about buffing is that when you buff, you NEVER need to worry\n\tabout “to hit” rolls, saving throws, or spell resistance. Your party members\n\tare all too glad to get that statistical advantage in combat, whether you are\n\toffering an extra attack, or a bonus to hit and damage. Buffing is also a\n\tgreat way to become very popular with the rest of the party, who act like kids\n\ton Christmas Morning when you start passing out buff spells.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nSo What Job is Best? It really depends on the circumstance. Personally, I\n\tthink a “God” wizard should put the greatest emphasis on Battlefield Control\n\tspells. If you have the right battlefield control spell for the situation, you\n\twill win the combat every time. It doesn’t win the combat on its own, but it\n\tcreates a tactical advantage that shift the odds far enough in the favor of\n\tyour allies that after that, they are assured of victory. Buffing is a good\n\tstandby for when no Battlefield Control spell is appropriate, and you are\n\tconcerned about resistances or immunities of your enemies. Debuffing is\n\tusually more significant than buffing at the same level, so if you think you\n\tcan “stick” some debuffs to an enemy or a group of enemies, it is a great way\n\tto go.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

Why to skip the “all powerful” Save or Die spells:<\/h2>\n\n\nYes, Save or Die is powerful, we’ve all heard why (who cares how many HP the\n\tmonster has? If they fail their save, the fight is over) – however, it is\n\toverrated. This is why:<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nLots of enemies: You take down one and do little else to help your Big Stupid\n\tFighter and Glass Cannon. You spent a high level spell doing so.\n\tCongratulations – you’ve been demoted from God to Glass Cannon. Ouch!<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nOne Big Enemy: You either do nothing or everything. The Big Stupid Fighter\n\tand Glass Cannon are either in lots of trouble or are feeling useless. This is\n\tlike the chess player who is addicted to using his queen – ends up losing it,\n\tand then sucks for the rest of the game. Use your lesser pieces (That’s the\n\tbig stupid fighter and the glass cannon – your peons) to your advantage – let\n\tthem do the dirty work – your job is to make it easy for them, not to take\n\ttheir place. Try to take their place your spells will run out fast. Besides,\n\tyou are a team, enjoy the benefits of that.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nBy Yourself: Now in this rare circumstance – save or dies are actually quite\n\tgood. However – avoid this situation like the plague. If you choose to test\n\tyour “phenomenal cosmic power” by going out alone, then YOU are the Big Stupid\n\tFighter, except you aren’t Big, you aren’t a Fighter, and you’re not….well,\n\tthat’s it pretty much.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

Why “Blast” spells should be recreation only:<\/h2>\n\n\nThe first point is that in 3.0, blast spells became in inefficient way to do\n\tdamage. Look at a fireball for example. Cast by a 5th level wizard it does 5d6\n\tdamage…but not really, saving throws can reduce that by half, evasion can\n\teliminate it entirely, and improved evasion will at least cut it in half. Then\n\tyou take into account fire resistance (AFTER the save), which will very\n\tcommonly reduce it further by 5, 10, 20, or even offer full immunity…and\n\tfire resistance and immunity is common. In the end, that Fireball will do 20\n\tpoints of damage if you are lucky, more likely, anywhere from 5 to 10 damage.\n\tSo how many creatures that you fight at level 5 can take that? Yep…all of\n\tthem, while laughing at you. Now how much damage does a Haste spell do? Well,\n\tyou would need to take one average attack from every party member for every\n\tround of duration and add them together. The number will vary, but it will\n\tdwarf the damage of a fireball spell. You don’t get the glory, but you did the\n\tright thing.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThen the second point is a tactical one. HP damage in Pathfinder does NOT\n\tdecrease your ability to fight. The big bad monster with 1 HP has just as\n\tdeadly attack as the big bad monster with 100 hp. “Softening” up the enemy\n\twith a blast spell may or may not change the time it takes the rest of the\n\tparty to drop the enemy, but it does nothing to ensure their safety during\n\tthat time. Giving your allies a tactical or mechanical advantage prevents\n\tparty deaths. Throw a blast when you have nothing better to do, but never\n\tthrow one when you have one of your 3 jobs to do.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nI’m not saying you should never do pure blast. In fact, my own wizards\n\tusually have a blast or two at hand. I’m just saying that blasting is\n\tsomething you do after you’ve ensured tactical advantage in the combat.\n\tBlasting in combat should be….what comes after tertiary?<\/p\n>\n\n\n

Summoning: God’s favorite spells<\/h2>\n\n\nI love summoning. So much so, that I need a special section to explain why\n\tyou should do it. So why summon? Here’s a few reasons. There’s more than this,\n\tbut these are the main advantages.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n1) Doing Damage: Summoned creatures aren’t a one shot damage spell. Summons\n\tdo damage every round, the blast spell that keeps on blasting. Note that\n\tCelestial and Fiendish Templates no longer add INT to the animal, but they do\n\tprovide the new and improved Smite Evil\/Good mechanic – which is a signficant\n\toffensive boost.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n2) Taking Damage: Every time the enemy attacks a summoned creature you win.\n\tThat’s an attack that would have gone towards you or your allies. Whether it\n\thits or not really doesn’t matter all that much, because you never need to\n\theal a summoned creature after battle.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n3) Battlefield Control: Summoned creatures do more than attack, they also\n\tgrapple, trip, and block the enemy, they also provide cover. Many summoned\n\tcreatures are amazing grapplers.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n4) Extra Spellcasting: Is casting one spell per round getting you down? Well,\n\tthere are always quickened spells, but they are so darned expensive. What if\n\tyou summon a creature with Spell Like Abilities? Then you cast, the summoned\n\tcreature casts, and it’s like your own little quickened spell every round.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n5) Mounts: Sometimes you need a quick way to get your pet Big Stupid Fighter\n\tup to the big bad guy with wings. That’s where a quick summoning can be very\n\thandy. A Dire Bat or any number of large (or larger) flying creatures can make\n\teffective quick mounts to get your party where they need to be.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n6) Perception: Ever had that invisible guy you just couldn’t find? You have a\n\tglitterdust but don’t know where to aim it, and you left your see invisible\n\tspell unmemorized today. No problem, a quick summoning spell and your Dire Bat\n\twill be attacking the offending square using its blindsense. Now you know\n\twhere to throw that glitterdust spell.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

What STATS does GOD have anyways?<\/h2>\n\n\nUnfortunately – no matter how close to God you become – your DM is closer.\n\tHopefully your DM has gone the more progressive route of a no-luck version of\n\tcharacter creation, and allows point buy for stats, but some old-time DM’s out\n\tthere can’t get past the idea that your stat rolls should involve the “luck of\n\tthe dice”.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nAny way you slice it – here’s the simple rules I follow when making my God.\n\tHighest stat goes to INT<\/span> – no exception. (and\n\tall my stat increases will be going into INT) My\n\tCON<\/span> is always at least 12 (14 is better if\n\tyou can). Whatever else I can afford goes into\n\tDEX<\/span>. This is before Racial modifiers.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nRandom Roll: Put your highest roll into INT. Dex and Con should have your\n\tsecond and third highest roll, and the other stats get the dump. If your 3rd\n\thighest stat is at least 12, put your second highest stat into DEX, otherwise,\n\tyour second highest stat should go to CON.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nPoint Buy: What point buy you have will make a difference, but you can dump\n\tStrength, Wisdom and Charisma, so usually you can do pretty well overall.\n\tHere’s some examples:<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n12-point buy: Str 7 (-4), Dex 16 (10), Con 12 (2), Int 16 (10), Wis 7 (-4),\n\tCha 8 (-2)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n15-point buy: Str 7 (-4), Dex 16 (10), Con 12 (2), Int 17 (13), Wis 7 (-4),\n\tCha 8 (-2)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n20-point buy: Str 7 (-4), Dex 16 (10), Con 13 (3), Int 18 (17), Wis 7 (-4),\n\tCha 8 (-2)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n25-point buy: Str 8 (-2), Dex 16 (10), Con 14 (5), Int 18 (17), Wis 7 (-4),\n\tCha 9 (-1)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nObviously these aren’t set in stone. If you want to have a decent strength\n\t(maybe your DM is an encumbrance Nazi), then drop the Dex to a 14, or whatever\n\tyou need to do. The most important thing is you make sure your Intelligence is\n\tyour top priority, and that Dex and Con, in that order, are number 2 and 3.\n\tThe rest are dump stats.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

What Race is God?<\/h2>\n\n\nIn Pathfinder, you’ve got great choices for Wizards, and more than that, your\n\tchoices are flexible. However, that said, your best, best choice is the\n\telf.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nElf<\/span>: Unlike previous editions, the Elf is\n\ttruly made for the Wizard class. A +2 to Dex and Int is huge, and the -2 Con\n\tis no longer a game-breaker. In addition, the elf gets a +2 bonus to defeat\n\tSR, being a racial bonus, this should stack with the Spell Penetration feats.\n\tBonuses to spellcraft (for identifying items) and low light vision, and other\n\tlittle tidbits are all welcome, although the primary selling features we’ve\n\talready covered.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nHuman:<\/span> With a flexible stat bonus you can\n\tget that all important +2 Int score. The lack of racial features matters less\n\twith the Wizard that can duplicate many of those features with spells. The\n\textra skill point is great, as is the extra feat.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nHalf-Elf:<\/span> Pretty much full out trumped by\n\tthe Elf, however, you’ll still take the +2 Int score, which is the most\n\timportant feature, and you get a skill focus which is nice too. Not my first\n\tchoice, but the +2 Int alone puts it in the same league.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nHalf Orc:<\/span> *cries*, yes, you can give your\n\tHalf-Orc a +2 Int. I know, I know, it’s just wrong. However, in addition to\n\tthat, the Half-Orc is actually an excellent choice, one I would only put\n\tsecond to the “made for the wizard” elf. The reason is the Orc Ferocity\n\tability. If there is any class that can benefit more from that\n\tone-last-standard-action than a Wizard, I don’t know what it is. That said,\n\thopefully this is an ability you will never need.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nDwarves, Halflings and Gnomes:<\/span> Not great\n\tchoices, though any of them can still make a good wizard. In general, a +2 Int\n\tscore is the best thing you can get with race, but not having it by no means\n\tmakes a Wizard worthless. However, this is an optimization Guide, and the\n\toptimal choices will have that +2 Int score.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

Arcane Bond: Pick your double edged sword:<\/h2>\n\n\nI’m not a huge fan of Arcane Bonds. Why? Well, they are both double edged\n\tswords. Advantages that have a significant possibility to be disadvantages.\n\tHowever, you get them anyways, so lets check out the choices, and the\n\tadvantage and disadvantage of each.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Bonded Object<\/span>: Remember in “The Two\n\tTowers” when Sauroman’s staff was destroyed how screwed he was? Welcome to the\n\tbonded object. This object gives you a free spell of any level you can cast,\n\tas long as it is in your spellbook, absolutely free and spontaneous, once per\n\tday. This is a nice advantage, especially at low levels when you are short on\n\tcastings. In fact, a first level Specialist Wizard with a Bonded Object gets\n\tjust as many castings per day as a Sorcerer. Yeah…nice.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nNow the downside. Bonded items can be destroyed or lost or stolen, and you\n\tonly can have one, and they take awhile to replace (1 week before you can cast\n\ta ritual that costs 200gp\/level to replace the item). During that time how\n\tscrewed are you? Super screwed. Every spell you cast requires a DC 20+spell\n\tlevel caster check. OK, EVENTUALLY this will be an auto-succeed, but that’s\n\tonly going to be at high levels. Until then, you have a chance (and at low\n\tlevels a good chance) for every spell you cast to fail. Welcome to Hell. Rings\n\tor amulets of course are less likely to be damaged in combat, but they are\n\teven easier to steal, and they take up a valuable item slot.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nNow how big a disadvantage this is really is going to depend on your DM.\n\tHowever, I always have a second copy of my spellbook hidden away somewhere,\n\tbut I can’t have a second copy of a Bonded Object, so that makes me nervous.\n\tIn the end, my point is, there are two sides to this coin, one gives you a\n\tmoderate advantage, the other absolutely destroys you for at least a week.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThe Bonded Creature (Familiar):<\/span> The obvious\n\tdisadvantage of a familiar is that, with 1\/2 your HP, the Familiar is\n\trelatively vulnerable, and not especially difficult to kill. However, also\n\tnote that unlike previous editions, there is no penalty to you for the death\n\tof your familiar (except the loss of the familiar, which can be replaced later\n\tat cost). If your familiar dies, you still can cast spells, which makes me\n\tmore inclined towards this bond.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nFamiliars can be quite useful, they can be great scouts, can eventually\n\tdeliver touch spells (though this is dangerous because it identifies the\n\tfamiliar as a target) and deliver messages, as well as give you a moderate\n\tbonus on a skill or saving throw (and you always get alertness when the\n\tfamiliar is close), and the familiar gets your skill ranks, so you get a\n\tsecond roll on knowledges or that type of thing.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nOverall, I’m not a huge fan of familiars, the bonuses they give are pretty\n\tminor, and sometimes keeping them safe is a pain, but they are never worse\n\tthan nothing, which a Bonded Object could be, so therefore, they get my\n\tcomplete endorsement and suggestion.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

Specialization, God’s choices:<\/h2>\n\n\nIn general you will find that the weight of spells that serve the style of\n\tthe “God Wizard” fall more under Conjuration than any other school. For that\n\treason, you should consider Conjuration specialty based on that alone. For the\n\tmost part, the specialization abilities are pretty minor (although nice to\n\thave), but that said, you can make a God Wizard that follows any school as a\n\tspecialist. Keep in mind that you can cast from prohibited schools, but should\n\tavoid doing so except when necessary, because the cost is double.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nAbjuration School:<\/span> As a school, Abjuration\n\tis for the most part a one-trick-pony. Weighted heavily in resistances and\n\tdefensive bonuses, interestingly though, many of the best defensive spells in\n\tthe game are not abjuration spells at all. That means that the greatest\n\tstrength of the Abjuration school isn’t unique to Abjuration, which is a bit\n\tof a letdown. All in all, Abjuration is a so-so choice.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nAbjuration abilities<\/span>: Provide small energy\n\tresistance bonuses to yourself, or deflection AC bonuses (that will not stack\n\twith rings of protection) in a 10′ circle. Largely redundant with the spells\n\tyou are specialized in, rather than enhancing them. These abilities can save\n\tyou a spell or two, unfortunately they save you the very spells you are forced\n\tto memorize.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nConjuration School:<\/span> The best selections of\n\tspells for a God Wizard, conjuration has the largest range of effects\n\tincluded, has spells<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nthat target all three saving throws, and lots of spells that offer no save or\n\tspell resistance. In addition, the conjuration list is more centered on\n\tBattlefield Control than any other list. When it comes to spells, which is\n\treally what being a Wizard is all about, you can’t go wrong with a Conjuration\n\tSpecialty<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nConjuration Abilities:<\/span> OK. The Summoning\n\tduration boost will be pretty good at low levels, and become less of a factor\n\tat higher levels (until 20th, which is a nice ability). The acid dart isn’t\n\tgreat, but could be an OK fallback at low levels. The Dimensional Step is your\n\tway out of a situation where you are being grappled by a silenced foe. Maybe\n\tcircumstantial, but very handy in that position.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nDivination School:<\/span> My main problem with\n\tDivination Specialty is redundancy. Lots of divination spells do very similar\n\tthings with different ranges, or perception types, or casting times, but to a\n\tlarge extent, the whole school is built around finding out what is around the\n\tnext corner without having to go there. That’s a bit extreme, divination does\n\tother things as well, but by and large, that is what much of the school is\n\tbuilt around. Filling one spell of every level in divination can actually be a\n\tbit daunting. Divination is a great school to have, but a weak school to\n\tspecialize in.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nDivination abilities:<\/span> Easily the best\n\t“specialization” abilities in the game. Mainly because of the “forewarned”\n\tability that always allows you to act in the surprise round, and gives 1\/2\n\tyour wizard level as a bonus to initiative. Winning initiative as a Wizard is\n\tvery helpful, more so than any other class, so this ability is prime. The\n\tother two abilities are both less impressive, but OK. This makes the\n\tspecialization in this school a good choice all on its own.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nEnchantment School:<\/span> My problem with\n\tspecializing in enchantment is that Enchantment Spells are very much confined\n\tto Will-Save Mind-Affecting type spells, so when you fight something with a\n\thigh will save, you are disadvantaged, and when you fight something immune to\n\tmind-affecting spells (which is pretty common), you are hooped with pretty\n\tmuch your entire specialized school. This is a big Achilles heel, and why I\n\tdon’t particularly recommend enchantment specialization.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nEnchantment Abilities:<\/span> The Aura of Despair\n\tability is a decent debuff with no save, though a Bard can do it much faster,\n\tthe other abilities do not impress. Diplomacy, Bluff and Intimidate are\n\tnon-class skills tied to a dump-stat, so moderate bonuses to those skills\n\tisn’t stellar, and the Daze ability requires a touch attack, and although it\n\tseems to offer no saving throw, I’m vary wary on touch attacks (and it’s not a\n\tspell, so your familiar can’t do it for you.)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nEvocation School:<\/span> Yeah, I don’t like\n\tblasts, however, Evocation is far better than its reputation. Evocation is the\n\thome of Force spells, which come in more shades than Magic Missile. In fact,\n\tI’m a fan of the magic hand Spells, and force makes great walls too. Add in\n\tsome wind spells, protection spells, light and darkness spells, and more,\n\tevocation is actually a very good battlefield control school. Not a bad choice\n\tat all.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nEvocation abilities:<\/span> Make your blasts more\n\tblasty, or replace them with a really wimpy blast replacement (OK at low\n\tlevel), the only ability that I find tempting is the 8th level ability to\n\tcreate a short duration energy-wall effect (a short term wall of fire, but\n\tchoose your energy type)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nIllusion School:<\/span> There is much more to\n\tillusion than Silent Image and it’s progression. Invisibility spells, mirror\n\timage spells, all kinds of defenses. Illusion is good for Battlefield Control\n\tand defensive buffing, weak on debuffing. Overall, not a bad choice for\n\tspecialization.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nIllusion abilities:<\/span> Some fairly decent\n\tabilities here if not earth-shattering, adding duration after you finish\n\tconcentrating is going to be handy, and the invisibility power is a swift\n\taction, which is the big draw there. The Blindness ability is a low level\n\tability really, but OK at that point.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nNecromancy School:<\/span> Not my first choice, but\n\tNecromancy is a good debuff school, in addition, if you like undead critters,\n\tthis is the school for that (though Clerics are probably better). Battlefield\n\tControl and Buffing are pretty much non-existent in this school, which is a\n\tproblem for the God wizard. Still, there are a good number of good spells in\n\tthis school.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nNecromancy abilities<\/span>: Not great. Turn\n\tundead works better for classes that don’t dump CHA, and the touch attack\n\tdoesn’t prevent your target from touching you back…hard. The Blindsight\n\tability is nice, but is a standard action to activate which hurts.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nTransmutation school:<\/span> Transmutation is the\n\tschool of the buff. There are some nice debuffs to, but no real battlefield\n\tcontrol spells. Nevertheless, Transmutation is a very nice school, and a\n\tdecent choice for specialization.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nTransmutation abilities:<\/span> You can simulate\n\tsome of your spells to a lesser degree (ho-hum) and are given a long range\n\tattack that will be of moderate use at low levels only. Not much here.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nUniversalist School:<\/span> What can I say? Give up\n\tone spell casting of every level you can cast, so that spells from 2 schools\n\tof your choice don’t take double slots to memorize? This is an absolutely\n\tterrible, awful, dismal deal. Being a generalist in Pathfinder is a practice\n\tin masochism, don’t do it.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nUniversalist abilities:<\/span> Nothing here that\n\tgives you extra castings, so nothing even close to worth it. The 8th level\n\tability is nice, but nowhere near enough to make up for the pain of\n\tsignificantly reduced casting of spells.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

What Feats does God take?<\/h2>\n\n\nThe natural inclination of any player making a wizard is to jump to Metamagic\n\tor Item Creation feats first, and why not? After all, they are all feats\n\tspecially for casters, so shouldn’t they be your best option? Well, maybe they\n\tshould be.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\n

Metamagic Feats:<\/p>\n\n\n\nI guess the first thing I should mention about metamagic feats is that,\n\tgenerally speaking, a 3rd level spell plus 2 levels of metamagic is more\n\tpowerful than a normal 3rd level spell, but no more powerful than a 5th level\n\tspell (and very often, less powerful). So this leads to the question: Why am I\n\tspending feats so I can just fill my upper level slots with spells that aren’t\n\tany better than my upper level slots? Good question. In addition, if you are\n\tusing an offensive spell with metamagic, note that the DC will be based on the\n\toriginal spell level (unless you are using heighten)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThat said, some Metamagic feats are actually important to have access to.\n\tWhen you are silenced, it’s very important to have some non-verbal component\n\toptions, when you are paralyzed or movement restricted, some non-semantic\n\tspells are sure nice to have. When you get to high level, quicken spell is\n\talmost a must, allowing you to piggyback a lower level spell with a higher\n\tlevel one. I personally use extend all the time.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nHowever, this is where the metamagic rods come in. If you go to the Rods\n\tsection, you will see some followed by “X, lesser metamagic” these are the\n\tones you should jump at the chance to get. When you get silenced, even a\n\tlesser silent spell rod is going to give you lots of spell options\n\t(ummm..maybe dispel that silence?).<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nI’m not saying you shouldn’t take metamagic feats, but it’s easy to overvalue\n\tthem. Pick up a few through your career, but don’t overdo it, you’ll find you\n\taren’t getting enough out of them otherwise.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nEmpower Spell:<\/span> Skip it. Really, skip it. The\n\tclarification that Jason has given on this is that it only works for the die\n\troll, not for any additions to the die roll, and for a 2 level increase, I\n\tjust can’t see any case where it is worth it.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nEnlarge Spell:<\/span> Double the range of your\n\tspell. Note that “Sizes” are not increased, so you can’t enlarge a cone spell\n\tfor example. As a 1 level metamagic, it’s not too expensive, and I could\n\tpotentially see using it on a long range spell to snipe at super range,\n\thowever, really, probably a once in a blue moon kind of metamagic.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nExtend Spell:<\/span> I do recommend it. It’s one\n\tlevel for double the duration. Spells like Rope Trick are going to rely on\n\textend until you get to 8th level for example. I find myself using this on\n\tself buffs or utilities fairly regularly.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nHeighten Spell:<\/span> Mostly of use in loopholes\n\tand abuses, for regular use I just can’t recommend making a 3rd level spell a\n\t4th level spell, just so it’s treated as 4th level. You are almost universally\n\tbetter just memorizing a 4th level spell.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nMaximize Spell:<\/span> For the 3 levels this adds to\n\tthe spell, there is almost no spell I think maximize spell is worth it for.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nQuicken Spell:<\/span> At high level, using\n\tquickened spells is kind of a staple (even necessary), though I find that a\n\tlesser quicken rod can serve you nicely if you don’t want to spend the feat\n\tslot (or pay the 4 level increase)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nSilent Spell:<\/span> Although I don’t recommend\n\tthis for wizards (because you never know when you need it), a lesser silent\n\trod may be the best item for the cost you can get in the game. When you get\n\tsilenced, you draw the rod and cast away. For a sorcerer, this feat is\n\thandy.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nStill Spell:<\/span> Not available as a standard\n\tmetamagic rod unfortunately, but mainly for use if you are paralyzed, or\n\treally don’t want to be identified as the source of a spell (in these cases,\n\tStill spell will need to be used in conjunction with Silent Spell). Some\n\tmethod of casting silent spells (such as the Rod) should really be obtained\n\tfirst, as Silent Spell is more useful on its own than Still Spell is.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nWiden Spell:<\/span> Increasing a spells area by 100%\n\tsounds great, but 3 levels to increase – an absolutely terrible deal.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

Item Creation Feats:<\/h3>\n\n\nTo me, item creation feats are kind of like watching Martha Stewart (“Why buy\n\tyour magic items at the store, you can make them at home for a fraction of the\n\tcost!”). That’s pretty much it. Make your own magic items and save your money.\n\tIt’s like selling your feat slots. In a campaign where magic items are hard to\n\tcome by, you may actually need these feats to properly equip yourself and your\n\tparty, but you don’t have to like it. In the end, you aren’t going to get much\n\tfun out of item creation feats, and I personally almost never take them, but\n\tif you need to create, or you want to sell off your feat slots for cash,\n\there’s some options.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nBrew Potion:<\/span> Potions are bad, overpriced, and\n\trestricted for which spells you can apply. Avoid buying potions, and\n\tdefinitely avoid brewing them.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nCraft Magic Arms and Armor:<\/span> The most\n\tcommon magic items to find in the game, however, there could be some definite\n\tvalue to this if someone in your party uses a rare style of weapon, and you\n\tcan’t buy magic items, but for most wizards this isn’t worthwhile.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nCraft Rod:<\/span> Well you will definitely want\n\trods, however, usually you want the rod to cover a metamagic feat that you\n\tDON’T have. I think you should just buy your rods, they’re cheap.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nCraft Staff:<\/span> If you are going to item\n\tcreate, this is a good choice. Staves are expensive, and I’m not a big fan\n\tbecause of the cost, but at half price they are better. The important thing is\n\tyou can cast the spells on the staff with your full caster level, even when\n\tyou enchant the staff with a lesser caster level. Also there is no limit to\n\tthe level of spell you can put in the staff.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nCraft Wand<\/span>: Probably worse than craft staff\n\tin almost every way.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nCraft Wondrous Item:<\/span> Could be handy for a\n\tcampaign where you have trouble buying this stuff.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nForge Ring:<\/span> Very specialized item list,\n\treally, I can’t see this being worth a feat.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nScribe Scroll:<\/span> Actually one of the better\n\titem creation feats, and you’ve got it anyways. Use this to create scrolls of\n\tthose really circumstantial spells that you don’t want to memorize, but may\n\treally need someday.<\/p\n>\n\n\n

Other Feats you may want to consider:<\/h3>\n\n\nArcane Armor Training:<\/span> Only listed to tell\n\tyou not to get this. If you use this feat you must activate it every round you\n\tcast as a swift action – so no quickened spells for you. All this for armor\n\tthat you can effectively duplicate with spells. Besides – AC isn’t your best\n\tdefense anyways.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nAugment Summoning:<\/span> I love summoning, did I\n\tmention that? Augment Summoning adds a substantial bonus to your summoned\n\tcritters (+4 Str and Con), that’s 2 HP per HD, and a nice offensive boost. If\n\tyou are going to be summoning, this is worthwhile.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nArcane Strike<\/span>: God wizards don’t want this,\n\tit’s more for Gish types.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nCombat Casting:<\/span> Get a +4 to defensive\n\tcasting checks or checks to cast when grappled. You can’t get Skill Focus:\n\tConcentration anymore, since Concentration isn’t a skill – so this is really\n\tthe only feat to boost your defensive casting. Personally I avoid situations\n\twhere I would need to defensive cast like the plague, but defensive casting is\n\tharder in Pathfinder, so this may be worth the investment.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nDefensive Combat Training:<\/span> Use your HD\n\tinstead of your BAB when determining your CMD. I think this is a really good\n\tchoice for any wizard.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nEschew materials:<\/span> I wouldn’t jump at this\n\tfeat, but at higher levels when you start polymorphing into dragons, your\n\tcomponent pouch disappears.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nImproved Counterspell:<\/span> Counterspelling really\n\tisn’t very good most of the time, rather than ready a spell to counterspell,\n\tjust casting a spell is usually better.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nImproved Familiar:<\/span> I wouldn’t jump at this\n\tat the first opportunity, but when the 7th level critters become available,\n\tthe Mephits, Imp or Quasit are all great choices. If you take opposable\n\tthumbs, the ability to speak, and the Use Magic Device skill for example (or\n\tjust having the familiar throw the alchemists fire before you cast\n\tPyrotechnics) – opportunities abound. Be aware that you are giving up the\n\tfamiliar “bonus” you received with your old familiar.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nImproved Initiative:<\/span> Get this. Initiative is\n\tmore important for the party Wizard than any other party member, and even more\n\timportant for battlefield control spells.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nMounted Combat:<\/span> Specifically you want this\n\tonly if you use a phantom steed – if you do use a phantom steed, this is a\n\treally good feat to have. (Phantom steeds are squishy) For earlier levels,\n\tthis spell is really only one you want if the campaign is “horse-friendly”\n\tsince most horses will not accompany you down into the dungeon.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nNimble Moves:<\/span> losing a 5′ step in\n\tdifficult terrain can be bad when you are caught by a big pointy stick user.\n\tNimble Moves lets you take that important 5′ step away before turning him into\n\ta newt for his impudence.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nSpell Focus:<\/span> Increases the DC by 1 for one\n\tschool. Certain schools benefit more from this feat (Enchantment, Evocation,\n\tNecromancy, Conjuration), and certain ones less (Transmutation, Abjuration,\n\tDivination). Generally, you want Spell Focus Conjuration if you want Augment\n\tSummoning, then maybe one for your specialist school, depending on the\n\tschool.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nGreater Spell Focus:<\/span> Unless you really\n\tconcentrate on one school of spell, this probably isn’t worth it. Maybe in\n\tyour specialist school, depending on what school that is.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nSpell Mastery<\/span>: I’ve never had use of this,\n\tand the use is rather specific. If it allowed you to prepare all your spells\n\twithout a spellbook I would be all over it. Just make sure you get 2\n\tspellbooks as soon as you can. Illusion Spells and “Interaction”<\/h2>\n\n\nA lot of Illusion spells specifically only allow a save if the illusion is\n\tinteracted with. This seems fairly self-explanatory for something like an\n\tillusionary wall. If you touch the wall, you’ve interacted with it, and\n\ttherefore get a saving throw. How about if the illusion contains sound? If you\n\tmake an illusion of a roaring dragon, does hearing the roar constitute\n\tinteraction? This is when the rules become vague.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nI’m not aware of any Pathfinder Specific clarification, but Skip Williams did\n\tdo a clarification for 3.5 that makes a lot of sense, and I would recommend\n\tthat using Pathfinder, the same guidelines be used. (The article in question\n\thas been lost to time, sadly)<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nTo sum up, he says that a saving throw for an illusion that requires\n\tinteraction to provide a saving throw, should require either touching or\n\tstudying the illusion, and that should, under most circumstances, require an\n\taction. So for the Dragon example, if a character is to use a standard action\n\tto either study the image, or carefully listen to the roar, or carefully smell\n\tthe dragon’s stench, etc, then a saving throw is received. Just hearing the\n\troar by itself however, does not constitute “interaction”.<\/p\n>\n\n\n\nThis definition makes sense, and is as close to official as I think we can\n\thope for, so my ratings will assume that your DM uses this criteria. It never\n\thurts to check though, or at least make sure your DM gets a link to the\n\tarticle.<\/p\n>\n\n\n
