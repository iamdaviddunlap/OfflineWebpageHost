Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

A character and their animal companion acting in almost supernatural unison as a fighting style. We see this archetype everywhere: in stories, in movies, in video games, and in DnD 5e as the Beast Master Ranger. This subclass has a troubled past as, on release, it was practically unusable. Requiring your action every turn to perform the basic function of your identity is a crippling design decision, and it took WotC a long time to do something about that. Fortunately, they finally did.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

The Primal Companion \u201coptional\u201d feature is essentially a complete replacement of the mechanics of the subclass that fixes the entire problem. With these changes, it\u2019s a very satisfying way to live out the trope and still feel like you\u2019re meaningfully contributing to combat in an optimized way.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

This article is for the 2014 rules. For the 2024 rules, see our 2024 DnD Beast Master Ranger Subclass Guide<\/a>.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                Primal Companion Options<\/h5>\n\n\n\n

                Tasha’s Cauldron of Everything presents a replacement mechanic for the Beast Master’s Companion feature. Rather than selecting an animal with a published stat block, you pick from one of three generic beasts and you can describe it however you like. If you want a sabretooth tiger, a dinosaur, a komodo dragon, or just a dog, you’re using the same stat block.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                More important than simplifying the challenges of picking a companion, the\n\tnew Companion feature also corrects the action economy issues which were the\n\tfundamental problem which made the Beast Master such a poor option. Commanding\n\tyour companion is now done as a Bonus Action, allowing you to do interesting\n\tthings on your turn like attacking or casting spells without leaving your pet\n\tto stand adjacent to your enemies and Dodge.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Raising your companion from the dead now takes a spell slot (so long as you\n\tdo so within an hour of its death), allowing you throw your companion into\n\tcombat with little regard for its safey confident in the knowledge that you\n\tcan bring it right back to life good as new. Obviously that’s a horrible way\n\tto treat an animal that you’re supposed to be emotionally bonded with, but\n\tthis is a game, your companion is a class feature, and as a DM I wouldn’t make\n\ta fighter spend 8 hours feeding a wild longsword table scraps until they were\n\tfriends, so no one should hold rangers to the same standard.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Changing your companion is also much easier now. If you suddenly find\n\tyourself on a boat trip and want a suitable aquatic companion, you can change\n\tyour companion after a Long Rest. This may be hard for players who want to\n\tbond with a single animal, but you might choose to think of your ranger as\n\tmaster of all<\/em> beasts rather than a master of just one beast that you\n\tdrag around everywhere.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                The only shortcoming of the Primal Companion feature is the limited set of\n\tcompanion choices. None of them appear to work as mounts (though your DM might\n\tstill allow it), and no matter how you dress up the stats any two beast\n\tmasters are going to feel very similar to one another.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
                  \n\t
                • Beast of the Land<\/span>: Simple and iconic.\n\t\tWith 40 ft. land and climb speed, this thing can get around very quickly.\n\t\tThe damage will match or exceed a weapon, and Charge allows your companion\n\t\tto knock foes prone if they can get a running start. Unfortunately, since\n\t\tyour beast only gets one attack until you get Bestial Fury at level 11, it\n\t\tcan’t capitalize on knocking enemies prone on its own for half of the level\n\t\trange.<\/li>\n\t
                • Beast of the Sea<\/span>: Only viable in aquatic\n\t\tcampaigns. 5-foot land speed is not enough for your sharktopus to hobble\n\t\taround on land and be effective in combat. If you can somehow get its speed\n\t\tup to reasonable amount (look for spells like Longstrider with long\n\t\tdurations which don’t require Concentration), you may be able to make this\n\t\twork on land. If you can do it, the Beast of the Sea’s attacks grapple when\n\t\tthey hit, which is a powerful option not normally available to players.<\/li>\n\t
                • Beast of the Sky<\/span>: 60-foot fly speed and\n\t\tFlyby. Less hit points than the other options, but with the ability to\n\t\tremain perpetually out of melee reach that’s less of a problem. The damage\n\t\tis also slightly lower than Beast of the Land, but Beast of the Land deals\n\t\t6.5+PB on average and the Beast of the Sky’s 5.5+PB isn’t noticably lower.\n\t\tHaving a flying companion discourages you from using your companion as a\n\t\tDefender by interposing itself between you and your enemies, but Beast of\n\t\tthe Sky’s AC is just as good as the other options and if your companion’s\n\t\thit points are looking problematic it can use Flyby to withdraw to\n\t\tsafety.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/div><\/div>\n\n\n\n\n
