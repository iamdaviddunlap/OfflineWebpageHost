Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

Illusion is one of the most complex, open-ended, and flexible schools of\n\tmagic. It is limited only by your and your DM’s imaginations. If you are a\n\tcreative, artistic person who thinks on their feet, Illusions can be\n\texceptionally powerful. While there are spell options like Phantasmal Killer\n\tand Simulacrum which go well beyond the concept of sensory illusions (making\n\tstuff which appears to be real), School of Illusion strictly emphasizes\n\tsensory illusions, and gives you new options to make those spells more\n\tflexible, versatile, and powerful.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

However, the freeform nature of illusions also means that your spells place a great deal of burden on your DM to interpret their effects and how other creatures will respond to them. If your DM is the sort of person who will play along with your shenanigans, School of Illusion can be a ton of fun. But if your DM is a mechanical stickler or in any way adversarial or contrarian, you may find that illusions are difficult to use to great effect.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

I recommend reserving School of Illusion for games where the DM is reasonably experienced and comfortable running the game. It’s unkind to throw something so disruptive into a game where your DM is still getting comfortable with the game’s mechanics.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Assuming that an illusionist works in your game, they can be effective Controllers and Strikers. Spells like Invisibility and ample options for distraction and hiding may also make you an effective Scout. To get the most out of your illusion spells, be sure to read our Practical Guide to Illusions<\/a>.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

This article is for the 2014 rules. For the 2024 rules, see our 2024 DnD Illusionist Wizard Subclass Guide<\/a>.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        1. Illusion Savant<\/span>: Standard for PHB wizard\n\t\tsubclasses. Strangely, this feature incentivizes you to use your two free\n\t\tspells learned per level to learn literally anything except spells from your\n\t\tfavorite school.<\/li>\n\t
        2. Improved Minor Illusion<\/span>: Minor Illusion\n\t\tcan be incredibly powerful in the hands of a smart caster, and adding both\n\t\teffects on one casting makes it considerably more useful. Creating an\n\t\tillusion of a pot lying on the floor accompanied by the sound of a pot\n\t\tfalling, or an illusion of a small animal accompanied by appropriate sounds\n\t\t(I’m fond of chickens for this purpose) can be excellent distractions, and\n\t\tcreatures don’t get a save or a skill check to disbelieve the illusion until\n\t\tthey spend an Action to do so.<\/li>\n\t
        3. Malleable Illusions<\/span>: Most illusion spells\n\t\twhich create an ongoing illusory effect (Minor Image, Hallucinatory Terrain,\n\t\tetc.) do not allow you to change the illusion once it’s in place. Minor\n\t\tImage and Major Image allow you to move and animate the illusion, but that\n\t\tcan turn a red ball into a rolling or bouncing red ball, but nothing more.\n\t\tMalleable Illusions could turn your red ball into a statue of a dragon or\n\t\tsomething, effectively replacing your existing illusion without re-casting\n\t\tthe spell. This adds a ton of flexibility to such spells.<\/li>\n\t
        4. Illusory Self<\/span>: Infallibly ignore one\n\t\tattack against you for free, once per Short or Long Rest. Very good for a\n\t\tsquishy Wizard. Combined with other defensive measures like hiding and using\n\t\tcover, you may even be able to forgo Shield.<\/li>\n\t
        5. Illusory Reality<\/span>: This turns your\n\t\tillusions into creation spells, which means that any tool that you can\n\t\timagine is available for the low cost of casting Silent Image. Sadly,\n\t\tMalleable Illusions likely doesn’t let you change the object which you’ve\n\t\tchosen to make real because Illusory Reality only applies when you cast the\n\t\tspell.\n\t\t

          You can’t directly harm creatures with Illusory Reality, but the rules\n\t\t\tsay nothing about impeding, restraining, blocking, or even dropping them.\n\t\t\tThrow up a Silent Image to create a wall of stone between you and your\n\t\t\tfoes, or to put a cage around them. Create an illusory bridge with a\n\t\t\tconvenient trap door (or just time it so that they’re on the bridge when\n\t\t\tthe 1-minute duration expires) and drop your enemies into a pit.<\/p><\/li>\n<\/ol>\n\n\n\n\n

            Example Build – Everything is Cake<\/s> an Illusion<\/h2>\n\n\n

            In this build we’ll lean into illusions as control options and as a tool for scouting, allowing us to function similarly to a rogue outside of combat, then switch to crowd control when it’s time to fight.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

            <\/div>\n\n\n\n

            Ability Scores<\/h3>\n\n\n

            We’ll mostly use the ability scores recommended in the Wizard Handbook. Sensory illusion spells like Minor Illusion barely care about your spellcasting DC, but many illusion spells do, so we do still need to invest in our Intelligence.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

            The one change we’ll make is switching Wisdom and Charisma because the Kenku’s Mimicry is Charisma-based. We’ll put out +2 in Intelligence and the remaining +1 wherever we want, then try not to let an odd-numbered ability score both us for the rest of our career.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
