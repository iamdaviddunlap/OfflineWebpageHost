Monstrous Companions Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

MCDM Productions’\n\tBeastheart and Monstrous Companions<\/a>\n\tintroduces Monstrous Companions, a system for bringing a monster into the\n\tparty as a pet. The system is a bit more complex than using the official\n\tSidekick rules, but that additional complexity brings some fun mechanics which\n\tcan make it very satisfying to play with a companion in the party.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Tasha’s Cauldron of Everything gave us a satisfying solution for the Beast\n\tMaster Ranger, and the Drakewarden Ranger gave us a way to get a pet dragon.\n\tThe sidekick rules gave us a way to bring many low-CR creatures into the party\n\tin a generic fashion, but only works for creatures of CR up to 1\/2, which\n\tseverely limits your options. Monstrous Companions offers a system for making\n\tmonsters work in the party.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

As of writing this article, we’ve only seen 15 companion options, but that’s\n\talready an impressively diverse roster. The existing options fit a broad range\n\tof personal tastes and tactical options, and MCDM has done a good job of\n\tmaking each creature function in a way that’s thematically and mechanically\n\tunique.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

While I wouldn’t bring this rules into every game, I like the advice offered\n\tin the document of comparing the companion to a powerful magic item. If one of\n\tyour players doesn’t have enough buttons to push in combat (maybe they’re\n\tplaying a very simple build like a champion fighter), a Monstrous Companion\n\tcan be both a wonderful storytelling device and a great mechanical addition to\n\ta character. Unfortunately, the optimization aspect may make it difficult for\n\tevery character to be a good caretaker, but if you really want to go this\n\troute you could give your player proficiency in Animal Handling somehow.<\/p>\n\n\n

        Understanding the Mechanics<\/h2>\n\n\n

        If you’re familiar with the Beast Companion Options presented as an Optional\n\tRule for the Ranger in Tasha’s Cauldron of Everything, many parts of the\n\tMonstrous Companion will feel familiar. Much like the Beast Companion, the\n\tMonstrous Companion’s stats are modified by the Caregiver’s level and\n\tProficiency Bonus, and commanding them works the same way.<\/p>\n\n\n

        The Caregiver<\/h3>\n\n\n

        Each Monstrous Companion will have exactly one caregiver. The caregiver is\n\tresponsible for commanding the companion and for preventing it from going on a\n\trampage. While anyone in the party can fill this role, a few criteria can help\n\tthe party select who makes the best caregiver.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          \n
        • Animal Handing. Wisdom (Animal Handling) checks are used to prevent\n\t\trampages, so a high bonus is essential.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
        • Bonus Actions. Commanding the companion requires a Bonus Action unless you\n\t\tjust want to let it rampage, so characters who lean on their Bonus Action\n\t\theavily are a poor choice for caregiver.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
        • High Level. The companion’s stats are dependent on the caretaker’s level\n\t\tand Proficiency Bonus, so in parties with varying level it’s help for the\n\t\thighest-level character to be the caretaker.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

          The criteria mean that a cleric, a druid, or a ranger are often good\n\tcandidates for caregiver depending on their build and their combat tactics.\n\tAnd of course, the Beastheart is built around Monstrous Companions, so they’re\n\tan obvious choice.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Commanding the Companion<\/h3>\n\n\n

          Commanding the Monstrous Companion takes a Bonus Action. If the companion\n\tdoesn’t receive commands, it will act on its own. Unlike official pet\n\tmechanics (more recent summoning spells, Beast Companions, etc.), the\n\tMonstrous Companion is not limited to the Dodge action. The Monstrous\n\tCompanion can Dash, Dodge, or Disengage on its own, it can take its Bonus\n\tAction on its own. There’s also the possibility of a Rampage.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Because companions takes their turn during the Caretaker’s turn, the\n\tCaretaker can intersperse their own actions with their companion’s. For\n\texample, the Caretaker might move, command their companion as a Bonus Action\n\t(which causes their companion to then take their turn), move a bit more, spend\n\ttheir own Action to do something, then move some more.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Ferocity and Rampage<\/h3>\n\n\n

          Ferocity is the Monstrous Companion’s in-combat resource pool. It accumulates\n\twhen the Caretaker and the Companion’s shared turn starts, and is spent when\n\tthe companion takes a Ferocity Action, when a Beastheart uses a Primal\n\tExploit, when it completes a Rampage, or when combat ends. Managing the pool\n\tof ferocity is a crucial part of playing a caregiver.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Generally you want your companion’s Ferocity as high as possible, but 10 or\n\thigher gets into dangerous territory because it can trigger a rampage. The\n\thigher your Animal Handling bonus is, the safer you are at 10 Ferocity or\n\tmore. Fortunately, if your companion is surrounded by enemies who are causing\n\tits Ferocity to add up, a rampage will typically result in it attacking an\n\tadjacent enemy, so other than losing Ferocity the danger is minimal unless you\n\thave allies fighting adjacent to the companion or riding it. The damage bonus\n\tfrom their lost Ferocity also makes this attack a little bit more impactful\n\tthan usual, so a rampage isn’t a total loss.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          In order to collect Ferocity as quickly as possible, it’s helpful to position\n\tyour companion adjacent to multiple foes. Ferocity Actions cost 2, 5, or 8\n\tFerocity, so if you can keep an enemy in melee with your companion, that will\n\tmean that you can almost always use a Ferocity Action rather than resorting to\n\tthe companion’s Signature Attack (the minimum Ferocity gained is 1d4+1 with\n\tone adjacent enemy). You start combat with your companion at 0 Ferocity, so\n\ttheir first turn will almost certainly be spent moving into melee and maybe\n\tmaking a Signature Attack if they don’t need to Dash.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          At the end of combat your companion heals an amount equal to their current\n\tFerocity, so if you were amassing Ferocity and didn’t use it, don’t feel bad\n\tabout it. Your companion needs to not be dying, but if they’re stabilized\n\t(grab a Healer’s Kit) and have some Ferocity, they’ll stand up at the end of\n\tcombat with a few hit points. That feels a little silly, but that’s 5e for\n\tyou. It’s no sillier than using Healing Word every round so your fighter can\n\tbe repeatedly beaten unconscious without consequence.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Cleverly, MCDM has included a sidebar on the “Bag of Rats” abuse case. They\n\tdon’t want players using that to abuse the Ferocity rules, and encourage DMs\n\tto tell players that it simply doesn’t work. This is good DM advice, and I’m\n\thappy that MCDM was smart enough to encourage a social fix to the abuse case\n\trather than imposing some convoluted mechanical solution to prevent me from\n\twaving rats over my pet basilisk to intentionally make them mad in combat.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Ferocity Actions<\/h3>\n\n\n

          Each companion has three Ferocity Actions which consume Ferocity in order to\n\tdo something cooler than their Signature Attack. These are all performed as an\n\tAction, so your companion can only do one per turn.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          The first Ferocity Action is immediately available and costs 2 Ferocity. It’s\n\tbasically your companion’s Signature Attack, but spicy. It’ll apply the\n\tcaretaker’s Proficiency Bonus as additional damage (meaning that you’ll add PB\n\tto damage twice for that attack), plus it will have a secondary effect like\n\trepositioning the target.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          The second Ferocity Action unlocks when the caretaker reaches level 3 and\n\tcosts 5 Ferocity. These Ferocity Actions are more unique to the companion than\n\ttheir first Ferocity Action, and they’re much more impactful.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          The third Ferocity Action unlocks when the caretaker reaches level 5 and\n\tcosts 8 Ferocity. The high cost makes these actions difficult to use early in\n\ta fight, but if you can raise Ferocity to 8 or more within one fight, the\n\teffects are excellent.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Barding<\/h3>\n\n\n

          The sidebar on page 6 describes the rules used for barding for Monstrous\n\tCompanions. Normally your companion adds the caregiver’s Proficiency Bonus to\n\ttheir AC, but barding replaces that bonus. A character’s Proficiency Bonus\n\twill be at most +6 (+7 if you have an Ioun Stone of Master, but that’s a\n\tLegendary item), so for some companions you should consider armor just as you\n\twould for a player character.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Many companions will have a high base AC, such as the Basilisk Companion,\n\twhich has an impressive 15+PB AC. For those companions, armor isn’t especially\n\thelpful.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Riding Your Companion<\/h3>\n\n\n

          5e’s Mounted Combat rules are one of my favorite punching bags (See my\n\tPractical Guide to Mounted Combat<\/a>\n\tand\n\tRPGBOT.Podcast Episode 2<\/a>), and I think MCDM managed to find a workable solution.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          First, and perhaps most important, companions and caretakers take their turns\n\tat the same time. No more worrying about timing and readying actions just to\n\tmake a mount work in melee. Even better, your companion can move and take the\n\tDash, Dodge, and Disengage actions without you spending a Bonus Action to\n\tcommand it! That means that you can use your companion for hit-and-run melee\n\tattacks without cutting into your action economy.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          If you use your Bonus Action, you can even have your companion attack. This is especially effective for companions like the bulette which can forcibly move enemies and for the deinonychus which can prevent the target from taking Reactions. Just keep in mind that hit-and-run tactics will typically leave your companion out of melee with enemies (that’s the whole point of hit-and-run tactics) so they won’t be accumulating ferocity as quickly. They still get 1d4 per turn, but if you want more you’ll need to stick around in melee.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Monstrous Companion Options<\/h2>\n\n\n

          The Mystic Connection feature is exclusive to the Beastheart, and they\n\trecieve the feature at level 9. Other caretakers cannot benefit from this\n\tfeature and shouldn’t consider it when picking a companion.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Basilisk<\/h3>\n\n\n\n

          With 15+PB AC and good hit points, the basilisk is a sturdy melee threat. Its\n\tattacks aren’t especially dangerous, and their Ferocity Actions rely on poison\n\tand Constitution saves, both of which face challenges dues to the prevalence\n\tof poison resistance\/immunity and due to Constitution saves being consistently\n\thigh, which doesn’t help in the face of monstrous companions’ meager 10+PB\n\tsave DC. Even the Basilisk’s Heavy Glare Reaction targets a Constitution\n\tsave.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          The Basilisk is notably proficient in Athletics, shoving enemies prone is an\n\toption. It won’t keep up with a character for Athletics, but honestly it\n\tdoesn’t need to if you just need it to occasionally knock someone over.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Altogether, the basilisk is a good option for parties that need an additional\n\tfront-line character, but many of its features won’t be reliable for anyone\n\texcept a Beastheart who can raise its save DCs with Master Caregiver.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Heavy Glare<\/span>: This can help frail allies\n\tescape melee and keep melee enemies from moving into reach, provided that the\n\tConstitution saves aren’t a problem.<\/p>\n

          Mystic Connection<\/span>: Absolutely amazing. Use\n\tthis, jump in front of your party, and draw attacks.<\/p>\n\n\n

          Ferocity Actions<\/h4>\n\n\n
          • Poison Spittle<\/span>: A bit of extra damage to\n\t\tthe main target and some damage to a secondary target. Poison resistance is\n\t\tcommon, unfortunately, and the positioning can be difficult since the\n\t\tsecondary target needs to be within 5 feet of the primary target.<\/li>
          • Poison Gaze<\/span>: I like that this targets 3\n\t\tcreatures, because it means that something might actually fail the Con save\n\t\tagainst your companion’s low save DC. Even so, it face issues with poison\n\t\tresistance\/immunity being common on top of Monstrous Companion’s typically\n\t\tpoor save DC and Constitution saves being high on average.<\/li>
          • Lesser Petrifying Gaze<\/span>: For the cost, this\n\t\tis disappointingly unreliable. Constitution saves tend to be high and your\n\t\tcompanions save DCs are low, so enemies passing this save should be the\n\t\texpectation. I understand why the designers didn’t want to give players a\n\t\tsave-or-suck option that could be recharged by standing still, but I don’t\n\t\tthink this is worth the Ferocity unless you’re a beastheart and raise the save DC.<\/li><\/ul>\n\n\n\n

            Blood Hawk<\/h3>\n\n\n\n

            A great striker, but very frail. Fewer hit points than most companions, and\n\tit’s riding entirely on Dexterity for AC. If you DM allows it, grab it the\n\theaviest medium armor you can get. Putting a hawk into half plate feels\n\tabsolutely ridiculous, but 17 AC will exceed its unarmored AC for a long time.\n\tFlight is a powerful advantage for the Blood Hawk, but remember that Ferocity\n\tonly recharges while within 5 feet of an enemy, so your companion will need to\n\tspend time in melee rather than relying entirely on hit-and-run attacks.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

            The Blood Hawk’s Reaction allows it to take a hit in place of its caretaker.\n\tThis is thematically excellent, and the caretaker will almost certainly be\n\tmore effective than the companion, so having the hawk take damage to prevent\n\tthe caretaker from dropping to 0 is a good tactical decision.\n<\/p>\n\n\n\n

            While it’s only proficient in one skill, the Blood Hawk’s proficiency in\n\tPerception and Keen Sight make it excellent at locating enemies. Coupled with\n\tflight, it’s easy to have your hawk fly above the party to watch for\n\tdanger.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

            The Blood Hawk is an easy fit into almost any party since additional damage\n\toutput is always welcome. The hawk is frail and locked into melee, especially\n\tif you want to charge Ferocity, so you need to be careful about not letting\n\tyour companion draw too many attacks.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

            Swoop In<\/span>: Having your companion tank a hit\n\tfor you is a risky move for most caretakers, so use it cautiously. For the\n\tBeastheart, it takes one minute to bring your companion back to life, so if\n\tthey “die” it’s really not a big deal as long as you and your party can get\n\tthrough the rest of the fight in one piece.<\/p>\n

            Mystic Connection<\/span>: An hour of flight will\n\tget you through several encounters and over or around many obstacles. It’s not\n\tquite enough for long-distance travel, but for basically anything else this is\n\tfantastic.<\/p>\n\n\n

            Ferocity Actions<\/h4>\n\n\n
            • Distracting Attack<\/span>: Great if your party\n\t\thas big single attacks to follow this with. If you have a rogue in the party\n\t\tor if someone has high-damage attack spells like Inflict Wounds, this is an\n\t\teasy way to grant Advantage in a hurry.<\/li>
            • Swooping Attack<\/span>: Situational. Most\n\t\tenemies don’t hold items. Against the ones who do, disarming them of a\n\t\tweapon, spellcasting focus, or potentially a shield can seriously hinder\n\t\tthem.<\/li>
            • Storm of Talons<\/span>: Good damage and\n\t\tpotentially blind the target. Against big melee foes (which frequently have\n\t\tpoor Dexterity saves) this is an excellent option. Keep in mind that your\n\t\thawk is left within reach of the target, so unless they’re blinded the hawk\n\t\tmoving away will provoke opportunity attacks.<\/li><\/ul>\n\n\n\n

              Bulette<\/h3>\n\n\n\n

              Good AC, tremorsense, a burrow speed, and you can ride it while it burrows.\n\tThe Bulette’s attack options are good, but mostly rely on moving creatures or\n\tknocking them prone.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              This is a great addition for a melee-heavy party, and despite its 30-foot\n\tspeed the Bulette is a great mount option. Just remember that you don’t gain\n\tany special ability to breath underground.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Plated Protection<\/span>: The ability to ride your\n\tbulette while it burrows is an amazing tactical advantage. Just remember that\n\tyou can probably neither breath nor see while underground.<\/p>\n

              Mystic Connection<\/span>: Most creatures can’t do\n\tanything to you while you’re underground, so a burrow speed is a fantastic way\n\tto get in or out of a place undetected, to hide from dangerous enemies, or\n\tgenerally just to travel without being bothered. This is somewhat redundant\n\tsince you can just ride your bulette, but if someone else is riding them or if\n\tit’s not safe for you to ride them at the moment, this is a great substitute.\n\tKeep in mind that you don’t have any additional ability to see or breath while\n\tburrowing, so you’ll need to pop your head out of the ground periodically like\n\tgiant gopher to take a breath, look around, and shout commands to your\n\tcompanion. Conveniently, 5e’s rules allow you to stay buried between rounds,\n\tmove above ground to act, then dart back underground before your turn ends,\n\tmaking you hard to target and infuriating your DM beyond belief.<\/p>\n\n\n

              Ferocity Actions<\/h4>\n\n\n
              • Violent Attack<\/span>: Repositioning enemies\n\t\tisn’t always necessary, but it’s a useful tactical option at a low cost. You\n\t\tcan reposition enemies to break grapples or to move them away from your\n\t\tcompanion so that it can avoid opportunity attacks.<\/li>
              • Burrowing Trip<\/span>: Unless you have allies\n\t\twho can capitalize on the targets being prone, this isn’t helpful.\n\t\tCaretakers share turns with their companion so at least the caretaker can\n\t\trush in to make a melee attack, but otherwise it comes down to where your\n\t\tmelee allies landed in the initiative order.<\/li>
              • Deadly Leap<\/span>: A 30-foot leap on top of the\n\t\tbulette’s movement should get it into melee. If you can target multiple\n\t\tenemies with this, you’ll put the bulette in a good position to recharge its\n\t\tFerocity. The damage is decent, especially if you can hit more than one\n\t\ttarget, but it’s not going to win any fights on its own, so be sure to\n\t\tfollow this with attacks by the rest of the party.<\/li><\/ul>\n\n\n\n

                Deinonychus<\/h3>\n\n\n\n

                A striker, plain and simple. The Deinonychus’s AC is poor, and putting it in\n\tmedium or heavy armor will make Clever Girl unreliable. Breastplate will raise\n\tits AC to 16, but that’ll be matched by its unarmored AC once you hit level 5,\n\tso there’s a narrow window where you can buy your dinosaur slightly better\n\tarmor.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                The Deinonychus makes a good mount for a small rider. With 40-foot move speed it’ll outrun most things (not horses). Overwhelming Attack makes it easy to break melee, Clever Girl is a great way to ambush targets, and Keep Them Down holds foes prone so that you can beat on them with Advantage from the comfort of the saddle.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Part of the Pack<\/span>: Technically situational,\n\tbut undeniable effective. Normally, removing those conditions requires a spell\n\tcast as an Action. You don’t need to spend actions to command your Monstrous\n\tCompanion to use their Bonus Action, so you can even use this while\n\tstunned.<\/p>\n

                Mystic Connection<\/span>: Neat, but it doesn’t\n\twork often enough that you can make this a staple part of your tactics. More\n\tlikely you’ll use this when you’re injured and need to get out of harm’s way,\n\tor when stealth is unusually helpful for some reason.<\/p>\n\n\n

                Ferocity Actions<\/h4>\n\n\n
                • Overwhelming Attack<\/span>: Not always\n\t\timportant, but this does allow your whole party to move away from the target\n\t\tsafely, which can get frail allies out of melee or allow your party to rush\n\t\tback-line enemies more easily.<\/li>
                • Clever Girl<\/span>: The intent here is to hide,\n\t\tmove, then attack with Advantage, or to attack, knock the target prone, then\n\t\trun away and hide. Either option is good, and knocking the target prone\n\t\twithout a further check or save is excellent. However, it encourages\n\t\thit-and-run attacks which can make it difficult to accumulate Ferocity.<\/li>
                • Keep Them Down<\/span>: An excellent way to\n\t\teliminate single targets, including big melee brutes that are often good at\n\t\tavoiding grapples. The damage is excellent compared to other 5th-level\n\t\tFerocity Actions, and it leaves the target extremely vulnerable to other\n\t\tmelee attacks. The text “A creature knocked prone by this feature can\u2019t\n\t\tstand up until they are no longer grappled.” is unnecessary since that’s\n\t\talready true. You can’t expend movement with a speed of 0, so you can’t\n\t\tstand.<\/li><\/ul>\n\n\n\n

                  Dragon Wyrmling<\/h3>\n\n\n\n

                  Good AC. Flight, but not very fast. A ranged attack and a breath weapon, but\n\tlittle tactical application beyond damage. The companion gets a damage\n\timmunity and grants resistance to the caretaker. The wyrmling is a decent all\n\taround companion that will fit into any party, but it doesn’t excel in any\n\tspecific capacity.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                  Since it’s medium and can fly, the wyrmling makes a suitable mount for small\n\tcreatures. Just keep in mind that if you keep it out of melee it won’t\n\trecharge Ferocity very quickly. If you’re okay with that, you and your dragon\n\tcan fight at range safely using Spit Breath and whatever ranged weapon you\n\twant to wave about.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                  If you’re familiar with dragons in Dungeons and Dragons, the fact that the\n\twyrmling only has 5 Intelligence is a little bit odd. Dragon wyrmlings are\n\ttypically at least human-level intelligent and already speak draconic (except\n\tfor white dragons, who are barely smarter than most animals), so this wyrmling\n\tis apparently so freshly hatched that it’s less intelligent than a typical\n\twyrmling. Just ignore it. You want a dragon in your party? You’ll need to make\n\tsome compromises.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                  Shared Resistance<\/span>: Persistent damage\n\tresistance is always nice. Getting the resistance constantly may be enough to\n\tjustify picking fire or poison as youre companion’s damage type.<\/p>\n

                  Mystic Connection<\/span>: 1d10 is a decent damage\n\tboost, and curiously this feature doesn’t require you to be the one to use the\n\tweapon, just that you’re the one holding it when you use this. Borrow your\n\tparty’s Fighter’s weapon, set it on fire (or acid or whatever) and hand it\n\tback. The 10-minute duration will get you through a fight or two with enough\n\twiggle room that you can activate this ahead of time and go looking for\n\ttrouble.<\/p>\n\n\n

                  Ferocity Actions<\/h4>\n\n\n
                  • Spit Breath<\/span>: A little extra damage and it\n\t\tworks at range. Unfortunately you can’t use it in melee without suffering\n\t\tDisadvantage, so your companion will need to pull itself out of melee to\n\t\tmake this effective.
