        College of Creation gives the Bard access to some powerful new tools. Mote of Potential expands on Bardic Inspiration by adding unique rider effects, and the other subclass features grant the ability to create and animate objects. As the Bard gains levels, you can create larger and more valuable items, allowing you to produce tools to solve a variety of problems.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Because half of the College of Creation’s class features are tied up in Performance of Creation, it’s crucial that you and your DM are on the same page about how it works. The effect is less specific than spells like Creation or Fabricate, which can make it both unpredictable and abusable. Your DM might reasonably limit you to published items (Adventurer’s league went that route), which dramatically handicaps the feature because the items published in the Player’s Handbook and other sources are tools and items catered to adventurers but typically omit common items which might be useful in real-world scenarios (though some variety of artisans tools suffice in many cases).<\/p>\n\n\n\n

You generally don’t\n\t\twant to use this in combat because you could spend your Bonus Action to draw\n\t\tsomething that’s not helpful.                     Since this costs an inspiration die and expires when you take a Short or\n\t\t\tLong Rest, it’s costly at levels 3 and 4, but it gets much much more\n\t\t\tmanageable at level 5 when you pick up Font of Inspiration. Even then,\n\t\t\tsome of the effects may not be worth the inspiration die to get them.<\/p>\n\t\t

                    Because you roll a Bardic Inspiration die to get your tale, your options\n\t\t\tare limited by the size of your die. The higher-numbered options are\n\t\t\tgenerally better, so there’s new stuff to look forward to as you gain\n\t\t\tlevels.                     2. Renowned Duelist<\/span>: Decent\n\t\t\t\tsingle-target damage, but it’s not much better than just casting a\n\t\t\t\tcantrip, especially once you add the damage bonus from Spiritual Focus\n\t\t\t\tat level 6.                     3. Beloved Friends<\/span>: Nearly always useful.\n\t\t\t\tThe temporary hit points don’t have a specified expiration time, so they\n\t\t\t\tlast until you rest. If you roll this, use it early when it’s easy to hit\n\t\t\t\ttwo targets and consider getting another Tale.<\/li>\n\t\t\t
                    4. Runaway<\/span>: Reposition your entire party.\n\t\t\t\tEscape grapples, bindings, and dangerous positions. You do need to be\n\t\t\t\table to see the destination, but that’s still enough to solve a lot of\n\t\t\t\tproblems.<\/li>\n\t\t\t
                    5. Avenger<\/span>: Throw this on your party’s\n\t\t\t\tDefender. It only works for melee attacks, unfortunately, but the damage\n\t\t\t\tfrom this can add up very quickly.<\/li>\n\t\t\t
                    6. Traveler<\/span>: Trade the second target\n\t\t\t\tfrom Beloved Friends for a +1 AC bonus and some extra speed. Given the\n\t\t\t\tchoice between the two and no other information I would likely take\n\t\t\t\tBeloved Friends, but in situations where enemies are making large\n\t\t\t\tnumbers of attacks a +1 AC bonus can make a big difference.<\/li>\n\t\t\t
                    7. Beguiler<\/span>: Rob a single target of the\n\t\t\t\timportant parts of their turn. I’d be excited about this even if it didn’t deal damage.<\/li>\n\t\t\t
                    8. Phantom<\/span>: Being invisible will \n\t\t\t\tgive your target Advantage on their attack, which is always welcome, and\n\t\t\t\tmay allow them to avoid things like Opportunity Attacks.                     9. Brute<\/span>: Turn an ally into the center\n\t\t\t\tof a fireball. The AOE is big, the damage type is good, and your target\n\t\t\t\tcan pick which creatures take damage.                     11. Angel<\/span>: Lesser Restoration plus a bit\n\t\t\t\tof healing. Not always required, but always handy.<\/li>\n\t\t\t

                    13. Spirit Session<\/span>: Similar in many ways to\n\t\tMagical Secrets, but considerably more complicated and with some additional\n\t\tlimitations in exchange for the ability to change the spell daily. Because\n\t\tthis only teaches you the spell temporarily, it’s great for odd, situational\n\t\tspells that you typically can’t justify learning permanently.                       The problem is easy to overlook (I did it on my initial read): Spiritual\n\t\t\tFocus’s bonus only works for spells which are cast through the focus,\n\t\t\twhich means that it only applies to spells with inexpensive material\n\t\t\tcomponents that aren’t consumed by the spell.                     15. Mystical Connection<\/span>: By this level you’re\n\t\trolling a d10 for inspiration, and you get d12 at level 15, so Tales From\n\t\tBeyond is totally unpredictable. Mystic Connection does a lot to address\n\t\tthat, but keeps enough unpredictability to keep Tales From Beyond \n\t\tfun and surprising.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n\n\n\n\n

                      College of Swords<\/a> (XGtE<\/a>)<\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n
                      <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                      The College of Swords Bard is the second attempt to bring martial capabilities to the Bard, leaning into light armor and finesse weapons and adding a splash of Battle Master to the mix. The result is an exciting mix, though there are certainly some challenges. Thematically similar to College of Valor, College of Swords places more emphasis on offense than College of Valor, offering access to Fighting Style and some interesting tactical options via Blade Flourish. However, the subclass’s resource management and the need to split focus between Dexterity and Charisma makes it very difficult to play without a Hexblade dip.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                      1. Bonus Proficiencies<\/span>: Medium armor is nice\n\t\tuntil you get to 18 or 20 Dexterity, but you don’t get shields, so your AC\n\t\twon’t be as good as a College of Valor Bard. Scimitars are useful if you\n\t\tplan to use two-weapon fighting, which looks viable thanks to\n\t\tFighting Style.                           • Mobile Flourish<\/span>: I would just assume\n\t\t\t\tthat you won’t get more than the base 5 feet of pushing since the\n\t\t\t\taverage roll of your inspiration die won’t exceed 5 feet until level 10,\n\t\t\t\tand even then your odds are only slightly better than 50%.                             College of Valor<\/a> (PHB<\/a>)<\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n
                            <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                            A martially-minded Bard may prefer College of Valor. By improving the Bard’s ability to wade into melee safely, the Bard can fill nearly every role in a party. If you’re in a small party, this is an exciting option. However, in a party of 4 or more the Valor Bard’s lack of focus will make it hard for the College of Valor Bard to truly shine.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            When DnD 5e was just the core rules, College of Valor allowed the bard to toe the line between sorcerer-like spellcasting and fighter-like martial capabilities. However, newer, similar options like the Bladesigner Wizard, the Hexblade Warlock, and the Artificer have all left College of Valor far behind them in effectiveness. It remains a simple, approachable bard option that’s both effective and appealing for newer players, but it’s not going to impress you mechanically.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                            1. Bonus Proficiencies<\/span>: Medium armor and a\n\t\tshield will significantly improve your AC. Half-plate will get you\n\t\tmore AC, but you might prefer to avoid Disadvantage on Stealth checks. If\n\t\tyou eventually get to 18 Dexterity, consider switching back to light armor.\n\t\tYou also get access to all martial weapons, but you’re probably going to\n\t\twant to stick to a Rapier, and all Bards get proficiency with rapiers.<\/li>\n\t

Combat Inspiration<\/span>: The ability to add\n\t\tthe inspiration die to damage is wasteful (unless it gets used on a critical\n\t\thit, in which case it’s multiplied since it’s “extra damage” rather than an\n\t\tadditional source of damage). You’ll have much better results using\n\t\tinspiration to prevent attacks. This isn’t quite as good as the College of\n\t\tLore’s Cutting Words ability, but it allows your allies to make the decision\n\t\tto use the die themselves, which is a nice mental load off of your\n\t\tshoulders.<\/li>\n\t

Psychic Blades<\/span>: This works with\n\t\tranged weapons, so you’re not forced to go swing a rapier, however, the\n\t\tdamage is pitiful compared to how useful a Bardic Inspiration die is. You\n\t\tcan decide to use this after rolling a critical hit so that you can double\n\t\tthe damage dice (similar to effects like Divine Smite or Hunter’s Mark), but\n\t\tCollege of Whispers never gets Extra Attack, so you’re gambling on a single\n\t\tattack each turn with a 5% chance to crit when you should be casting spells\n\t\tinstead. If you want direct single-target damage, you can find more\n\t\t\treliable and more damaging options on your spell list without the need to\n\t\t\tinvest in making weapon attacks reliable.<\/p>\n\t<\/li>\n\t

                              3. Mantle of Whispers<\/span>: Situational, but it’s\n\t\tnotably better than options like Disguise Self due to your ability\n\t\tto glean mundane information about the person you’re impersonating. I can’t\n\t\tthink of how many times a disguise has been foiled by something as simple as\n\t\tthe assumed identity’s associates attempting to make small-talk. You could\n\t\talso combine this with Words of Terror to infiltrate somewhere in disguise\n\t\tand get someone talking long enough to make them Frightened before your\n\t\tparty jumps out of a closet or something.<\/li>\n\t
                              4. Shadow Lore<\/span>: Once per day you get a\n\t\tsomewhat diminished version of Dominate Monster<\/em> with an 8-hour\n\t\tduration.
