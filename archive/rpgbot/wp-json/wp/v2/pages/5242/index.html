Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

The Brawler<\/a> fighter archetype focuses on distraction, hindrance, and close combat. The fighter trades their signature weapon and armor training for focus on the Close weapon group, some nice crowd control effects, and some minor debuffs. Altogether, the Brawler is good at battlefield control, disabling single targets, and hunting spellcasters. They may lack some of the basic damage of other fighter builds, but they make up for it in utility.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Since spiked shields count as Close weapons, brawlers make an excellent sword-and-board duel-wielding fighters. They won’t be using big two-handed weapons or reach weapons, so we’re going to rely on Two-weapon Fighting to supplement our damage output.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Brawlers also get bonuses to bull rush, drag, and reposition combat maneuver\n\tchecks. These maneuvers require Power Attack instead of combat expertise, and\n\tcan get let us do some fun things in combat.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        Fighter Class Features<\/h2>\n\n\n

        Hit Points<\/span>: Between your d10 hit points and\n\tyour heavy armor, you are very durable.<\/p>\n

        Base Attack Bonus<\/span>: The biggest and best in\n\tthe game.<\/p>\n

        Saves<\/span>: Your fortitude saves are good, but\n\tyou will need to invest in your other saves.<\/p>\n

        Proficiencies<\/span>: You get the best\n\tproficiencies of any class.<\/p>\n

        Skills<\/span>: Fighters only get 2+ skills, and very\n\tfew of your skill choices are worthwhile, so you will likely dump\n\tintelligence.<\/p>\n

        Bonus Feats<\/span>: The main reason to play a\n\tfighter. Everything a fighter would want to take is a combat feat, and there\n\tare enough options that you never have to play the same fighter twice.<\/p>\n

        Bravery<\/span>: The brawler is one of very few\n\tfighter archetypes which doesn’t trade in Bravery for something more\n\tinteresting. A bonus to fear saves is very rarely useful, but it certainly\n\tdoesn’t hurt.<\/p>\n\n\n

        Archetype Features<\/h2>\n\n\n

        Proficiencies<\/span>: Fighters are proficient with\n\tbasically everything except exotic weapons. Some Close weapons are exotic, but\n\tthey are generally not worth a feat.<\/p>\n

        Skills<\/span>: Fighters get 2+ skills, and have a\n\tvery short skill list.<\/p>\n

        Close Control (Ex)<\/span>: Bull Rush, Drag, and\n\tReposition are only situationally useful, and all do effectively the same\n\tthing: move the enemy from point A to point B. If you spend the feats, you can\n\tforce the enemy to draw attacks of opportunity when you move them. Unless your\n\tparty is full of rogues with combat reflexes, you’re probably better of just\n\thitting things instead of dragging them around between your party.<\/p>\n

        Close Combatant (Ex)<\/span>: The bonus to attacks\n\tis basically the same as weapon training. The +3 damage is two points higher\n\tthan the normal weapon training bonus, which makes Close weapons roughly\n\tequivalent to most one-handed weapons in terms of damage. The close weapon\n\tgroup has very few fantasic options, which is unfortunate. I’m still confused\n\twhy Daggers aren’t a Close weapon.<\/p>\n

        Menacing Stance (Ex)<\/span>: This is the first\n\tsignature ability for the brawler. It makes it hard to hit you in melee, and\n\tmakes you very effective at locking down spellcasters. Combined with the Step\n\tUp and Disruptive feats, you are going to be very problematic for\n\tspellcasters. The scaling penalties keep this ability relevant into higher\n\tlevels.<\/p>\n

        No Escape (Ex)<\/span>: Combined with Step Up, this\n\tability is absolutely fantastic. Not only can people not get away from you,\n\tyou get an attack of opportunity when they try. If they try to withdraw, make\n\ta trip attempt. If they do manage to withdraw, just charge them next round.<\/p>\n

        Stand Still<\/span>: You should already have Combat\n\tReflexes and Stand Still by now, so this is basically another combat feat. The\n\treal benefit here is the bonus on combat maneuver checks when using Stand\n\tStill.<\/p>\n

        Weapon Mastery (Ex)<\/span>: Decent, but\n\tconsidering no close weapon has a threat range greater than 20, it’s not going\n\tto see a lot of use. Your CMD should be insane by level 20, so being disarmed\n\treally shouldn’t be a problem.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

        Abilities<\/h2>\n\n\n

        Str<\/span>: Any melee character’s\n\tbread-and-butter, but we need high dex to get the two-weapon fighting\n\tfeats.<\/p>\n

        Dex<\/span>: Dexterity opens up two-weapon fighting.\n\tSince you don’t get armor training, heavy armor is less appealing, which makes\n\tmedium armor attractive. Dex also adds additional effect to Combat Reflexes,\n\twhich can get some serious mileage on a brawler.<\/p>\n

        Con<\/span>: You have d10 hit points and strong\n\tfortitude saves, but you’re a front-line tank, so you need them both.<\/p>\n

        Int<\/span>: You may want 13 for Combat Expertise,\n\tbut won’t benefit much from anything past that. Your skill list is garbage, so\n\textra skill points really won’t get you much. If you’re not using combat\n\texpertise, dump to 7.<\/p>\n

        Wis<\/span>: The only real benefit you get here is\n\twill saves. While will saves are certainly important, your other abilities\n\tneed more focus. Plus, you can always take Iron Will if you need it.<\/p>\n

        Cha<\/span>: Dump to 7 or 8. Even if you plan on\n\tintimidating, it’s not worth wasting crucial ability score points on\n\tcharisma.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
