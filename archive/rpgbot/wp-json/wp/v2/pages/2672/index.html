        Arcane Trickster<\/a> (PHB<\/a>)<\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n
        <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

        The Arcane Trickster brings magic to the Rogue. Enchantment and Illusion\n\tspells both offer fantastic options, giving the Arcane Trickster powerful\n\toptions utility and trickery, but also bringing powerful offensive magic\n\toptions.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

        Because it’s the only rogue subclass with a spell list (or with any decision\n\tpoints made after level 3), the Arcane Trickster is the most complex rogue\n\tsubclass to build and in many ways it’s also the most complex to play because\n\tyou have so many options to consider in any given situation.           Assassin<\/a> (PHB<\/a>)<\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n
          <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

          Assassins are good at two things: Infiltration and (you guessed it)\n\tassassination. If you want to get into places unsuspected or just straight up\n\tmurder people, the Assassin is the way to go. However, they lack utility\n\toptions for handling situations outside their specialized skillset.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Assassins have a weird divergence in their subclass features where Assasinate\n\tand Death Strike emphasize frequent combat, while Infiltration Expertise and\n\tImposter both work in campaigns where you’re doing a lot of spying,\n\tinfiltration, and intrigue. This doesn’t stop them from being excellent in\n\tyour typical dungeon crawl, but if you’re going from levels 1 to 20 in a full\n\tcampaign you may rarely see some features come into play.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          Because their premise and their features are so simple, the Assassin is one of the easiest rogue subclasses to play. They don’t have resources to track or complicated additional mechanics, so if you’re comfortable with the complexity of the Rogue’s core features you’re already ready to handle the Assassin.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

            Inquisitive<\/a> (<\/sup>XGtE<\/a>)<\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n
            <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

            If you want to be really good at Insight and Perception, this is the way to\n\tdo it. Combined with Expertise there’s no one who could reasonably compete\n\twith the Inquisitive’s powers of perception.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Mastermind<\/a> (SCAG \/ XGtE<\/a>)<\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n
              <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

              The Mastermind Rogue works best in a game with a lot of intrigue and social interactions. The only feature which applies in combat situations or in a dungeon crawl or something is Master of Tactics; everything else is for social situations. You can scrape by on core Rogue class features, but outside of a highly social campaign, the Mastermind is going to struggle compared to other Rogues. Even in campaigns where the Mastermind is well suited, you will need to work to make your features meaningful and your DM will need to tailor the game to your capabilities.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Phantom<\/a> (TCoE<\/a>)<\/sup><\/h3>\n\n\n\n
                <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

                Okay, but what if Ouija boards were a subclass? What if, like, you got class\n\tfeatures from talking to dead people? And what if those class features\n\tincluded necrotic damage and turning into a ghost? Enter the Phantom.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                The Phantom is a novel exploration of interactions with the dead as a class\n\tfeature, making necrotic damage and death-related stuff available to a class\n\twith no ability to cast spells. Normally, interacting with the dead is\n\tprimarily done by clerics and certain necromancy spells. The Phantom has a\n\tmuch more direct connection to the spirits of the dead, gaining proficiencies,\n\tdamage output, and the ability to turn (mostly) into a ghost.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                The Phantom’s most novel and interesting mechanic is their pool of Soul\n\tTrinkets. These trinkets allow the Phantom to fuel their most interesting\n\tfeatures by gathering trinkets when creatures die nearby, then spend those\n\ttrinkets for extra damage or to ask dead creatures questions. Tragically, this\n\tfeature doesn’t become available until 9th level. Waiting for creatures to die\n\trather than being reduced to 0 hit points also makes it important for the DM\n\tto track death saves for enemy creatures, which is a minor annoyance that most\n\tDMs are happy to avoid, so discuss how your DM would prefer to handle that\n\taspect of the subclass.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Players may also find that Wails from the Grave is underwhelming at low levels compared to other subclass features at the same level. Phantom definitely builds slowly, and may not feel really satisfying until level 9. As a quick fix, give players the Soul Trinket mechanic at level 3 instead of level 9, but don’t grant them Advantage on Constitution saves or the ability to spend Soul Trinkets to ask dead creatures questions until they reach level 9. Essentially, you’re splitting the Tokens of the Departed feature into two. At low levels, the player’s low Proficiency Bonus will keep Wails from the Grave from getting out of hand, and even if they’re spamming it constantly the damage is minor so it’s not going to result in a massive boost in damage output. This change will make the subclass more satisfying to play and will give access to the subclass’s signature features earlier without significantly changing the balance of the subclass.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                1. Whispers of the Dead<\/span>: The ability to pick\n\t\tup a proficiency on short notice is incredibly useful.                   Most of the time you can leave this on a low-importance skill\n\t\t\tproficiency, but most tools won’t come up unless you’ve got time for a\n\t\t\tshort rest. No one is going to ambush you and ask you to break out the\n\t\t\tBrewer’s Tools and make them some beer (I’ve made beer. It takes a few\n\t\t\thours, and spending one hour to take a nap first isn’t a big ask. I’ve\n\t\t\tdone that, too.).<\/p>\n\t<\/li>\n\t

                2. Wails from the Grave<\/span>: Starts very slow\n\t\t(1d6 twice a day), but ramps up exponentially as your Sneak Attack damage\n\t\tand your Proficiency Bonus increase.
