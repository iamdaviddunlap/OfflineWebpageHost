Status Conditions Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

We’ve all been there as a player: the DM informs you that your character has been stunned. \u201cThat’s like paralyzed\u201d the cleric says. The DM gives an informative \u201cWell, yes. But no.\u201d The cleric should know better. The reality of status conditions (conditions, statuses, status effects, whatever) in 5e is that most don’t affect play often enough to memorize. It’s no easy feat to build a character around supporting the party with status conditions. As a DM, certain monsters use status conditions heavily. Lucky for the DM, the standard 5e DM screen has a handy list of status conditions to remind themselves what \u201cstunned\u201d means.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                                The Incapacitated Family<\/h2>\n\n\n

                                The first grouping of status conditions we should discuss is the\n\t\u201cIncapacitated Family.\u201d Most sources in 5e don’t directly cause Incapacitated.\n\tInstead, we have a list of four effects that each cause the incapacitated\n\teffect AND something else.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

                                <\/div>\n\n\n\n

                                Incapacitated<\/h3>\n\n

                                What can’t we do?<\/h4>\n\n\n

                                An incapacitated creature can’t take actions, bonus actions, or reactions. If\n\tthat creature was maintaining concentration, they lose it.<\/p>\n\n\n

                                What can we do?<\/h4>\n\n\n

                                We can move, but not dash (because Dash is an Action). We can speak.<\/p>\n\n\n

                                Why is this first?<\/h4>\n\n\n

                                Because everything causes Incapacitation.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n
