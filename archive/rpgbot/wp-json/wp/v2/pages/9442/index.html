Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n

MCDM Productions released Beastheart and Monstrous Companions<\/a> in late 2021, a PDF supplement which introduces The Beastheart and a system for bringing Monstrous Companions into your game so that your party can have monsters like gelatinous cubes and hellhounds as pets or sidekicks.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

At its simplest, the Beastheart is a “pet class”. Their primary function is\n\tto serve as the Caretaker for a Monstrous Companion, and their class features\n\trevolve around that central capability. Dexterity\/Wisdom builds will be\n\tcommon, and like the Ranger, Strength\/Wisdom builds are possible but difficult\n\twith little or no payoff for the additional difficulty.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Comparisons to the Beast Master Ranger and the Drakewarden Ranger are\n\tnatural. But where the Ranger is a ranger with a pet, the Beastheart is built\n\taround their Monstrous Companion, and without a companion they largely cease\n\tto function until they can find a minute to meditate and return their\n\tcompanion to life. You’re still capable of making an attack which does\n\tpassable damage, but your companion provides the Ferocity needed to fuel\n\teverything interesting that the Beastheart does in combat.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

All that said, when your companion works, it can really<\/em> work. Between Primal Exploits and the class’s other features, you and your companion can be very effective both offensively and defensively, allowing you to serve as both a Defender and a Striker.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Outside of combat you’re reliant exclusively on skills, but your effective rolls extend little beyond Scout. You can supplement these capabilities with your companion’s skill proficiencies, but most companions won’t have useful skills until your get Beyond Instinct at level 5, and even then their effectiveness won’t match a player. Altogether the Beastheart fills a role in the party similar to a fighter or a ranger.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Because the Beastheart is built around Monstrous Companions, I strongly\n\trecommend reading my\n\tPractical Guide to Monstrous Companions<\/a>.<\/p>\n\n\n

              Beastheart Class Features<\/h2>\n\n\n

              Optional Class Features are detailed below under Optional Class Features<\/a>.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

              <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

              Hit Points<\/span>: 8+ hit points is standard for\n\tStriker classes and some spellcasters. You won’t be durable enough to serve as\n\ta Defender, but between you and your Monstrous Companion you have more total\n\thit points than anyone else in the party.<\/p>\n

              Saves<\/span>: Strength and Wisdom.<\/p>\n

              Proficiencies<\/span>: Medium armor, shields, and\n\tenough martial weapon options that you don’t need to look for more. You also\n\tget three skills from a skill list which strongly resembles the Ranger’s.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

              <\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

              Companion<\/span>: The Beastheart’s signature\n\tfeature. I strongly recommend also reading my\n\tPractical Guide to Monstrous Companions<\/a>.<\/p>\n

              Natural Language<\/span>: Tgis isn’t quite Speak\n\tWith Animals. In some ways it’s worse, but in some ways it’s better. Speak\n\tWith Animals allows you to communicate more complex ideas, which is helpful if\n\tyou need more specific information. But Natural Language also works on\n\tMonstrosities, and doesn’t require your 8-Charisma druid or ranger to make\n\tCharisma checks, instead allowing you to use Wisdom (Animal Handling) which\n\tthe Beastheart should already be good at.<\/p>\n

              Primal Exploits<\/strong>: You start with three and get two more at\n\teach of levels 10 and 17 for a total of 7. See Primal Exploits, below, for\n\thelp picking.<\/p>\n

              Superior Ferocity<\/span>: Monstrous Companions use\n\ta DC of 10+PB for their features. You DC is 10+Wis+PB, so for this to have any\n\timpact your Wisdom needs to be 16 or better.<\/p>\n

              Companion Bond<\/strong>: Companion Bond is the Beastheart’s subclass.\n\tFor help, see Subclasses – Companion Bond, below.<\/p>\n

              Master Caregiver<\/span>: Excellent. This saves us\n\tthe trouble of taking Skill Expert or a rogue dip. Animal Handling becomes\n\tcrucial as you get better at generating more Ferocity and want to avoid\n\trampages, and Master Caregiver arrives early in your career before you have\n\ttime to wish that you got it sooner.<\/p>\n

              Beyond Instinct<\/span>: The additional Ferocity\n\tgeneration is minor, but still very helpful to power both your companion’s\n\tfeatures and your Primal Exploits. An additional saving throw is a massive\n\tdefensive benefit. I recommend Constitutution, Wisdom, Dexterity, Charisma,\n\tStrength, and Intelligence in that order of priority. They’ll get more at\n\thigher levels. They also gain a skill from a short list. I recommend\n\tPerception or Stealth in most cases, but Investigation can also be useful,\n\tespecially since Beyond Instinct allows them to use Wisdom in place of your\n\tcompanion’s 5-ish Intelligence.<\/p>\n

              Improved Signature Attack (1 die)<\/span>:\n\tMonstrous Companions’ attacks don’t improve by any other method, so this is a\n\tcrucial damage increase as you gain levels. This level also makes your\n\tcompanion’s attacks magical, which is important for overcoming increasingly\n\tcommon resistance to non-magic weapon damage types.<\/p>\n

              Faithful Companion<\/span>: Rampages, while\n\tannoying, should be a rarity thanks to your presumably high Animal Handling\n\tbonus. At higher levels they may become a problem once Beyond Instinct causes\n\tFerocity to accumulate faster, and you’ll eventually want to build up Ferocity\n\tfor high-level Primal Exploits. The removal of the Bonus Action cost to\n\tcommand your companion isn’t especially helpful right away (though see\n\tRejuvenating Ferocity, below), but in a few levels Mystic Connection gives you\n\ta powerful Bonus Action option, so you will have a few occasional uses without\n\tneed to stress over what’s going to eat your Bonus Action on any given\n\tturn.<\/p>\n

              Rejuvenating Ferocity<\/span>: The amount healed\n\talmost certainly isn’t large, and since you can only use this a few times per\n\tlong rest, you’re likely only going to use this when your companion is at 0\n\thit points. As long as they dropped to 0 with at least 1 point of Ferocity,\n\tyou can pop them back up and get right back to fighting, just like you would\n\theal a party member with Healing Word. 5e is weird that way. You’re not going\n\tto use this to heal your companion back to full hp, and you’re not intended to\n\tdo so.<\/p>\n

              Primal Strike (1d8)<\/span>: Pick Thunder damage.\n\tThis gives you a reason to attack beyond triggering your Primal Exploits. It\n\tfollows the same damage scale as the Cleric’s Divine Strike subclass feature,\n\tbut since the Beastheart needs to take the Attack action to trigger some\n\tPrimal Exploits, you may not be able to complement this damage with Booming\n\tBlade.\n<\/p>\n

              Mystic Connection<\/strong>: Mystic Connection offers an additional\n\tbenefit tied to your type of companion. These are typically either a passive\n\tability like as a move speed or an ability activated as a Bonus Action.\n\tIndividual types of Mystic Connection are assessed in my\n\tPractical Guide to Monstrous Companions<\/a>.<\/p>\n

              Beyond Instinct Improvement<\/span>: 2 more Ferocity\n\tgeneration for a total of 1d4+3+X, and another saving throw proficiency. At\n\tthis level you have a total of 5 exploits and your companion has had all of\n\ttheir feature for a long time, so there’s a lot that you can spend Ferocity\n\ton.<\/p>\n

              Improved Signature Attack (2 dice)<\/span>: More\n\tdamage. You should be at 3d6 at this level.<\/p>\n

              Loyal to the End<\/span>: Charm and fear effects\n\tare common, so immunity to both provides broad protection against problematic\n\tstatus conditions imposed by many enemies like dragons and vampires.<\/p>\n

              Keen Senses<\/span>: Perception is the most-rolled\n\tskill in the game, and this is essentially permanent Advantage on every\n\tPerception check that you care about (though it doesn’t apply to taste or\n\ttouch). This also raises your Passive Perception by 5 (as normal for\n\tAdvantage), making it very hard for other creatures to evade your notice. The\n\tSearch<\/a>\n\taction is frequently overlooked by players, but it’s essential for finding\n\tenemies who are hiding, invisible, or otherwise undetected. It can often feel\n\tlike a problematically high cost to spend your Action to Search, so doing it\n\tas a Bonus Action with your absurdly effective Perception means that hidden\n\tenemies are basically no longer a problem. Of course, you need to roll a 16 or\n\tbetter to beat your Passive Perception, so if you have a better use for your\n\tBonus Action, do that instead.<\/p>\n

              Primal Strike (2d8)<\/span>: Your attacks still\n\taren’t amazing, but they’re enough to make a difference even though you\n\tprobably only make one attack each turn.<\/p>\n

              Beyond Instinct Improvement<\/span>: 1d4+5+X\n\tFerocity per turn and another save. That’s enough Ferocity that on average\n\twhen facing one enemy, your companion generates 8.5 Ferocity, enough to use\n\ttheir most powerful feature every turn. That’s not always the right option,\n\tbut the fact that it is an option is exciting.<\/p>\n

              Improved Signature Attack (3 dice)<\/span>: 4d6\n\tdamage is respectable. It won’t match a fighter or another player character,\n\tbut you’re also not relying exclusively on your companion’s Signature\n\tAttack.<\/p>\n

              Summon the Wilds<\/span>: A big area of effect, a\n\tgreat duration, the effects are devastating, and you can move it 30 feet to\n\tkeep pace with most creatures’ move speeds. You can even move it upward to hit\n\tflying creatures. If you have a save-or-suck caster in the party if your\n\tcompanion has a save-or-suck effect like the Basilisk companion’s Lesser\n\tPetrifying Gaze, this makes those options horrifyingly effective, not to\n\tmention making enemies dramatically less threatening. This could easily be the\n\twin condition in every encounter in which you use it. If this was once per\n\tlong rest I would still rate it blue<\/span>.<\/p>\n

              Unbreakable Friendship<\/span>: No more rampages.\n\tYour companion’s hit points essentially stop mattering unless something hits\n\tit for half of its hit point maximum in one hit (at this level that should be\n\tsomewhere around 65 damage at minimum), so it functionally has unlimited hit\n\tpoints but could go down to a big critical hit or a spell. You also finally\n\tstart combat with enough Ferocity to do something exciting on turn 1.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n

              Beastheart Subclasses – Companion Bond<\/h3>\n\n\n\n

              Ferocious Bond<\/h4>\n\n\n

              Have you considered just letting your companion Rampage all the time instead\n\tof actually commanding them? Because that’s what Ferocious Bond is all about.\n\tLet your companion rampage and chase them around hitting stuff. Unfortunately,\n\trampage eats most or all of your companion’s Ferocity, which largely prevents\n\tusing Ferocity Actions or Primal Exploits, thereby negating many of your build\n\tchoices. The subclass expects that you’re going to lean into rampage on most\n\tor all of your turns and encourages you to do so, but the tactical cost is\n\thigh.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              While Ferocious Bond is fine at throwing yourself your companion into melee\n\tto go poke things with sticks, the fact that the subclass struggles to work\n\talongside Primal Exploits makes it extremely difficult to play to any great\n\teffectiveness if you want to do anything except poke things with sticks. It is\n\tabsolutely simplest beastheart subclass, but I don’t think it’s especially\n\teffective due to the fundamental disconnect between how the subclass works and\n\thow you use Ferocity Actions and Primal Exploits.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              This is the one character on which I would consider Armor of the Caregiver a\n\tgood choice. Occasionally your positioning won’t work out how you’d like and\n\tyour rampaging companion will take a swing at you.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Frenzied Charge<\/span>: This is dangerous, but\n\tit’s the central idea around which the subclass is build. You need to be\n\tcertain to keep your companion far away from yourself and your allies in\n\tcombat for risk of your companion attacking you. The extra attack is nice,\n\teffectively doubling your damage output at low levels. The added damage output\n\tis minor as you gain levels since Primal Strikes only works once on each of\n\tyour turns, but being able to attack as a Reaction (especially once you get\n\tAdvantage from Furious Rampage) leaves your Action for other things like\n\tactivating exploits, using items, or potentially casting spells if you\n\tmulticlass.<\/p>\n

              Fury of the Wise<\/span>: This makes Intimidation a\n\tworthwhile Face skill for you.<\/p>\n

              Energizing Rampage<\/span>: If you’re going to let\n\tyour companion Rampage, this is nice because they’ll still have some Ferocity\n\tleft for Primal Exploits. Or if you’re just letting Rampage do the work,\n\tthey’ll get back to their next Rampage much sooner. If you do plan to use\n\texploits, try to keep the cost down to 4 Ferocity, as you’ll find it nearly\n\timpossible to use more expensive exploits without turning off your entire\n\tsubclass for a round.<\/p>\n

              Furious Rampage<\/span>: By this level, Beyond\n\tInstinct adds 3 Ferocity when your companion gains Ferocity at the beginning\n\tof their turn. Since you start from a floor of 4 whenever your companion\n\trampages, you have a 50% chance of hitting the 10 Ferocity needed to trigger a\n\trampage before you consider any creatures which might be adjavent to your\n\tcompanion. This means that your companion can rampage frequently (or\n\tconstantly once Beyond Instinct improves again at level 15), triggering\n\tFrenzied Charge often. Be sure to stay close to your companion (ideally with\n\tan enemy between the two of you) so that you both get easy Advantage on your\n\tattacks.<\/p>\n

              Invigorated Rampage<\/span>: Make them blinded first\n\tor frightened if they’re already blinded. By now you should have consistent\n\tAdvantage on your companion’s rampage attack and your Frenzied Charge attack,\n\tso you and your companion should be able to hit reliably.<\/p>\n\n\n

              Hunter Bond<\/h4>\n\n\n

              A natural Scout and Striker, the Hunter Bond feels conceptually similar to the Ranger. It’s good at stealth and damage output, but doesn’t add a lot of complexity beyond the base class.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Chosen Quarry<\/span>: Similar in concept to the\n\tdamage boosts from Hex or Hunter’s Mark. Because this is used without an\n\taction when your companion gains Ferocity, it’s entirely possible to use this\n\tat the beginning of your first turn in combat, especially once Beyond Instinct\n\tadds additional Ferocity each turn.<\/p>\n

              The damage applies when you or your\n\tcompanion hit the target, which encourages you and your companion to focus\n\tyour attacks and make numerous attacks just as you would with Hex or Hunter’s\n\tMark. You can’t use this with Eldritch Blast since it requires weapon attacks\n\tand your Bonus Action is monopolized by commanding your companion for a while,\n\tso you likely won’t get more than 2d6 damage out of this per turn. Still,\n\tthat’s a decent damage boost.<\/p>\n

              Hunter’s Instincts<\/span>: Survival is only\n\tsituationally useful, but making it work in place of Insight and giving you\n\tExpertise in it certainly makes it more effective.<\/p>\n

              Primal Warding<\/span>: The effect is good, but\n\tthis is difficult to use in combat, and outside of combat it’s often easy to\n\tcircumvent. The trap explicitly requires that creatures “walk” into it, but I\n\tassume that’s to exclude other movement types, not to allow slithering,\n\tcrawling, dancing, etc.<\/p>\n

              Synchronized Stealth<\/span>: If you take the Quick\n\tHide Primal Exploit, you can take the Attack action, allow your companion to\n\tHide as a Reaction, triggering Synchronized Stealth to allow you to then Hide\n\tas a Reaction. Hiding in combat is a good way to get Advantage on your attack\n\tand to avoid drawing fire, but it typically requires hit-and-run tactics or\n\tranged combat, both of which are difficult for most companions.<\/p>\n

              Unseen Hunters<\/span>: Note that this is not\n\tinvisibility as the spell. It’s just the inivisibility condition, so you have\n\tnone of the restrictions of the spell Invisibility. You’re free to attack or\n\twhatever else for the effect’s duration.<\/p>\n\n\n

              Infernal Bond<\/h4>\n\n\n

              Similar in theme to the Warlock, Infernal Bond uses the power of devils to empower your and your companion. Infernal Bond offers more decision points than any other variety of beastheart, allowing you more options to customize and optimize your build. Among its options are the ability to give any companion flight and the ability to teleport, offering powerful mobility options for a class that’s often limited to walking.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Devil’s Understanding<\/span>: These aren’t great\n\tskills for the Beastheart, but they fit the theme of the subclass nicely.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Infernal Exploits<\/strong>: A surprisingly large list of additional\n\texploit options. Note that you can only take one<\/em> Infernal Exploit at\n\t3rd level and one more at level 11. These exploits aren’t available as an\n\toptions when the core class features grant you Primal Exploits. See below for\n\tmore.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Hell’s Charmer<\/span>: <\/p>\n\n\n\n

              Fiendish Traits<\/strong>: A great way to customize your companion.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                \n\t
              • Barbed Hide<\/span>: Nice, but the damage isn’t\n\t\tsignificant enough to deter most CR-appropriate enemies. This will keep mobs\n\t\tof low-CR enemies from swarming your companion, but that’s not a frequent\n\t\toccurance, and you can use those situations to accumulate Ferocity quickly\n\t\tand retaliate with expensive Ferocity Actions or Primal Exploirts.<\/li>\n\t
              • Fiendish Immunities<\/span>: Immunity to two of\n\t\tthe most common damage types in the game.<\/li>\n\t
              • Fiery Weapons<\/span>: 1d6 damage isn’t a huge\n\t\tdamage boost since your companion typically makes just one attack per round,\n\t\tand fire resistance\/immunity is common.<\/li>\n\t
              • Wings<\/span>: Flight is a crucial tactical option\n\t\tthat most compenions simply can’t produce, and without the ability to fight\n\t\tat range, most companions need this to be functional against flying\n\t\tenemies.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

                Infernal Exploits improvement<\/strong>: One more exploit from either\n\tthe 3rd or 11th-level list.<\/p>\n

                Fiendish Form<\/span>: This should be your Bonus\n\tAction on your first turn of nearly every encounter. The benefits are\n\texcellent, and by this level Beyond Instinct allows you to generate 1d4+5+X\n\tFerocity, so even if you roll a 1 you have enough.<\/p>\n\n\n

                Infernal Exploits<\/h5>\n\n
                3rd Level<\/h6>\n\n\n

                Drain Them<\/span>: Expensive, and you may\n\tfrequently find that your companion loses roughly the same amount of hit\n\tpoints over the course of the next round. If you just need to keep your\n\tcompanion going for another round or two, this can buy you time for the rest\n\tof your party to come to the rescue, but using the same Ferocity to\n\tincapacitate your target in some way (such as by using your companion’s\n\t5th-level Ferocity Action) will often be a better option because it will both\n\tprevent damage to your companion and harm your opponent. That said, this works\n\ton critical hits, and healing a bunch of damage on a crit feels nice.\n\tUnfortunately, with only one attack per turn, crits will be rare.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Hellish Wound<\/span>: Ongoing damage is great for\n\tenemies with a big pile of hit points, such as legendary creatures, but the\n\tdamage doesn’t scale in any way and 1d10 damage per round will quickly stop\n\tbeing impactful. Most combats last 3-5 rounds, so you really don’t have time\n\tto stack a bunch of these onto one target.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Infernal Teleport<\/span>: Teleportation breaks\n\tgrapples, removes you from dangerous locations, and potentially gets you into\n\tmelee with frail enemies who are hiding behind more sturdy creatures. The\n\trange is decent, and weirdly this doesn’t require line of sight. Unfortunately\n\tyou can’t use it outside of combat since Ferocity only exists in combat.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Wicked Deception<\/span>: Only situationally\n\tuseful, and immunity to the Charmed condition is common.<\/p>\n\n\n

                11th Level<\/h6>\n\n\n

                Brimstone Teleport<\/span>: If you can hit two or\n\tmore enemies with this, you will consistently do more damage than you could\n\twith weapon attacks. Keep in mind that the range is considerably smaller than\n\tInfernal Teleport and doesn’t allow your companion to teleport. I wouldn’t\n\ttake both teleportation options.<\/p>\n

                Chains from Hell<\/span>: Restrained is a great\n\tstatus condition. Even without the damage, this would be very good. If you can\n\tgenerate enough Ferocity, you can repeatedly restrain targets, severely\n\tlimiting their ability to fight back.<\/p>\n

                Dark of Hell<\/span>: Most creatures can’t see in\n\tmagical darkness, so this puts you and your companion at an advantage both\n\toffensively and defensively.<\/p>\n

                Infernal Flames<\/span>: Decent damage in a\n\treasonably large AOE. If you can hit two or three enemies with this, it will\n\teasily do more damage than a Signature Attack. Of course, the cost is high, so\n\tyou really want to hit 3 or more enemies to justify the cost.<\/p>\n

                Poison Rain<\/span>: Poisoned is a great status\n\tcondition, but Constitution saves tend to be high, and resistance\/immunity to\n\tpoison are common, so this will be unreliable<\/p>\n\n\n

                Primordial Bond<\/h4>\n\n\n

                Similar in them to the druid, Primordial Bond gives you exploits which feel very similar to druid spells, plus some nature-themed options which make your companion more dangerous. The addition of blasting options can help handle crowds, which is normally a challenge for the Beastheart, but few of the Nature Exploit options are truly worthwhile.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Nature Exploits<\/strong>: You get one at 3rd level and another at\n\t11th level. You can retrain a Nature Exploit every level (Fiendish Bond can’t\n\tdo this), allowing you to retrain your 3rd-level exploit for a new 11th-level\n\texploit when you reach 11th level if you’d like.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Primal Understanding<\/span>: Nature isn’t a great\n\tskill for the Beastheart since it’s Intelligence-based, but it fits the\n\ttheme.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Allied Earth<\/span>: This is a great way to make\n\tyour companion “sticky” so that it’s difficult for enemies to get away from\n\tthem. Any move speed except land speed doesn’t care about this, unfortunately,\n\tbut most fights still take place on the ground.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Nature Exploits improvement<\/strong>: A second exploit. Consider\n\tretraining your first Nature Exploit if you want another 11th-level\n\texploit.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Spirit Stampede<\/span>: Potentially very damaging\n\tif there are numerous enemies in the are and you can accumulate a bunch of\n\tFerocity to raise the damage dealt.                 Protector Bond attempts to turn the Beastheart and their companion into a bodyguard build.                 Thickened Hide<\/span>: Monstrous Companions don’t\n\tget fantastic AC until your Proficiency Bonus scales a bit. It’s not clear if\n\tthis would apply when your companion is wearing armor, but I assume that it\n\tdoes not.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Beastheart Ability Scores<\/h2>\n\n\n

                The Beastheart’s ability score needs closely resemble the Druid’s. The Quick Build entry for the class recommends making either Strength or Dexterity your primary ability score, but that seems misguided. Your Wisdom score determines your most important skills (Animal Handling and Perception), your Primal Exploit DC, and your Monstrous Companion’s DC’s for their abilities.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                Dexterity only matters for your AC and your one attack per turn, and while that single attack will be your Action on most turns, Wisdom will have better tactical benefits then just a little extra damage from higher Strength or Dexterity.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

                If you can find a way to get Shillelagh (Magic Initiate, Druid, Ranger), you\n\tcan build around Constitution and Wisdom exclusively, simplifying your ability\n\tscore needs considerably.<\/p>\n\n\n\n\n
